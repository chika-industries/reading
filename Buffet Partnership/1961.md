# Summary of Buffett's Fifth Year (1961)

## The Performance: A Blowout Year

1961 was a strongly rising market—the kind where Buffett had repeatedly warned he might struggle to keep pace. The Dow gained 22.2% including dividends. But Buffett's partnerships crushed it, averaging **45.9%** for the full year.

The cumulative results since 1957 were staggering:

| Period | Partnership | Dow |
|--------|-------------|-----|
| 1957-61 | +251.0% | +74.3% |

He was quick to add perspective: a strongly rising market was *not* where he expected to shine. That he did so well in 1961 was, in part, luck.

## A Word About Par (The Yardstick)

Buffett insisted on using the same yardstick for everyone—the Dow.

> "If my performance is poor, I expect partners to withdraw, and indeed, I should look for a new source of investment for my own funds. If performance is good, I am assured of doing splendidly, a state of affairs to which I am sure I can adjust."

He then presented data showing that most mutual funds *failed* to beat the Dow. Of 38 funds he considered comparable, only 6 did better—and none by more than a few percentage points annually.

His point: the Dow is "no pushover." Beating it consistently is hard. Most professionals don't.

## The Three Categories of Investment (Now Fully Articulated)

This is where Buffett laid out the framework he'd use for years.

**1. Generals (Undervalued Securities)**
Stocks where he had nothing to say about corporate policy and no timetable. Usually 5-10% positions in five or six issues, with smaller positions in another ten to fifteen.

> "Sometimes these work out very fast; many times they take years. It is difficult at the time of purchase to know any specific reason why they should appreciate in price."

The key: substantial excess of value over price creates a "comfortable margin of safety." Generals tend to move with the Dow—they'll go down in a bear market and lead in a bull market.

**2. Work-outs**
Securities with a timetable—mergers, liquidations, reorganizations, spin-offs. He waits until he can "read it in the paper."

> "The risk is that something will upset the applecart, not that the economic picture will deteriorate and stocks decline generally."

Work-outs produce reasonably stable earnings year to year, largely independent of the Dow. He uses borrowed money against them (his self-imposed limit: 25% of partnership net worth). Expected returns: 10-20%.

**3. Controls**
Rare, but when they occur, they're large. Either he controls the company or takes a very large position to influence policy.

> "Such operations should definitely be measured on the basis of several years. In a given year, they may produce nothing as it is usually to our advantage to have the stock be stagnant market-wise for a long period while we are acquiring it."

The Dempster Mill Manufacturing Company was his first major control situation (more on that below).

## Dempster Mill: The First Control Situation

Buffett had first bought Dempster stock five years earlier as a general. A block became available, he went on the board four years prior, and in August 1961 he obtained majority control (70% owned, with another 10% held by associates).

Dempster made farm implements and water systems. Sales ~$9 million, but only nominal profits relative to invested capital. Book value: $75 per share. Working capital: $50 per share. Buffett valued his controlling interest at **$35 per share**—a discount to book, reflecting the poor earning power.

He'd acquired control at an average price of about $28 per share. This holding represented 21% of partnership net assets.

> "Of course, this section of our portfolio is not going to be worth more money merely because General Motors, U.S. Steel, etc., sell higher."

Controls provide insulation from the market's whims.

## The Question of Conservatism (A Classic Buffett Rant)

Buffett dismantled conventional notions of "conservative investing."

> "Many people some years back thought they were behaving in the most conservative manner by purchasing medium or long-term municipal or government bonds. This policy has produced substantial market depreciation in many cases, and most certainly has failed to maintain or increase real buying power."

Then he turned to the inflation-phobic crowd:

> "Conscious, perhaps overly conscious, of inflation, many people now feel that they are behaving in a conservative manner by buying blue chip securities almost regardless of price-earnings ratios, dividend yields, etc. Without the benefit of hindsight as in the bond example, I feel this course of action is fraught with danger."

His definition of true conservatism:

> "You will not be right simply because a large number of people momentarily agree with you. You will not be right simply because important people agree with you. ... You will be right, over the course of many transactions, if your hypotheses are correct, your facts are correct, and your reasoning is correct. True conservatism is only possible through knowledge and reason."

He offered an objective test: performance in down markets. In the mild declines of 1957 and 1960, his partnerships held up far better than the Dow.

> "We have never suffered a realized loss of more than 0.5% of 1% of total net assets, and our ratio of total dollars of realized gains to total realized losses is something like 100 to 1."

## The Question of Size

Larger funds tug in two directions:
- **Passive investments** (generals, workouts): larger sums hurt results (harder to buy without moving price)
- **Control situations**: larger sums help (can't do a Sanborn Map without firepower)

His verdict: "There is no reason to think these should not be offsetting factors."

## The Prediction (Famous Passage)

> "I think you can be quite sure that over the next ten years there are going to be a few years when the general market is plus 20% or 25%, a few when it is minus on the same order, and a majority when it is in between."

He expected the Dow to produce 5-7% compounded annually over long periods. His job: pile up yearly advantages.

> "I would consider a year in which we were down 15% and the Dow declined 25% to be much superior to a year when both the partnership and the Dow advanced 20%."

His specific scenario:

> "If the market should be down 35% or 40% in a year (and I feel this has a high probability of occurring one year in the next ten—no one knows which one), we should be down only 15% or 20%. If it is more or less unchanged during the year, we would hope to be up about ten percentage points. If it is up 20% or more, we would struggle to be up as much."

The consequence: if the Dow does 5-7%, he hopes for 15-17%.

> "The above expectations may sound somewhat rash, and there is no question but that they may appear very much so when viewed from the vantage point of 1965 or 1970."

## How His Mind Works (1961 Edition)

**He measures everything:**
The entire letter is built around yardsticks. He doesn't just say "we did well." He shows the Dow, the mutual funds, the cumulative compounding, the down-market performance. He wants to be judged—but judged fairly, on the right metrics.

**He thinks in scenarios, not predictions:**
He's not predicting a 35-40% market drop. He's saying it *could* happen, and if it does, here's how he'd perform. This is probabilistic thinking, not forecasting.

**He separates "cerebral agreement" from "visceral agreement":**
> "I have stressed this point in talking with partners and have watched them nod their heads with varying degrees of enthusiasm. It is most important to me that you fully understand my reasoning in this regard and agree with me not only in your cerebral regions, but also down in the pit of your stomach."

He knows that intellectually agreeing with a strategy is different from emotionally surviving it. He wants partners who can handle the down years without panic.

**He's transparent about his own money:**
> "Susie and I have an interest in the partnership amounting to $1,025,000.00 and other relatives of mine have a combined interest totaling $782,600.00."

He eats his own cooking.

## The Jokes

The "vicuna coat" line: His father shared office space and did brokerage, but "none of our brokerage is done through him so we have no 'vicuna coat' situation"—a reference to the 1950s scandal where a fund manager directed brokerage to firms that gave him vicuna coats.

The aside about Noah's diversification: "I've never gone overboard for Noah's ideas on diversification either." (Referring to two of every animal.)

And the self-deprecating prediction: "The above expectations may sound somewhat rash" — he knew he was setting himself up to be quoted later.

## The Key Quote

> "You will not be right simply because a large number of people momentarily agree with you."

This is Buffett's intellectual independence in a single sentence. He doesn't need consensus. He needs facts, hypotheses, and reasoning. Everything else is noise.
