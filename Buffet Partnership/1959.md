# Summary of Buffett's Third Year (1959)

## The Market Picture: Faulty Indicators

Buffett opened by pointing out that the Dow-Jones Industrial Average—up 16.4% for the year (19.9% with dividends)—presented a "somewhat faulty picture." Why? Because despite that robust headline, **more stocks declined than advanced** on the NYSE: 710 to 628. The Railroad and Utility Averages both fell.

Most investment trusts struggled relative to the Dow. Tri-Continental Corp., the nation's largest closed-end fund (assets $400 million), had an overall gain of only about 5.7%. Its president, Fred Brown, gave Buffett a perfect quote to capture the mood:

> "Perhaps we haven't had our space boots adjusted properly."

Brown admitted his value-oriented team was ill-suited to a market driven by "investor sentiment and enthusiasm."

## Buffett's Apprehension (Unpopular, but Unchanged)

> "Most of you know I have been very apprehensive about general stock market levels for several years. To date, this caution has been unnecessary."

He acknowledged being wrong so far—but refused to abandon his framework. He saw "a substantial speculative component" in blue chip prices, with corresponding risk of loss. Maybe new valuation standards were evolving. Maybe trees really could grow to the sky.

> "I don't think so. I may very well be wrong; however, I would rather sustain the penalties resulting from over-conservatism than face the consequences of error, perhaps with permanent capital loss, resulting from the adoption of a 'New Era' philosophy where trees really do grow to the sky."

That's Buffett in a nutshell: **he'd rather be wrong and cautious than wrong and permanently broke.**

## The Results

Six partnerships operated the full year. Net gains ranged from 22.3% to 30.0%, averaging about 25.9%. The Dow (with dividends) gained 19.9%. So a solid relative performance—but again, this was not the kind of market where he expected to shine.

He also noted a new expense: the Nebraska Intangibles Tax, which took 0.4% of market value on practically all securities. "Last year represented the first time that this tax had been effectively enforced."

## The New Large Commitment (35% of Assets)

Buffett mentioned that a commitment he'd referred to the previous year (about 25% of assets) had grown to about 35% of assets. This was an unusually large concentration, but he had "strong reasons."

The company was *partially an investment trust*—it owned thirty or forty other high-quality securities. Buffett bought at a substantial discount to the asset value (based on market prices of those securities plus a conservative appraisal of the operating business).

He was already the largest stockholder by a considerable margin, and the two other large holders agreed with his ideas.

> "The probability is extremely high that the performance of this investment will be superior to that of the general market until its disposition, and I am hopeful that this will take place this year."

## How His Mind Works (1959 Edition)

**He doesn't need the crowd to agree with him:**
His apprehension about the market had been "unnecessary" for several years running. Most people would have dropped the caution. He didn't. He was willing to look foolish for years if it meant avoiding a catastrophic mistake.

**He thinks in terms of "permanent capital loss"—not volatility:**
Notice his phrase: "permanent capital loss." He distinguishes between temporary price drops (which he can tolerate) and permanent destruction of capital (which he will do almost anything to avoid). The "New Era" philosophy risked the latter.

**He uses unusual metrics for value:**
The 35% commitment wasn't a typical stock. It was effectively a holding company trading at a discount to its own portfolio. He was buying a discount on top of discounts.

**He builds alliances, not just positions:**
"The two other large holders agree with our ideas." He doesn't need 51% control to influence outcomes. He just needs a few like-minded large holders.

## The Prediction (That He Hoped Would Be Tested)

> "This policy should lead to superior results in bear markets and average performance in bull markets. The first prediction may be subject to test this year since, at this writing, the Dow-Jones Industrials have retraced over half of their 1959 advance."

He was almost eager for a market decline—not because he wanted to lose money, but because he wanted the *relative* test. He knew his method worked best when others were hurting.

## The Joke

The "space boots" quote from Tri-Continental's president is the best of the year. Buffett didn't write it, but he chose to include it—and the gentle mockery is clear. These highly paid professionals, with their fancy metaphors, couldn't keep up with an unmanaged index.

The other joke is subtler: his own admission that his caution had been "unnecessary" for years. He's not defensive. He's almost amused by his own stubbornness. He'd rather be the guy who cried wolf and was wrong than the guy who got eaten.
