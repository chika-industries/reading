# Summary of Buffett's First Year (1957)

## The Big Picture

In his second annual letter (1957), Buffett laid out the core framework that would define his partnership for years to come. The market was not in a good place by his lights—he believed blue-chip securities were "priced above intrinsic value." He wasn't predicting a crash, but he thought it unlikely that current levels would look cheap five years out.

## The Two-Bucket Framework

Buffett introduced a distinction that became central to his method:

- **Undervalued securities** ("generals"): Stocks trading below their intrinsic value, dependent on general market appreciation to realize gains
- **Work-outs**: Investments where profit depends on a specific corporate action (merger, liquidation, tender, etc.) rather than market movement. The risk here isn't the economy—it's "that something will upset the applecart."

At the end of 1956, his ratio was about 70% generals / 30% work-outs. By the end of 1957, it was 85% generals / 15% work-outs—the market decline had created better opportunities in undervalued situations.

## Two Larger Positions

Buffett had taken positions in two companies where he expected to "take some part in corporate decisions"—one at 10-20% of partnership assets, another at about 5%. Both would likely take 3-5 years to play out, but offered "high average annual rate of return with a minimum of risk." These had "very little dependence on the general action of the stock market."

## The Results

The Dow fell from 499 to 435—a 64-point loss. With dividends, the overall loss was 8.47%. The three partnerships formed in 1956 all showed gains: about 6.2%, 7.8%, and 25% respectively.

That last figure got attention. Buffett's explanation? **Luck.** The third partnership started later in 1956, when the market was lower and several securities were particularly attractive. The earlier partnerships were already substantially invested and could only take small positions. His point: "Basically, all partnerships are invested in the same securities and in approximately the same percentages."

## A Key Insight on Performance Measurement

> "Over the years, I will be quite satisfied with a performance that is 10% per year better than the Averages."

This became his enduring benchmark. Not absolute returns. Relative advantage.

## How His Mind Works (First Glimpses)

**He thinks in probabilities, not predictions:**
> "I make no attempt to forecast either business or the stock market."

He explicitly separates market analysis from security analysis. Primary attention goes to "detection of substantially undervalued securities."

**He embraces counterintuitive patience:**
> "Obviously during any acquisition period, our primary interest is to have the stock do nothing or decline rather than advance."

When building a position, he wants prices low and stagnant—not rising. "Therefore, at any given time, a fair proportion of our portfolio may be in the sterile stage." He calls this "sterile"—no immediate gratification—but necessary for long-term profit.

**He understands his edge is contextual:**
> "Our performance, relatively, is likely to be better in a bear market than in a bull market."

This is brutally honest self-awareness. He's not claiming to outperform in every environment. He knows where his method works best and where it struggles.

**He values the portfolio's "value" over its market price:**
> "I can definitely say that our portfolio represents better value at the end of 1957 than it did at the end of 1956."

He judges himself by the underlying quality and price of what he owns, not by what the market says it's worth today.

**He's willing to look foolish in the short term:**
The "sterile stage" strategy means underperforming in a rising market. He's okay with that. He's playing a multi-year game.

## The Joke (Mild, But Present)

No belly laughs in 1957. The humor is dry and understated—his later letters get funnier. But there's a wry tone in how he handles the 25% vs. 6.2% disparity between partnerships: "Naturally a question is created as to the vastly superior performance of the last partnership, particularly in the mind of the partners of the first two." He then calmly explains it was mostly timing and luck. The joke is on human nature—everyone wants to know why *they* didn't get the better number.
