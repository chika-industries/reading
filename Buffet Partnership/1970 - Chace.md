# Analysis of the First Berkshire Hathaway Letter (April 3, 1970)

## Background

This letter is not written by Warren Buffett. It is signed by **Kenneth V. Chace**, President of Berkshire Hathaway. But Buffett was the controlling shareholder (70% owned through BPL at the time of the partnership's dissolution, with BPL partners receiving shares). The strategy, the tone, and the capital allocation decisions are all Buffett's. Chace was the operating man Buffett trusted to run the textile business while Buffett focused on redeploying capital.

This letter marks the beginning of Berkshire's transformation from a struggling textile company into the vehicle Buffett would use to build his empire.

## The Big Picture: A Four‑Year Strategy

Chace (Buffett) opens by summarizing the strategy adopted four years earlier:

> "Four years ago your management committed itself to the development of more substantial and more consistent earning power than appeared possible if capital continued to be invested exclusively in the textile industry."

The plan: use the textile business's cash flow to buy marketable securities temporarily, then use those funds to acquire operating businesses in more attractive industries.

> "This policy has proved reasonably successful – particularly when contrasted with results achieved by firms which have continued to commit large sums to textile expansion in the face of totally inadequate returns."

This is a direct jab at competitors who kept pouring money into a dying industry. Buffett's insight: don't throw good money after bad.

## The Results: 10% Return Overall, Despite Textile Drag

Berkshire achieved an "over‑all return of more than 10% on average stockholders' equity" in 1969. But the textile portion of the business returned "less than 5%." The difference came from the newly acquired insurance and banking operations.

**Key financial highlight:** They liquidated their entire marketable securities portfolio over the last two years at a profit of **more than $5 million after taxes**. This provided the funds to make a major acquisition in 1969 (the Illinois National Bank) at a time when borrowed money was "generally most difficult to obtain."

> "We anticipate no further purchases of marketable securities, but our search for desirable acquisitions continues."

This is a pivotal moment: Berkshire is moving from a holding company that parks cash in stocks to a holding company that *owns operating businesses*.

## Textile Operations (The Problem Child)

Sales were down 12% from 1968. Net earnings were slightly higher only because of cost‑cutting, including the shutdown of the Box Loom Division at a loss. Buffett (through Chace) is being ruthless with capital – closing unprofitable operations even when it hurts current earnings.

The industry was in a "textile recession of greater intensity than we have seen for some years." Demand was so weak that both the Menswear Lining and Home Fabrics divisions had to shut down for two weeks in the first quarter of 1970 to avoid inventory buildup.

> "Recovery from this cycle will probably be dependent upon Federal Government action on economic factors they can control."

This is interesting – Buffett rarely blames government or waits for government to save him. But here he's being realistic: the textile industry is so cyclical and commodity‑like that only macro factors can lift it. He's not betting on it.

He notes that improvements have been made that should reduce costs, but the outlook for 1970 is "lower profits."

## Insurance Operations (The Star)

Jack Ringwalt, running National Indemnity, turned in "new records in just about every department." In a year when the fire and casualty insurance industry experienced "substantial underwriting losses," Berkshire's insurance subsidiaries achieved "significant adjusted underwriting profits."

> "Since establishment of the business in 1941, Mr. Ringwalt has held to the principle of underwriting for a profit – a policy which is frequently talked about within the industry but much less frequently achieved."

This is pure Buffett: he loves managers who do what they say, who focus on underwriting profit rather than market share, who are disciplined when others are reckless.

**Expansion:** New surety department, entry into workmen's compensation in California (branch office in Los Angeles), a new reinsurance division (though it will take years to evaluate), and plans for a "home state" insurance operation.

Phil Liesche, "over 20 years a major contributor," was elected Executive Vice President.

## Banking Operations (The New Jewel)

The "most significant event of 1969" was the acquisition of 97.7% of The Illinois National Bank and Trust Co. of Rockford, Illinois.

> "This bank had been built by Eugene Abegg, without addition of outside capital, from $250,000 of net worth and $400,000 of deposits in 1931 to $17 million of net worth and $100 million of deposits in 1969."

Abegg continued as Chairman and produced record operating earnings of approximately $2 million in 1969. Earnings as a percentage of deposits or assets were "close to the top among larger commercial banks in the country."

Buffett (through Chace) is effusive:

> "After almost a year of ownership, we are delighted with our investment in Illinois National Bank, and our association with Mr. Abegg."

**The downside:** The unit banking law of Illinois makes deposit growth difficult for a downtown bank. And the bank is already highly efficient – there's not much fat to cut. So 1970 may not see earnings growth.

## How Buffett's Mind Works (Through Chace's Words)

**1. He redeploys capital from dying businesses to better ones.**

The textile business earns less than 5% on capital. Insurance and banking earn much more. So he takes cash from textiles (and from liquidated marketable securities) and buys insurance and banking. This is the essence of capital allocation.

**2. He is comfortable closing operations that don't work.**

The Box Loom Division was shut down at a loss. He doesn't throw good money after bad.

**3. He seeks managers with a long‑term, profit‑focused mindset.**

Ringwalt underwriting for profit, not market share. Abegg building a bank without outside capital over 38 years. These are Buffett's kind of people.

**4. He is patient with certain businesses (textiles) but ruthless with capital.**

He won't liquidate the textile business and throw 1,100 people out of work just to squeeze out a few more percentage points. But he also won't invest another dollar in expansion. He runs it for cash flow.

**5. He thinks in terms of return on equity, not growth for growth's sake.**

> "an over‑all return of more than 10% on average stockholders' equity" – that's the metric.

**6. He is honest about difficulties.**

The textile recession, the difficulty of growing the bank under Illinois law, the risk that reinsurance takes years to judge – no hype.

## The Jokes (None, Really – It's a Corporate Letter)

This is a formal shareholder letter, not a Buffett partnership letter. The humor is absent. But the dry understatement is there: "reasonably successful" (after a $5 million profit and two major acquisitions). And the quiet dig at competitors who "commit large sums to textile expansion" – that's Buffett's competitive edge showing.

## The Key Quote (From Buffett's Philosophy, Not Directly in This Letter)

Though not written by Buffett, the letter reflects his core belief:

> "Put capital to work where it earns the highest return, with managers you trust, and don't be sentimental about dying industries."

The textile business would continue to struggle for years. Buffett would eventually close most of it. But in 1970, he was already building the machine that would make him one of the richest people in history – one insurance company, one bank, and one disciplined capital allocator at a time.

## Transition Note

This is the first public communication from the new Berkshire Hathaway under Buffett's effective control. The partnership letters are over. The Berkshire letters begin. And the tone is different – more corporate, less personal, fewer jokes. But the strategic mind is the same: value, discipline, people, and patience.
