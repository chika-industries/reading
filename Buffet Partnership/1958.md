# Summary of Buffett's Second Year (1958)

## The Market Climate

1958 was a roaring year. The Dow-Jones Industrial Average advanced from 435 to 583—a gain of 38.5% including dividends. Buffett quoted a friend who ran an investment trust: "The mercurial temperament, characteristic of the American people, produced a major transformation in 1958 and 'exuberant' would be the proper word for the stock market, at least."

Buffett wasn't buying the euphoria. He noted that "almost any reason has been seized upon to justify 'Investing' in the market." He believed the widespread public belief in the inevitability of stock profits would lead to "eventual trouble." But he made no attempt to forecast when—his job was finding undervalued securities.

## The Results

The five partnerships that operated the full year averaged slightly better than the Dow's 38.5%, with individual results ranging from 36.7% to 46.2%. He was satisfied—but not thrilled. This was a bull market, and he'd already warned that keeping pace with a rising market would be "all we can do."

## The Commonwealth Trust Story (A Masterclass in His Thinking)

Buffett walked through a real investment to show how his mind worked. This is one of the most illuminating passages in the early letters.

**The setup:**
- Commonwealth Trust Co. of Union City, New Jersey
- Intrinsic value: ~$125 per share (conservative estimate)
- Earnings: ~$10 per share
- But it paid **no cash dividend at all**
- Result: Depressed price of ~$50 per share

**The qualitative factors:**
- Very well managed bank
- Management was friendly to new stockholders
- Risk of ultimate loss seemed "minimal"

**The catalyst (or potential catalyst):**
- 25.5% owned by a larger bank that had wanted a merger for many years
- Merger had been prevented "for personal reasons"—but that wouldn't last indefinitely

**The three-part thesis:**
1. Very strong defensive characteristics
2. Good solid value building up at a satisfactory pace
3. Evidence that eventually this value would be unlocked—though it might take one year or ten

> "If the latter were true, the value would presumably have been built up to a considerably larger figure, say, $250 per share."

**The acquisition:**
Over a year or so, Buffett bought about 12% of the bank at an average price of ~$51 per share. He wanted the stock to *stay dormant* while he bought—and it did.

**The exit:**
Late in the year, he found an even better opportunity. He sold Commonwealth at $80 per share, even though the quoted market was about 20% lower at the time.

**His reflection on timing:**
> "The year when a situation such as Commonwealth results in a realized profit is, to a great extent, fortuitous. Thus, our performance for any single year has serious limitations as a basis for estimating long term results."

He notes that if he hadn't found that replacement opportunity, he'd still be sitting with $50 stock patiently buying. "I would be quite happy with such a program although our performance relative to the market last year would have looked poor."

## How His Mind Works (1958 Edition)

**He buys for the long term, sells when something better appears—not when the market says so:**
He sold Commonwealth at $80 with intrinsic value of $135. The new situation (which he didn't name) was somewhat larger, represented about 25% of partnership assets, and he was the largest stockholder. That controlling position gave him "substantial advantages many times in determining the length of time required to correct the undervaluation."

> "In this particular holding we are virtually assured of a performance better than that of the Dow-Jones for the period we hold it."

**He thinks in terms of "value to a private owner," not market price:**
The whole Commonwealth analysis rested on what the business was worth to someone who could buy the whole thing—not what the stock market was currently bidding.

**He understands that his method scales strangely:**
> "Very small buying orders can create price changes of this magnitude in an inactive stock, which explains the importance of not having any 'Leakage' regarding our portfolio holdings."

Secrecy isn't paranoia—it's practical. If people knew what he was buying, the price would move against him.

**He's honest about the changing opportunity set:**
> "The higher the level of the market, the fewer the undervalued securities and I am finding some difficulty in securing an adequate number of attractive investments."

Rather than lower his standards, he shifted strategy: "I am attempting to create my own work-outs by acquiring large positions in several undervalued securities."

## The Joke (Still Dry, But There)

No laugh-out-loud moment in 1958, but his tone carries a wry awareness of how Wall Street works. When he quotes his friend calling the market "exuberant," and then calmly says he believes "widespread public belief in the inevitability of profits from investment in stocks will lead to eventual trouble"—that's Buffett's quiet, almost amused skepticism of crowd behavior. He's not angry at the exuberant. He just knows they'll eventually get hurt.

## The Key Quote That Sums Up 1958

> "I will continue to forecast that our results will be above average in a declining or level market, but it will be all we can do to keep pace with a rising market."

He said it in 1957. He said it again in 1958. He'd keep saying it for years. And he was right—about both his strengths and his limitations.
