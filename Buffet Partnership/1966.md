# Summary of Buffett's Tenth Year (1966)

## The Performance: A Record Margin (But Not Repeatable)

The Dow had a terrible year, falling **15.6%** (including dividends). The Partnership gained **20.4%** before allocation to the general partner. Limited partners received **16.8%**.

The margin over the Dow was **36 percentage points** – the largest in the partnership's history.

But Buffett was not celebratory. He called it "pleasant but non-repeatable." The Dow's poor performance was partially due to the heavy weighting of high-priced components like DuPont and GM, which lagged badly. Most investment managers actually outperformed the Dow in 1966 – it was an easy year to beat that particular index.

Cumulative since 1957: Partnership up **1156.0%** vs. Dow up **122.9%**. Annual compounded rate for limited partners: **23.9%** over ten years.

## The First Decade: Perspective and Humility

> "The results of the first ten years have absolutely no chance of being duplicated or even remotely approximated during the next decade."

He explained why: the environment had changed. In the early years, there were "substantial numbers of securities selling at well below the 'value to a private owner' criterion." Now, he found only "two or three new ideas a year" that met his standard of ten percentage points superior to the Dow.

Three factors: (1) a changed market environment; (2) increased size ($54 million in assets); (3) substantially more competition.

> "It is obvious that a business based upon only a trickle of fine ideas has poorer prospects than one based upon a steady flow of such ideas."

## The Hochschild, Kohn Acquisition (First Whole Business Purchase)

During the first half of 1966, Buffett and two 10% partners bought all of Hochschild, Kohn & Co., a privately owned Baltimore department store. This was the first time the partnership had purchased an entire business by negotiation.

> "We have topnotch people (both from a personal and business standpoint) handling the operation. Despite the edge that my extensive 75 cents an hour experience at the Penney's store in Omaha some years back gives us (I became an authority on the Minimum Wage Act), they will continue to run the business as in the past."

The investment represented only about 10% of BPL's net assets – he noted that he had over three times that amount in a single marketable security that would never come to public attention. "I still prefer the iceberg approach toward investment disclosure."

## Market Forecasting (Ground Rule #6, Revisited)

After the Dow declined from 995 to 865 in the spring of 1966, Buffett got calls from partners who thought stocks were going lower. His response was characteristically sharp:

> "If they knew in February that the Dow was going to 865 in May, why didn't they let me in on it then? And, if they didn't know what was going to happen during the ensuing three months back in February, how do they know in May?"

He added: "No one ever seems to call after the market has gone up one hundred points to focus my attention on how unclear everything is."

His policy: "We don't buy and sell stocks based upon what other people think the stock market is going to do (I never have an opinion) but rather upon what we think the company is going to do."

He recommended Benjamin Graham's *The Intelligent Investor*, Chapter 2 ("The Investor and Stock Market Fluctuations"), as "more investment importance than anything else that has been written."

**Key insight:** The "Relatively Undervalued" category was dominated by ONE holding – the same one that had outperformed in 1964 and 1965. This concentration (up to 40% of assets, per Ground Rule 7) was responsible for much of the partnership's success.

But he also noted that this same concentration would produce "wide swings in short term performance – some, most certainly, unpleasant."

## The Conflict Between Short-Term Performance and Long-Term Results

Buffett recounted a painful example from the "Generals – Private Owner" category. He had been buying a very attractive security, hoping to invest $10 million or more over two or three years. But the stock rose above cost, slowing his buying. Then, in 1966, another party issued a tender offer, and Buffett sold – realizing a profit of $1,269,181.

He was not happy.

> "We would have been much better off from a long range standpoint if 1966 results had been five percentage points worse and we were continuing to buy substantial quantities of the stock at the depressed prices."

This is a crucial Buffett insight: **he would rather have a lower reported return this year if it means a better position for future years.** Short-term performance can conflict with long-term wealth creation.

## The Generals – Relatively Undervalued (The Big One)

> "Our relative performance in this category was the best we have ever had – due to one holding which was our largest investment at yearend 1965 and also yearend 1966."

He spent "considerable effort continuously evaluating every facet of the company" and comparing it to alternatives. The concentration was intentional – and he warned partners to expect "substantial underperformance (far more likely in sharply rising markets)" in exchange for hoped-for long-term gains.

## How His Mind Works (1966 Edition)

**He is willing to change his own goals rather than chase the market:**
This letter sets the stage for his famous October 1967 letter (which the user will see next). He's already signaling that the old 10-point margin goal may be unrealistic. He's honest about diminishing opportunities.

**He distinguishes between "conventionalism" and true conservatism:**
He argued that the big funds, despite their "conservative" reputation, had not protected investors in down markets. Over the three full-year down periods (1957, 1960, 1962), the Dow declined 20.6% cumulatively – and the funds declined 9.7% to 24.6%. Not exactly safe.

> "Most investors would climb a rung intellectually if they clearly delineated between the above two interpretations of conservatism."

**He is comfortable being out of step:**
> "We will not go into businesses where technology which is away over my head is crucial to the investment decision. I know about as much about semi-conductors or integrated circuits as I do of the mating habits of the chrzaszcz (that's a Polish May bug)."

He knows his circle of competence and stays inside it.

**He thinks in terms of "expectancy," not certainty:**
The entire framework of categories, probabilities, and long-term averages is probabilistic. He doesn't need to be right every time – just more often than wrong, with the wins bigger than the losses.

## The Jokes

The "chrzaszcz" (Polish May bug) is a great bit – he even gives pronunciation advice ("rhyme it with thrzaszcz").

His Penney's store experience: "I became an authority on the Minimum Wage Act" – self-deprecating and funny.

The Herman Hickman quote about Yale football: "I see my job as one of keeping the alumni silent but not mutinous." Buffett uses this to describe his own modest objectives.

And his closing line about the partnership's anniversary: "The celebration was appropriate – an all-time record (both past and future) was established." The "both past and future" is a sly joke – he's saying it will never be repeated.

## The Key Quote

> "We don't buy and sell stocks based upon what other people think the stock market is going to do (I never have an opinion) but rather upon what we think the company is going to do. The course of the stock market will determine, to a great degree, when we will be right, but the accuracy of our analysis of the company will largely determine whether we will be right. In other words, we tend to concentrate on what should happen, not when it should happen."

This is the essence of value investing: focus on the *what*, not the *when*. Timing is secondary to analysis. The market tells you when you're right; your research tells you *if* you're right.
