# Summary of Buffett's Twelfth Year (1968)

## The Performance: A Freak Year (Again)

The Dow gained 7.7% (including dividends). The Partnership gained **58.8%** before allocation to the general partner. Limited partners received **45.6%**.

Cumulative since 1957: Partnership up **2610.6%** vs. Dow up **185.7%**. Annual compounded rate for limited partners: **25.3%** over twelve years.

Buffett called this result "a freak like picking up thirteen spades in a bridge game."

> "You bid the slam, make it look modest, pocket the money and then get back to work on the part scores. We will also have our share of hands when we go set."

He had begun 1968 feeling that prospects were poorer than at any time in the partnership's history. But "due in considerable measure to one simple but sound idea whose time had come (investment ideas, like women, are often more exciting than punctual)," they recorded an overall gain of over $40 million.

## The Investment Environment: "Acute Hypertension"

Buffett noted that the investment management business had swung from "excessive lethargy" to "acute hypertension." He quoted one manager (from a well-known firm handling over $1 billion in mutual funds) who said upon launching a new advisory service:

> "The complexities of national and international economics make money management a full-time job. A good money manager cannot maintain a study of securities on a week-by-week or even a day-by-day basis. Securities must be studied in a minute-by-minute program."

Buffett's response: "Wow! This sort of stuff makes me feel guilty when I go out for a Pepsi."

He observed that the "go-go" funds of previous years had been rechristened "no-go" funds. For example, Gerald Tsai's Manhattan Fund came in at minus 6.9% for 1968. Many smaller investment entities continued to outperform, but "in nothing like the quantities of 1966 and 1967."

## The Four Categories: 1968 Results

| Category | Average Investment | Overall Gain |
|----------|-------------------|---------------|
| Controls | $24,996,998 | $5,886,109 |
| Generals – Private Owner | $16,363,100 | $21,994,736 |
| Generals – Relatively Undervalued | $8,766,878 | $4,271,825 |
| Workouts | $18,980,602 | $7,317,128 |
| Miscellaneous (T-bills, etc.) | $12,744,973 | $839,496 |

**The standout:** Generals – Private Owner. This category, where Buffett felt "by far the greatest certainty regarding consistently decent results," produced almost $22 million on a $16 million average investment. He noted: "The cash register really rang on one simple industry idea (implemented in several ways) in this area in 1968."

But he also warned: "Our total investment in this category is presently under $2 million and I have nothing at all in the hopper to bolster this. What came through like the Johnstown flood in 1968 looks more like a leaky faucet in Altoona for 1969."

## The Controlled Companies: Berkshire and Diversified Retailing

Buffett reported that Diversified Retailing (80% owned) and Berkshire Hathaway (70% owned) had combined after-tax earnings of over $5 million.

He singled out two executives:

> "Particularly outstanding performances were turned in by Associated Cotton Shops, a subsidiary of DRC run by Ben Rosner, and National Indemnity Company, a subsidiary of B-H run by Jack Ringwalt. Both of these companies earned about 20% on capital employed in their businesses."

For context, among Fortune's "500," only 37 companies achieved 20% on capital in 1967. "Our boys outshone such mildly better-known (but not better appreciated) companies as IBM, General Electric, General Motors, Procter & Gamble, DuPont, Control Data, Hewlett-Packard, etc."

He also addressed partners who asked about Berkshire's stock price: "Say, Berkshire is up four points – that's great!" His response:

> "Market price is irrelevant to us in the valuation of our controlling interests. ... Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

## The Quality and Quantity of Ideas (All-Time Low)

Buffett repeated his warning from the October 1967 letter: the quality and quantity of ideas was "presently at an all-time low."

> "I can't emphasize too strongly that the quality and quantity of ideas is presently at an all time low – the product of the factors mentioned in my October 9th, 1967 letter, which have largely been intensified since then."

He used a baseball metaphor:

> "It is possible for an old, overweight ball player, whose legs and batting eye are gone, to tag a fast ball on the nose for a pinch-hit home run, but you don't change your line-up because of it."

## The Growth of the Partnership (Retrospective)

Buffett provided a fascinating snapshot of the partnership's growth:

- **May 5, 1956:** Buffett Associates, Ltd. formed with seven limited partners, $105,000 contributed, and the general partner's $100.
- **January 1, 1957:** Combined net assets $303,726. Gain for the year: $31,615.97 (10.4%).
- **1968:** Net assets $104,429,431.

He calculated that in 1968, with the NYSE open about 1,200 hours, their gain was about $33,000 per hour – "roughly the same as the full year gain in 1957."

Meanwhile, expenses had barely budged since 1963: rent from $3,947 to $5,823, travel from $3,206 to $3,603, dues from $900 to $994. "If one of Parkinson's Laws is operating, at least the situation hasn't gotten completely out of control."

He quoted Gypsy Rose Lee on her 55th birthday: "I have everything I had twenty years ago – it's just that it's all lower." But for the partnership, assets had gone up, not down.

## How His Mind Works (1968 Edition)

**He is brutally honest about the future:**
> "We have a number of important negatives operating on our future and, while they shouldn't add up to futility, they certainly don't add up to more than an average of quite moderate profitability."

No sales pitch. No hype. Just reality.

**He separates price from value with absolute clarity:**
> "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

This is the simplest and most powerful statement of his philosophy. Market price is an input, not an output. Value is what matters.

**He values operating talent as much as investment talent:**
The praise for Ben Rosner and Jack Ringwalt is effusive. He knows that great businesses are run by great people. He's not trying to be a hero – he's trying to back heroes.

**He is amused by the frenzy around him, not tempted by it:**
The "minute-by-minute" quote from the investment manager is held up for gentle ridicule. Buffett's "wow" and his guilt about going out for a Pepsi are pure Buffett – he's not going to change his pace to match the hyperactive crowd.

**He understands that past success does not guarantee future results:**
The "old, overweight ball player" metaphor is a confession. He knows that 1968's home run doesn't mean the lineup is fixed. The pipeline is drying up.

## The Jokes

The Gypsy Rose Lee quote is perfect – a 55-year-old burlesque star saying her assets are all lower. Buffett uses it to contrast with the partnership's growing assets.

The "Johnstown flood vs. leaky faucet in Altoona" is a great Midwestern analogy.

The "minute-by-minute" investment manager – Buffett doesn't even need to mock him directly. He just quotes him and says "Wow!" That's enough.

And his note about not signing a percentage lease for the office (so rent barely increased) – he's proud of his frugality, and he should be.

## The Key Quote

> "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

Short. Memorable. The entire investment philosophy in seven words. It appears here in the context of Berkshire Hathaway's valuation – but it applies to every security he ever bought.

But the quote that best captures the mood of 1968 might be:

> "It is possible for an old, overweight ball player, whose legs and batting eye are gone, to tag a fast ball on the nose for a pinch-hit home run, but you don't change your line-up because of it."

He knew 1968 was an anomaly. He knew the future would be harder. And he was preparing his partners – and himself – for what came next.
