# Summary of Buffett's Thirteenth Year (1969) – The End of the Partnership

## The Announcement: Retirement

On May 29, 1969, Buffett wrote a short, stunning letter to his partners. After twelve years of compounding at over 25% annually, he was retiring.

> "Before yearend, I intend to give all limited partners the required formal notice of my intention to retire."

The reasons were the same ones he had outlined in October 1967, but now intensified.

**The investing environment had become "more negative and frustrating":**
- Quantitative bargains had "virtually disappeared"
- Their $100 million in assets eliminated companies with less than about $100 million in market value
- The swelling interest in investment performance had created an "increasingly short-term oriented and (in my opinion) more speculative market"

**But the most important factor was personal:**
> "I expressed a desire to be relieved of the (self-imposed) necessity of focusing 100% on BPL. I have flunked this test completely during the last eighteen months."

He couldn't slow down while he was on stage. "The only way to slow down is to stop."

## The 1969 Performance (A Poor Year)

Buffett was honest: "I hate to end with a poor year, but we are going to have one in 1969."

His best guess: "a breakeven result for 1969 before any monthly payments to partners." This turned out to be slightly better than breakeven – he later said overall gain slightly over 6% – but it was a far cry from the 58.8% of 1968.

Workouts had been "atrocious." He felt "like the bird that inadvertently flew into the middle of a badminton game."

> "Documenting one's boners is unpleasant business. I find 'selective reporting' even more distasteful. Our poor experience this year is 100% my fault. It did not reflect bad luck, but rather an improper assessment of a very fast-developing governmental trend."

He had long believed the government should be doing what it finally did – but he didn't think it would happen. "I never believe in mixing what I think should happen (socially) with what I think will happen in making decisions – in this case, we would be some millions better off if I had."

## The Liquidation Plan

Buffett outlined a three-part plan:

1. **Cash distribution** – at least 56% (later increased to 64-70%) of January 1, 1969 capital, to be paid in early January 1970.

2. **Controlled company stock** – partners could either take cash or maintain proportional interests in Diversified Retailing Company (DRC) and Berkshire Hathaway (BH). These would be valued at fair value (not market price) and would represent about 30-35% of capital.

3. **Residual assets** – to be cleaned up over time, with final distribution by June 30, 1970.

## Bill Ruane: The Recommended Successor

Buffett had to suggest an alternative for partners who didn't want to manage their own money. He chose Bill Ruane, whom he had met in Ben Graham's class at Columbia in 1951.

> "If Susie and I were to die while our children are minors, he is one of three trustees who have carte blanche on investment matters."

He was candid about Ruane's record: from 1956-1961 and 1964-1968, his individual accounts averaged over 40% per annum. But in 1962, he was down about 50%. "While two years may sound like a short time when included in a table of performance, it may feel like a long time when your net worth is down 50%."

Buffett's assessment: "I consider Bill to be an exceptionally high probability decision on character and a high probability one on investment performance."

## The Bond vs. Stock Decision (A Major Shift in Thinking)

Buffett offered a remarkable analysis: for the first time in his investment lifetime, he believed there was "little choice for the average investor between professionally managed money in stocks and passive investment in bonds."

His reasoning:
- Tax-free bonds were yielding **6.5%** with excellent quality
- The ten-year expectation for stocks as a group: about 9% pre-tax (3% dividends + 6% gain in value)
- After-tax for a 40% bracket taxpayer: about 6.5% from stocks, the same as from tax-free bonds
- Even "excellent management" might only add 2-4 points per annum

> "The rather startling conclusion is that under today's historically unusual conditions, passive investment in tax-free bonds is likely to be fully the equivalent of expectations from professionally managed money in stocks."

He was not predicting. He was observing the math.

## The Controlled Companies: DRC and Berkshire Hathaway

In the December 5, 1969 letter, Buffett provided detailed information on the two companies partners could choose to hold.

**Diversified Retailing Company (DRC):**
- Owned 100% of Associated Retail Stores (formerly Associated Cotton Shops) – an excellent business with $37.5 million sales, ~$1 million net income
- Had sold Hochschild, Kohn for about $11 million
- Net tangible assets: $11.50-12.00 per share

**Berkshire Hathaway Inc. (BH):**
- Textile operation: employed $16 per share in capital, "cannot be judged a satisfactory business"
- Insurance (National Indemnity & National Fire & Marine): first-class, earning good returns on $15 per share of capital
- Illinois National Bank: first-class, $17 per share of capital
- Total tangible net asset value: about $43 per share (book value ~$45)

He estimated normal current earning power for BH at about $4 per share, with good growth prospects. His personal opinion: intrinsic value would grow at "approximately 10% per annum."

> "I think about them as businesses, not 'stocks,' and if the business does all right over the long term, so will the stock."

## The Dilemma of Restricted Stock

The DRC and BH shares were not freely marketable due to SEC restrictions. Buffett had wanted to provide a way for partners to convert them to cash, but after legal review, he concluded there was "no prudent way" to implement that.

He explained why he didn't register the shares for public sale:
- There was no existing market for DRC
- BH's floating supply was tiny (their 691,441 shares were 4-5 times the normal trading volume)
- A simultaneous sale by many partners could create "little short of chaotic" markets

Instead, partners who wanted to sell would have to do so via private placement. Buffett believed this would get them better prices.

He added: "I intend to hold our block of Blue Chip Stamps in BPL for a more advantageous disposal" – which showed he was still thinking like an investor, even in liquidation.

## How His Mind Works (1969 Edition)

**He knows when to quit.**
The most important insight of the year: Buffett recognized that the environment had changed, that his edge had diminished, and that he couldn't slow down while still running the partnership. So he stopped. That takes more self-awareness than starting.

**He takes full blame for mistakes.**
> "Our poor experience this year is 100% my fault."

No excuses. No blaming the government or the market. He misjudged, and he said so.

**He separates social goals from investment decisions.**
> "I never believe in mixing what I think should happen (socially) with what I think will happen in making decisions."

He wanted the government to act. He just didn't think it would happen quickly. He was wrong about the timing – and it cost him. But he doesn't regret the social outcome, only his investment mistake.

**He cares about his partners' outcomes after he's gone.**
The entire Ruane recommendation, the detailed analysis of bonds vs. stocks, the careful explanations of restricted stock – he wasn't just walking away. He was trying to leave people in a position to succeed.

**He values character as much as competence.**
> "I consider Bill to be an exceptionally high probability decision on character."

This is classic Buffett. He invests in people he trusts. Ruane had a 50% down year – but Buffett still recommended him because of integrity and ability.

## The Jokes

The "bird that inadvertently flew into the middle of a badminton game" – a perfect image for 1969's workout disasters.

The Gypsy Rose Lee quote from 1968 reappears in spirit, but the humor in 1969 is more subdued. The best line might be his self-deprecating admission: "I hate to end with a poor year" – he wanted to go out with a bang, but reality had other plans.

## The Key Quote

> "The only way to slow down is to stop."

After twelve years of compounding at 23.9% for limited partners, after building $105,100 into over $100 million, after beating the Dow in eleven of twelve years (and tying in the twelfth?), he walked away.

Not because he lost his touch. Not because the money wasn't there. Because he wanted to live a different life.

That's the mark of a man who knew what mattered. And it's why, even though the partnership ended, Warren Buffett was just getting started.
