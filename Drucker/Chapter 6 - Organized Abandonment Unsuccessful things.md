# Summary of Chapter 6: The Future of the Corporation and the Way Ahead

## Key Concepts

**Five Traditional Assumptions About the Corporation (1870–1970)**
1. The corporation is the “master,” the employee is the “servant” (corporation owns the means of production)
2. Most employees work full-time for the corporation; their job provides their only income
3. Most efficient way to produce is to bring as many activities as possible under one management (integration)
4. Suppliers/manufacturers have market power because they have information customers don’t have
5. One technology pertains to one industry, and vice versa

**All Five Have Been Turned Upside Down (since 1970)**

---

## New Assumptions

**1. Knowledge is the means of production**
- Owned by knowledge workers and highly portable
- Knowledge workers provide “capital” just as money providers do
- Makes knowledge worker an equal – an associate or partner

**2. Changing employment patterns**
- Many will still have full-time jobs, but a growing number will be part-timers, temporaries, consultants, or contractors
- Many who work for an organization may be employees of an outsourcing contractor

**3. Integration is no longer optimal**
- Knowledge is highly specialized; maintaining critical mass for every major task is increasingly expensive and difficult
- Knowledge deteriorates unless used constantly – intermittent activity guarantees incompetence
- Communications costs have become insignificant (business literacy grew; now Internet and e-mail practically eliminate physical costs)
- Most productive way to organize is to **disintegrate**
- Examples: outsourcing IT, manufacturing (Apple outsourced hardware to Japan/Singapore; Japanese companies outsourced to American contract manufacturers)
- Professional Employee Organizations (PEOs) and Business Processing Organizations (BPOs) now manage HR for millions of workers

**4. Customer now has the information**
- Whoever has the information has the power
- Power shifting to the customer (another business or ultimate consumer)
- Supplier will cease to be a seller and become a buyer *for* the customer

**5. Few unique technologies anymore**
- Knowledge needed in a given industry often comes from a totally different technology
- Fiberglass cables: developed by glass company (Corning), not telephone industry
- More than half of Bell Labs’ important inventions applied mainly outside the telephone industry

---

## The Future Corporation

**No Single Model**
- Different corporations will emerge, not just one kind
- The corporation acquires local characteristics but fundamental changes are underway

**Key Drivers of Change (since 1970)**
- Emergence of new institutional investors (pension funds, mutual trusts)
- Emergence of knowledge workers as the economy’s big new resource

**Multiple Human Organizations Within One Legal Entity**
- Traditional organization of full-time employees
- Closely linked but separately managed group of older people (associates/affiliates, not employees)
- “Perimeter” groups: people working for outsourcing contractors – no contractual relationship with the business they work for

**Managing Knowledge Workers**
- Bribery (bonuses, stock options) doesn’t work
- Treat and manage as **volunteers** (like nonprofits)
- They want to know: What is the company trying to do? Where is it going?
- They want personal achievement, responsibility, continuous learning/training
- They want respect – not for themselves but for their area of knowledge
- They expect to make decisions in their own area

## From Corporation to Confederation

**General Motors Example**
- First developed organizational concepts for large corporations (1920s)
- Based on: we own as much as possible; we own everything we do
- Now experimenting as minority partner in competing companies (Saab, Suzuki, Isuzu)
- Divested 70–80% of what it manufactures; turning itself into a merchant

**Toyota Way**
- Restructuring around core competency: manufacturing
- Moving from multiple suppliers to one or two everywhere
- Uses manufacturing competence to manage suppliers (they remain independent but part of Toyota in management terms)
- Similar to what Sears, Roebuck and Marks & Spencer did earlier

**Other Models**
- **Web site model**: Manufacturer creates worldwide site that takes orders and also takes orders for noncompeting products from smaller firms
- **Contract manufacturers**: Make products for competing Japanese consumer-electronics firms
- **Syndicate model** (in European Union): Medium-sized, family-owned manufacturers form a syndicate for production, delivery, and service in emerging markets (model: 19th-century farmers’ cooperative)

## Top Management’s New Responsibilities

- Direction, planning, strategy, values, principles
- Structure and relationships among various members
- Alliances, partnerships, joint ventures
- Research, design, innovation
- Management of key people and money (common resources)
- Representation to outside world (governments, public, media, organized labor)

**Balancing Three Dimensions**
- Economic organization
- Human organization
- Social organization

**Three Models (each stressed one dimension)**
- German “social market economy” → social dimension
- Japanese model → human dimension
- American “shareholder sovereignty” → economic dimension

None is adequate alone. The biggest challenge for the large company may be **social legitimacy** – values, mission, vision.

---

## Getting Ready for New Realities (Action Areas)

**1. Future corporation**
- Experiment with new forms, pilot studies in alliances, partnerships, joint ventures
- New structures and tasks for top management
- New models for geographical and product diversification

**2. People policies**
- Must cover all who work for enterprise (whether employed or not)
- Attract, hold, and make productive people past retirement age, independent contractors, non-full-time workers
- Model: professor emeritus (no salary, paid for what they do)

**3. Outside information**
- Information revolution may have made managements *less* well informed
- Most information technology focuses on internal matters
- Most important changes are outside – not usually available in computer-usable form
- Need to ask what outside information is needed and devise systems to collect it

**4. Change agents**
- Every organization must turn itself into a change agent
- Organized abandonment of unsuccessful things
- Continuous improvement (kaizen) of every product, service, process
- Exploitation of successes (especially unexpected ones)
- Systematic innovation
- Changes the mindset: change seen as opportunity, not threat

---

## Future Trends We Are Not Yet Aware Of

**Information Revolution parallels earlier industrial revolutions**
- Speed and impact similar to first (steam engine, railroad, telegraph) and second (late 19th century) industrial revolutions
- Main effects still lie ahead

**New institutions and theories emerging**
- New economic regions (EU, NAFTA) – neither traditional free-trade nor protectionist
- Transnational financial institutions (Citigroup, Goldman Sachs) – money beyond control of any country
- Schumpeter’s dynamic disequilibrium, creative destruction, technology as change agent

**Prediction**
- Society of 2030 will be very different from today
- Central features will be new institutions and new theories, ideologies, problems – not information technology alone
