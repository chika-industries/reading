# Summary of Chapter 5: New Demographics

## Key Concepts

**Major Demographic Shifts**
- By 2030, people over 65 in Germany will account for almost half the adult population (up from one-fifth now)
- Germany’s working-age population will fall by a full quarter (from 40 million to 30 million)
- Japan’s population will shrink from ~128 million to ~95 million by 2050
- Birth rates in most developed countries have fallen to 1.3 per woman (well below replacement level of 2.1)
- The only developed country that has so far avoided this fate is America, though its birth rate is also below replacement

**Political Implications**
- Winning support of older people becomes a political imperative in every developed country
- Pensions are already a regular election issue
- By 2030, full retirement benefits age will rise to mid-seventies in all developed countries
- Fixed retirement ages may be abolished to prevent unbearable burden on working population
- Young and middle-aged workers already doubt pension money will be available when they retire

## Immigration: Needed but Unwanted

**The Immigration Challenge**
- Germany may need to import 1 million working-age immigrants each year just to maintain its workforce
- Other rich European countries face similar needs
- Japan has discussed admitting 500,000 Koreans annually (and sending them home after five years)

**Political Backlash**
- Xenophobic, anti-immigration parties have gained ground in Austria, Belgium, Denmark, northern Italy
- In America, immigration upsets traditional political alignments:
  - Unions oppose immigration (globalization stance)
  - Businesses clamor for workers
  - Latino and immigrant populations support lenient policies

**America’s Advantage**
- Long tradition of immigration and cultural integration
- Recent immigrants (Latinos, Asians) may be integrating faster than ever (1/3 of Latino immigrants marrying non-Latinos)
- America’s population will continue growing (slowly) for next 30–40 years
- Australia and Canada have similar traditions; Japan and most European countries do not

## The End of the Single Mass Market

**From Homogeneous to Split Markets**
- Since WWII, developed countries have had one mass culture and one mass market
- Demographics are now pulling in opposite directions
- Markets have been dominated by youth values, habits, and preferences (1950–2000)
- Now the market is splitting

**Examples of Market Split**
- Financial services: day-trading (under 45) vs. mutual funds and annuities (over 50) – both growing
- Continuing education of adults (incompatible with youth culture) may be fastest-growing industry
- China’s one-child families spend more on one child than earlier families spent on four or five
- Luxury youth market emerging, but it is not homogeneous like the post-Korean War mass market

---

## Two Distinct Workforces

**Under-50s vs. Over-50s**
- Younger group needs steady income from permanent or full-time jobs
- Older group has more choice and can combine traditional jobs, nonconventional work, and leisure flexibly

**Starting with Female Knowledge Technologists**
- Nurses, computer technologists, paralegals can take 15 years out for children and return to full-time work
- Knowledge work is the first in history well-adapted to women’s needs as childbearers and to increasing longevity

**Why the Split?**
1. A 50-year working life is too long for one kind of work
2. Employing organizations have shrinking life expectancy (most last less than 30 years)
3. Knowledge workers will increasingly outlive even successful organizations

**Second Careers**
- “Second career” and “second half of one’s life” are buzzwords in America
- Employees take early retirement when pension rights are guaranteed, but keep working
- Work freelance, part-time, as temporaries, for outsourcing contractors, or as contractors themselves
- Most common among knowledge workers

## Beware: Demographic Changes Are Unpredictable

**Past Surprises**
- 1925–1935: first “baby bust” – birth rate dropped by almost half
- Late 1930s: experts predicted U.S. population would peak in 1945 and decline
- 1947–1957: baby boom – birth rate doubled from 1.8 to 3.6; babies rose from 2.5 million to 4.1 million
- 1961–1975: another baby bust – birth rate fell from 3.7 to 1.8
- Late 1980s–early 1990s: “baby boom echo” – triggered by large-scale immigration

**What We Don’t Know**
- No one knows what caused the baby busts or the baby boom
- Both busts occurred when the economy was doing well (should have encouraged childbearing)
- Baby boom should never have happened (birth rates historically drop after major wars)
- We simply do not understand what determines birth rates in modern societies

**Conclusion**
- Demographics will be the most important factor in the next society
- It will also be the least predictable and least controllable one
