
### Part 6 Summary: Conclusion and Practical Implications

The authors conclude by summarizing what explains Buffett’s success and what lessons investors can learn.

#### Recap of key findings

- **Berkshire Hathaway’s Sharpe ratio (1976–2017) was 0.79** – very good but not superhuman.  
  - The market’s Sharpe ratio was 0.49.  
  - Buffett’s information ratio (adjusting for market beta) was 0.64.

- **How did Buffett become one of the richest people in the world?**  
  - He stuck to a good strategy for a **long time** (buying cheap, safe, high‑quality stocks).  
  - He survived rough periods (e.g., 44% drawdown 1998–2000) where others would have been forced into fire sales or career changes.  
  - He used **leverage of about 1.7 to 1**, boosting returns and risk proportionally.

- **His stock selection is more important than his CEO role** – public stocks outperformed private holdings.

- **His cheap leverage comes largely from insurance float** (35% of liabilities, average cost 1.72%, well below T‑bill rate).

- **Standard factors (market, size, value, momentum) do not explain his alpha, but adding BAB (betting against beta) and QMJ (quality minus junk) does.**  
  - Buffett buys safe (low beta), high‑quality (profitable, growing, stable), cheap (value) stocks.

#### Practical implications for investors

1. **Market efficiency debate**  
   - Buffett’s success is neither luck nor magic. It is a reward for implementing value and quality exposures that historically have generated high returns.  
   - This supports Buffett’s side of the 1984 coin‑flipping debate: investors from “Graham‑and‑Doddsville” succeed because they follow similar principles, not because of random chance.

2. **Academic factor returns are realizable in practice**  
   - Buffett’s real‑world success shows that high returns of factors (BAB, QMJ, HML) are not just “paper returns.” After transaction costs and funding costs, they can be achieved – at least by Buffett.

3. **Realistic performance goals**  
   - Many investors seek Sharpe ratios above 1 or 2, but Buffett became one of the richest people with a Sharpe ratio of **0.79**.  
   - Most investors should target a Sharpe ratio between 0.5 (market) and 0.8 (Buffett), rather than chasing unattainably high numbers with suboptimal strategies.

#### Final quote from Buffett (1994 Annual Report)
> “Ben Graham taught me 45 years ago that in investing it is not necessary to do extraordinary things to get extraordinary results.”
