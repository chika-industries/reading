
### Part 2 Summary: Buffett’s Leverage – Magnitude and Cost

Warren Buffett’s high returns come from both a strong Sharpe ratio (0.79) and the **use of leverage** to amplify those returns. The authors analyze Berkshire Hathaway’s balance sheet to measure leverage and identify its cheap sources.

#### How leverage is measured
Leverage \( L_t \) is defined as:

\[
L_t = \frac{\text{TA}_t^{\text{MV}} - \text{Cash}_t^{\text{MV}}}{\text{Equity}_t^{\text{MV}}}
\]

where:
- \(\text{TA}_t^{\text{MV}}\) = market value of total assets
- \(\text{Cash}_t^{\text{MV}}\) = cash holdings
- \(\text{Equity}_t^{\text{MV}}\) = market value of equity

Because some balance sheet items are only available at book value, the authors estimate market value of assets as:

\[
\text{TA}_t^{\text{MV}} = \text{TA}_t^{\text{BV}} + \text{Equity}_t^{\text{MV}} - \text{Equity}_t^{\text{BV}}
\]

#### Estimated leverage
- **Average leverage: about 1.7 to 1** (i.e., for every $1 of equity, Berkshire holds $1.70 of assets net of cash).
- An alternative measure including only debt and insurance float gives about 1.4 to 1.
- This leverage helps explain why Berkshire’s stock volatility (23.5%) is higher than the volatility of its public stock portfolio (16.2%).
- If an investor leveraged the market 1.7×, the market’s excess return would rise to ~12.7%, still far below Berkshire’s 18.6%.

#### Sources of cheap leverage

1. **Debt with high credit rating**  
   - Berkshire had a AAA rating from 1989 to 2009.  
   - In 2002, it issued the first ever **negative-coupon security** (a senior note with a warrant).

2. **Insurance float** (most important source)  
   - Insurance premiums are collected upfront; claims are paid later. This is like an interest-free or low-interest loan.  
   - Hand-collected data from annual reports show that **35% of Berkshire’s liabilities**, on average, are insurance float.  
   - **Table 3** shows the estimated average annual cost of this float is only **1.72%** – about **3 percentage points below the T-bill rate**.  
   - In many years, the cost was negative (i.e., Berkshire earned money on the float).

3. **Deferred tax liabilities**  
   - Accelerated depreciation (allowed by IRS rules) acts like an interest-free loan. Berkshire enjoys tax savings early and pays the same dollar amount later without interest.

4. **Derivative contracts sold** (e.g., equity index put options, credit default obligations)  
   - Berkshire receives premiums upfront and typically has **no collateral posting requirements**.  
   - This provides cheap financing because investors who cannot easily access leverage are willing to pay a premium for embedded leverage.

#### Practical implication
Buffett’s unique access to **stable, low-cost, term leverage** – especially from insurance float – is a critical component of his success. It allows him to magnify the returns from his stock selection without facing margin calls or fire sales.
