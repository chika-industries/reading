
### Part 1 Summary: Introduction, Motivation, and Buffett’s Track Record

The paper begins by noting that while much has been written about Warren Buffett, rigorous empirical analysis of his performance is scarce. The authors aim to explain Buffett’s returns using recent academic research on return drivers.

They frame Buffett’s success within the efficient market debate. Michael Jensen argued that Buffett might simply be lucky – like a winner of a coin-flipping contest. Buffett countered that many successful investors come from the same “Graham-and-Doddsville” intellectual village, suggesting skill. The authors test this by showing that standard factors (market, size, value, momentum) cannot explain Buffett’s alpha, but adding “betting against beta” (BAB) and “quality minus junk” (QMJ) factors can.

**Key findings preview:**  
- Berkshire Hathaway’s Sharpe ratio is 0.79 (1976–2017).  
- Alpha becomes insignificant when controlling for BAB and QMJ.  
- Buffett uses leverage of about 1.7 to 1.  
- His returns come from buying cheap, safe, high-quality stocks and leveraging them.  
- Public stock selection explains more of his performance than his role as a CEO.

**Data sources:** CRSP stock returns, Compustat balance sheets, Thomson Reuters 13F holdings, hand-collected annual reports, CRSP mutual fund data, and factor returns from Ken French’s website and previous papers.

**Buffett’s track record (1976–2017):**  
- A dollar invested in Berkshire in October 1976 grew to $3,685 by March 2017.  
- Average annual excess return: 18.6% vs. market’s 7.5%.  
- Volatility: 23.5% (market 15.3%).  
- Sharpe ratio: 0.79 (market 0.49).  
- Market beta: only 0.69.  
- Information ratio: 0.64.  
- Berkshire experienced significant drawdowns (e.g., lost 44% from June 1998 to Feb 2000 while market gained 32%), but Buffett’s reputation and corporate structure allowed him to persist.

**Comparison to other stocks and mutual funds (Table 1):**  
- Among all mutual funds with at least 40-year history, Berkshire’s Sharpe ratio (0.79) ranks in the 100th percentile (i.e., highest).  
- Among all common stocks with at least 40-year history, Berkshire also has the highest Sharpe and information ratios.  
- Figures 1 and 2 show Berkshire in the extreme right tail of the distribution of information ratios for long-surviving funds and stocks.

**Optimal combination with the market:**  
- The optimal mix would put 72% in Berkshire and 28% in the market, yielding a Sharpe ratio of 0.81 – nearly the same as 100% Berkshire (0.79). Thus, Berkshire’s performance is near-optimal.
