# Summary of Chapter 2: "Warrants: Options on the Future"

This chapter introduces warrants and the fundamental concepts needed to understand the book's investment system.

## Key Points

**What is a warrant?**
- A warrant is an **option to buy common stock** at a specified price (the **exercise price**) within a certain time period (until the **expiration date**)
- Example: Sperry Rand warrants allowed buying one share of common at $25 (later $28) until September 15, 1967
- Some warrants are **perpetual** (no expiration date), e.g., Alleghany Corporation, Atlas Corporation, Tri-Continental Corporation

**Leverage – double‑edged sword:**
- Warrants can multiply both profits and losses dramatically
- Tri‑Continental warrants: cost 3¢ in 1942, sold for 55⅝ four years later – a 187‑fold increase
- Universal Pictures warrants: fell from $39 to $1.50 in two years – a 96% loss

**The warrant‑stock diagram:**
- A powerful visual tool: plot stock price (horizontal axis) vs. warrant price (vertical axis)
- Each point represents the two prices at the same time
- The diagram reveals how warrant and stock prices move together

**The two basic rules relating warrant to stock prices:**
1. **Warrant price < Stock price** – because common stock may pay dividends and has other advantages
2. **Warrant price + Exercise price ≥ Stock price** – otherwise arbitrage would allow risk‑free profit (buy warrant, convert, sell stock)

**Adjusted warrants and adjusted exercise price:**
- Many warrants convert into more or less than one share of common (due to stock splits/dividends)
- **Adjusted warrant** = warrant divided by number of shares it converts into (so one adjusted warrant converts into exactly one share)
- **Adjusted exercise price** = exercise price divided by number of shares
- The two rules apply using these adjusted figures

**Normal price curves:**
- For each warrant, there is a predictable "curve" in the warrant‑stock diagram
- Curves drop toward the **minimum value line** as expiration approaches
- The **minimum value line** represents the second rule (W + E = S)
- These curves allow forecasting of future warrant prices based on stock price

**The warrant‑stock law:**
- Despite not knowing future stock prices, the relationship between warrant and stock prices is predictable
- This predictability forms the scientific foundation of the system described in later chapters

The chapter ends with Thorp realizing that a scientific stock market system was now just a matter of time – a realization that would later lead to collaboration with Kassouf.
