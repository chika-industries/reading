# Summary of Chapter 1: "A System Is Born"

This chapter describes Sheen Kassouf's personal journey discovering the investment system that would later become the book's focus.

## Key Points

**Initial failures with conventional approaches:**
- Kassouf began investing in 1957, initially following advisory services and buying Emerson Radio
- He lost $1,500 before eventually selling at a small profit—only to watch the stock triple in price later
- He spent countless hours in brokerage boardrooms, becoming a "boardroom bum"

**Rejection of chart reading:**
- He observed chartists (technicians) who claimed price patterns predict future performance
- He tested chart readers by covering stock names and asking for predictions—their results were no better than random guessing

**Rejection of fundamental analysis:**
- He tried buying "value" stocks like Columbia Broadcasting and General Dynamics based on company fundamentals
- Both stocks declined while speculative "cats and dogs" rose
- He found fundamental analysis impractical because future earnings are nearly impossible to estimate beyond 1-2 years

**The discovery:**
- While studying Textron warrants and comparing them to the common stock, he noticed Molybdenum warrants appeared extremely overpriced
- One evening, analyzing the price relationship between Molybdenum warrants and common stock, he realized he could structure an investment that would profit whether the stock went **up or down**

**Results:**
- He entered the Molybdenum situation and made steady profits through the 1962 stock market crash
- Profits continued even when prices rose after the Cuban missile crisis
- From 1961 to 1966, his investments yielded 25% annually with virtually no risk

The chapter establishes that the system was discovered through practical experience, not academic theory, and was later refined with Professor Thorp.
