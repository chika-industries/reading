# Summary of Chapter 3: "Short Selling – Profits in Bad Times"

This chapter explains short selling, a crucial tool that allows the system to make money when stock prices fall.

## Key Points

**Why short selling matters:**
- Stocks generally rise about 9–11% per year on average, but bear markets can be devastating
- The 1929–1932 crash reduced stocks to 13% of their original values
- In 1962, stocks dropped 26% in just 3½ months
- Short selling enables profits during such declines

**How short selling works (four steps):**
1. Sell a security you do not own at the current price
2. Borrow the security (through your broker), leaving sale proceeds as collateral
3. Later buy the security in the market at a lower price
4. Return the newly purchased certificate to the lender and keep the difference as profit

**Example:** Short selling American Tobacco at 47 in March 1962 and covering at 30 in June 1962 yields a $1,700 profit instead of a loss.

**Important rules:**
- **Up‑tick rule:** A short sale can only be executed on an up‑tick (the last different‑price transaction was lower)
- **Margin account required:** Minimum deposit (typically $2,000) plus initial margin (70% for most listed stocks at time of writing)
- **Maintenance margin:** At least 30% of current market value must be maintained

**Selling warrants short:**
- Table 3.1 shows that buying short‑term warrants (18 months before expiration) produced an average **loss of 46%** in 16 months
- Therefore, selling those same warrants short would yield an average **gain of 46%** in 16 months (34.5% per year)
- With 50% margin, the annual gain rises to 69%

**The avalanche effect:**
- As a security’s price falls, margin requirements decrease and profits accumulate, releasing cash
- This released cash can be used to sell **more** of the security short – pyramiding the position
- Example with Molybdenum warrants (falling from 13 to ½ over one year):
  - Initial $6,500 investment (50% margin) grows to $84,292 in profits – more than a 14‑fold increase
- Theoretically, if IBM fell steadily from 500 to 5, a $250 investment could become nearly $1.7 million with 50% margin and continuous reinvestment

**Caution:** The avalanche effect greatly increases potential profits but also magnifies risk if the price reverses. However, expiring warrants often fall “almost to zero,” making them ideal candidates for this technique.
