# Summary of Chapter 4: "The Basic System"

This chapter introduces the core investment method of the book: **hedging** – combining two or more risky investments so the risks nearly cancel out while much of the profit potential remains.

## Key Points

**What is hedging?**
- Combining investments to reduce risk
- The basic system: buy common stock and simultaneously sell short **overpriced warrants** of the same company

**Illustrative example (hypothetical XYZ company):**
- Common stock at $6, warrant at $3, exercise price $10, warrant expires in 18 months
- Sell short 100 warrants at $3, buy 100 common shares at $6 (mix = 1 to 1)
- **Result:** Guaranteed profit of $300–$700 if common stays above $10 at expiration; only lose if common falls below $3
- Initial margin ~$720, expected profit ~$300 in 18 months = **28% per annum**

**Why hedging works:**
- Shorting overpriced warrants alone is profitable on average but risky (see Chapter 3)
- Buying common stock alone tends to be profitable but also risky
- When combined, the risks cancel: if both go up, the common gains offset warrant losses; if both go down, warrant gains offset common losses
- Daily price fluctuations have little net effect on a hedged position

**Changing the mix:**
- **Mix** = number of adjusted warrants short per share of common long
- Different mixes provide different protection profiles
- **1.5 to 1 mix** (short 300 warrants, long 200 common): profit if common stays between 1½ and 27 at expiration; investment $1,740, expected return ~34% per annum
- **2 to 1 mix** (short 200 warrants, long 100 common): profit if common between 0 and 20 at expiration; investment $1,020, expected return ~40% per annum

**Deeper insight using the warrant‑stock diagram:**
- Zero‑profit lines for different mixes can be drawn (slope = 1/M, where M is the mix)
- Points below the zero‑profit line represent profit at expiration
- The normal price curves (from Chapter 2) show where the warrant‑common point is likely to move over time
