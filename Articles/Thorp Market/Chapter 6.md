# Summary of Chapter 6: "How to Use the Basic System"

This chapter provides a practical, step-by-step guide for selecting and executing basic‑system investments. It covers identifying suitable warrants, standardizing prices, comparing to historical norms, choosing the hedge mix, and managing the account.

## 1. Identifying Listed Warrants

- Warrants are not traded on the NYSE; about 15–20 are listed on the American Stock Exchange (AMEX).
- Use the *Wall Street Journal* financial pages or the AMEX handbook (Francis Emory Fitch, Inc.) to find current warrants and their terms (exercise price, expiration date, conversion ratio).
- Verify terms with Standard & Poor’s fact sheets, Moody’s manuals, or the transfer agent.

## 2. Picking Short‑Sale Candidates

Limit candidates using three filters:
- **Expiration less than four years** – these decline fastest and offer the greatest profit potential.
- **Stock price below 1.2 times the adjusted exercise price** – above this threshold, shorting warrants is usually unprofitable.
- **Warrant price not too low** – avoid warrants trading below $1 because the $2.50 margin requirement sharply reduces percentage returns.

From the July 21, 1966 list, applying these filters left five candidates: General Acceptance, Pacific Petroleums, Rio Algom, Sperry Rand, and Universal American.

## 3. Using the Warrant‑Stock Diagram

To compare warrants with different exercise prices, standardize prices:
- **Standardized common price (S/E)** = common stock price ÷ adjusted exercise price.
- **Standardized warrant price (W/E)** = adjusted warrant price ÷ adjusted exercise price.

Plot each candidate on the warrant‑stock diagram. Figure 6.3 shows “normal price curves” for warrants with 24, 18, 12, 6, and 1.5 months until expiration, based on post‑1946 average behavior. Candidates above or near these curves are most attractive for shorting.

## 4. Choosing the Mix

The mix is the number of adjusted warrants short per share of common long.

- A simple rule of thumb derived from historical slopes:
  - If S/E < 0.3 → short warrants only, do not buy common.
  - If 0.3 ≤ S/E ≤ 1.2 → short 3 warrants for each share of common bought (3‑to‑1 mix).
  - If S/E > 1.2 → do not use the basic system.
- For more precise selection, draw a **zero‑profit line** through the starting point with slope = 1/mix. Points below the line represent profit; points above represent loss.
- By comparing the zero‑profit line with the normal price curve, an investor can assess short‑term safety and choose a mix that matches his or her outlook (higher mix for downside protection, lower mix for upside protection).

## 5. Evaluating Profit Profiles

- For a chosen mix, the profit at expiration can be graphed as a function of the common stock price.
- The peak profit occurs when the common equals the adjusted exercise price.
- Break‑even points (where profit = 0) are found where the zero‑profit line intersects the minimum value line.

## 6. Dividing Capital Among Candidates

- With $10,000 or less, concentrate on the single best situation (usually the one expiring soonest).
- For larger amounts, diversify among the most attractive situations to reduce risk.
- On July 21, 1966, a suggested allocation: 40% Universal American, 40% Pacific Petroleums, 20% Sperry Rand.

## 7. Final Points

- Open a margin account at a brokerage that can borrow the warrants you intend to short and has no policy against shorting them.
- Avoid warrants with a large short interest; you may be forced to cover early.
- Avoid warrants trading below $1 because of the $2.50 margin rule.
- Use the simplified “three‑to‑one” rule if you have no strong opinion about the common’s direction; it historically produced about 25% annual returns.

## Summary of the Basic System Steps

1. Identify listed warrants.
2. Keep those expiring in less than four years.
3. Keep those with stock price < 1.2 × adjusted exercise price.
4. Keep only warrants near or above the normal price curve (Figure 6.3).
5. Choose a mix (3‑to‑1 is a simple default).
6. Open a margin account and invest in one or a few situations, depending on available capital.
