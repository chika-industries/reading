## 📘 Section 1 Summary: The Difficulty of Appraising Growth Stocks

Durand opens by noting that investors are eager to buy stocks with growth potential, especially when appreciation is a primary goal. All else being equal, a forward-looking investor prefers growth stocks. However, in a sophisticated market, growth stocks are quickly recognized, and their prices rise so much that their price‑earnings ratios and dividend yields become unusually high or low by conventional standards. This forces investors to ask a critical question: **Does the market sometimes overpay for growth?**

He identifies two categories of problems in growth‑stock valuation:

1. **Practical difficulties** – forecasting future sales, earnings, and dividends.
2. **Theoretical difficulties** – reducing those forecasts to present values.

Historically, it was casually assumed that the present value of a future dividend stream equals the sum of all expected payments discounted at a **uniform rate**. But doubts emerged early on. In 1938, J. B. Williams suggested using **non‑uniform** discount rates. Later, Clendenin and Van Cleave (1954) showed that discounting a perpetually growing dividend series at a constant rate can lead to **absurdities or paradoxes** – specifically, implied present values of **infinity**. They quipped that they had not yet seen a growth stock marketed at “infinity dollars per share,” but noted that skeptical investors would discount very large, remote dividends at a high rate to bring the value down.

Durand then argues that Clendenin and Van Cleave could have made their point even better by noting the **remarkable analogy** between growth‑stock appraisal and the famous **Petersburg Paradox**, which puzzled probability theorists for two centuries.

> **Key takeaway of Section 1:**  
> Traditional uniform‑discount models can produce infinite valuations for growth stocks, which is unrealistic. This problem mirrors the Petersburg Paradox, where an infinite expected value does not correspond to any rational price an investor would pay. The rest of the paper will explore that analogy and its implications.
