
## 🧠 Section 4 Summary: Attempts to Resolve the Petersburg Paradox

Durand reviews historical attempts to resolve the paradox, noting they fall into two broad categories:

1. **Denying the basic assumptions** (unrealistic game).
2. **Adding assumptions** to show the value to Paul is less than the mathematical expectation.

### Practical objections to the original game:
- Peter and Paul are mortal – the game cannot continue forever.
- Peter’s solvency is questionable – after only 35 tails, his liability exceeds the U.S. gold reserve; after 38 tails, it exceeds the national debt.
- Even if they stop after 100 tosses, the stakes are unimaginably large.

### Notable theoretical resolutions:

| Proposer | Approach | Key result |
|----------|----------|-------------|
| **Gabriel Cramer** (early 1700s) | Diminishing marginal utility of money | If utility is proportional to money up to \(2^{24}\) ducats then constant → moral expectation ≈ 13 ducats. If utility = sqrt(amount) → ≈ 2.9 ducats. |
| **Daniel Bernoulli** (1738) | Marginal utility inversely proportional to wealth | Expectation depends on Paul’s initial wealth. With 10 ducats → ~3 ducats; with 100 → ~4; with 1000 → ~6. Only infinite wealth yields infinite valuation. |
| **W. A. Whitworth** (~1830s) | Risk of gambler’s ruin | A prudent gambler risks only a constant proportion of wealth. Paul’s entrance fee depends on his resources – reminiscent of Bernoulli’s utility approach but derived from ruin probability. |

### Key insight from Whitworth:
The evaluation of any risky venture depends not only on the odds but also on **the proportion of the risk-taker’s resources** that must be committed. A prudent portfolio manager will demand a higher expected return when investing a large fraction of assets in a risky security.

### Summary of the section’s conclusion:
All proposed solutions **change the problem** in some way – finite game, utility instead of cash, or prudent gambling rules. None satisfy pure theoretical expectations, but they explain why a real Paul would not pay the infinite mathematical expectation. As Keynes later summarized: we don’t believe Peter will pay, we don’t know what to do with so much money, we don’t think we’ll ever win, and it’s irrational to risk a large sum for an infinitely larger but infinitely unlikely gain.
