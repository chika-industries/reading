
## 📈 Section 5 Summary: Implications for Growth-Stock Appraisal

Durand now applies the lessons from the Petersburg Paradox directly to valuing growth stocks.

### Parallel structure:
Just as Paul would hesitate to pay the infinite mathematical expectation of the Petersburg game, an investor (Paul) should hesitate to pay the full discounted value of a growth stock when \( g \geq i \). The reasons fall into the same two categories as before:

1. **Denying the basic assumptions** – indefinite growth at a constant rate.
2. **Arguing that the stock is worth less than its theoretical DCF value** – due to utility, risk, or finite horizons.

### Objections to indefinite growth:
- J. B. Williams recognized that perpetual constant growth is unrealistic. He proposed a **logistic growth curve** – exponential growth for a while, then leveling off to an asymptote. This ensures a finite present value.
- Clendenin and Van Cleave argued that sustained rapid growth requires long-run inflation.
- **Equilibrium theorist’s objection:** If \( g \geq i \), then the return on capital \( r = E/B \) must be \( \geq i/(1-p) \). But in long-run equilibrium, the marginal return on capital should equal the interest rate. Therefore, growth stocks represent **temporary** opportunities where limited capital can earn above‑equilibrium returns. The appraiser’s job is to estimate how long this departure from equilibrium will last.

### Utility and risk adjustments:
Even if indefinite growth is assumed, investors will still discount the theoretical value:
- **Cramer & Bernoulli’s diminishing marginal utility** – large, remote dividends have lower subjective utility, so their monetary value should be scaled down.
- **Whitworth’s diversification approach** – a prudent investor with finite resources can only commit a fraction of their portfolio to any single risky stock; otherwise, they risk ruin. This also lowers the price they would pay.

### Key conclusion of Section 5:
All the reasons that prevent Paul from paying the infinite expectation in the Petersburg game also prevent an investor from paying the full discounted value of a growth stock – especially when \( g \geq i \). Unless the investor has infinite resources, the rational price is finite and often much lower than the DCF formula suggests.
