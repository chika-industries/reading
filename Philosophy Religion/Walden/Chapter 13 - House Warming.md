### Summary of Chapter 13: "House-Warming"

In this chapter, Thoreau describes the arrival of winter and his preparations to make his simple dwelling warm and habitable for the cold months. He begins by noting the autumn harvest of wild apples, chestnuts, and cranberries, and the wasps that sought shelter in his house as the weather turned.

As November arrived, Thoreau focused on building a proper fireplace and chimney. He took particular care in his masonry, using second-hand bricks and stones from the pond shore. He reflects that the fireplace is the "most vital part of the house," and he lingered around it as it took shape. Though he had lived in the house since July, it was only when he could warm it with a fire that he felt he truly began to "inhabit" it.

He describes gathering fuel for the winter—dead wood from the forest, driftwood from the pond, and the stumps from his bean-field. He notes that every man looks at his woodpile with affection, and that wood warms him twice: once when he splits it, and again when it burns. The chapter includes a poetic passage ("Light-winged Smoke, Icarian bird") celebrating the hearth fire.

Thoreau also reflects on the ideal of a simple, single-room dwelling—a "vast, rude, substantial, primitive hall" without partitions or excessive furniture—where all of life's essential activities happen in one open space. He contrasts this with modern houses filled with hidden rooms and secrecy, where hospitality often means keeping guests at a distance.

Finally, he notes that the pond began to freeze over in December, and he withdrew further into his shell, keeping a bright fire both in his house and in his breast. The chapter captures the satisfaction of self-sufficiency and the warmth—literal and metaphorical—of facing winter on one's own terms.
