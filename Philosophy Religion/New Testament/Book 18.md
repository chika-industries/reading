Based on the PDF, the next book is the **Epistle of Paul to Philemon** (often called **Philemon**). It is the eighteenth book in the New Testament.

Here is a summary of its content:

- **Paul’s Greeting and Thanksgiving for Philemon (Verses 1–7)** : Paul, a prisoner of Jesus Christ, writes to Philemon, his “dearly beloved” and fellow laborer, along with Apphia, Archippus, and the church in Philemon’s house. He gives thanks for Philemon’s love and faith toward the Lord Jesus and all saints, praying that the communication of his faith may become effectual by acknowledging every good thing in them for Christ’s sake.

- **Appeal for Onesimus, a Runaway Slave (Verses 8–16)** : Paul appeals to Philemon not by commandment but by love, as Paul the aged and now a prisoner. He pleads for Onesimus, whom he has begotten in his bonds. Onesimus was previously unprofitable to Philemon but is now profitable to both. Paul sends him back, asking Philemon to receive him not as a servant but as a beloved brother, both in the flesh and in the Lord.

- **Paul’s Promise and Request for Preparation (Verses 17–22)** : Paul urges Philemon to receive Onesimus as he would receive Paul himself. If Onesimus has wronged Philemon or owes anything, Paul promises to repay it, though he reminds Philemon that he owes his own self to Paul. Paul expresses confidence in Philemon’s obedience and asks him to prepare a guest room, hoping to be granted to them through their prayers.

- **Final Greetings and Benediction (Verses 23–25)** : Paul sends greetings from Epaphras, Mark, Aristarchus, Demas, Luke, and others. He closes with a blessing: “The grace of our Lord Jesus Christ be with your spirit. Amen.”

In summary, Philemon is a personal letter from Paul to a Christian named Philemon, whose runaway slave Onesimus had come to Paul in prison and been converted. Paul sends Onesimus back with this letter, appealing to Philemon to forgive him and receive him as a brother in Christ, not merely as a slave. It is a powerful demonstration of Christian forgiveness, love, and the breaking down of social barriers in the gospel.
