Based on the PDF, the next book is the **Second Epistle of Paul the Apostle to the Corinthians** (often called **2 Corinthians**). It is the eighth book in the New Testament.

Here is a summary of its content as presented in the document:

- **Comfort in Tribulation and Paul's Sincerity (Chapters 1-2)** : Paul begins by blessing God for comfort in all his tribulations, which allows him to comfort others. He explains his change of plans to visit them was not due to lightness but to spare them, emphasizing that his preaching was not "yea and nay" but confirmed in Christ. He urges them to forgive and comfort a repentant offender, lest he be swallowed up with overmuch sorrow, affirming his love for them.

- **Ministers of the New Testament and the Veiled Heart (Chapters 3-4)** : Paul declares that believers are the epistle of Christ, written not with ink but with the Spirit. He contrasts the ministration of death (the law) with the ministration of the Spirit, which is glorious. Unlike Moses, who put a veil over his face, believers have open-faced beholding the glory of the Lord. He describes his ministry as not preaching himself but Christ Jesus the Lord, carrying in the body the dying of the Lord Jesus.

- **The Eternal House and the Ministry of Reconciliation (Chapters 5-6)** : Paul speaks of the earthly house dissolving but having an eternal building in heaven. He urges that those who live should not live unto themselves but unto Him who died for them. He delivers the core message that God was in Christ, reconciling the world unto Himself, and has committed to them the word of reconciliation, beseeching others to be reconciled to God. He commends his ministry in much patience, afflictions, and by the Holy Ghost.

- **Separation from Unbelievers and Paul's Joy (Chapters 7-8)** : Paul exhorts them to cleanse themselves from all filthiness, perfecting holiness. He expresses his great joy that they were made sorry after a godly manner, which wrought repentance. He then speaks of the grace of God bestowed on the churches of Macedonia, who in deep poverty gave liberally, and urges the Corinthians to also abound in this grace of giving.

In summary, 2 Corinthians is a deeply personal letter where Paul defends his apostolic authority and ministry. He explains his conduct, contrasts the glory of the new covenant with the old, and shares profound teachings on suffering, comfort, and reconciliation through Christ. He also addresses practical matters of repentance and the importance of generous giving.
