Based on the PDF, the next book is the **Epistle of Paul to Titus** (often called **Titus**). It is the seventeenth book in the New Testament.

Here is a summary of its content:

- **Paul’s Greeting and Purpose for Leaving Titus in Crete (Chapter 1)** : Paul introduces himself as a servant of God and an apostle of Jesus Christ. He addresses Titus as his “own son after the common faith.” He left Titus in Crete to set in order the things that are wanting and to ordain elders (bishops) in every city. Paul lists the qualifications for an elder: blameless, husband of one wife, having faithful children not accused of riot or unruly, a steward of God, not self-willed, not soon angry, not given to wine, no striker, not given to filthy lucre, but a lover of hospitality, a lover of good men, sober, just, holy, temperate, holding fast the faithful word. He warns that there are many unruly and vain talkers and deceivers, especially those of the circumcision, who must be rebuked sharply.

- **Teach Sound Doctrine and How to Exhort Different Groups (Chapter 2)** : Paul instructs Titus to speak the things which become sound doctrine. Older men should be sober, grave, temperate, sound in faith, charity, and patience. Older women should be reverent, not false accusers, not given to much wine, teachers of good things. They are to teach young women to love their husbands and children, to be discreet, chaste, keepers at home, good, obedient to their own husbands. Young men are to be sober-minded. Titus himself is to show a pattern of good works, with uncorruptness, gravity, sincerity, and sound speech. Servants are to be obedient to their masters, not answering back, not purloining, but showing good fidelity. The grace of God that brings salvation has appeared to all men, teaching us to deny ungodliness and worldly lusts and to live soberly, righteously, and godly in this present world.

- **Reminders of God’s Mercy and Exhortations to Good Works (Chapter 3)** : Paul reminds them to be subject to principalities and powers, to obey magistrates, to be ready to every good work, to speak evil of no man, to be no brawlers, but gentle, showing meekness to all men. He recalls that they themselves were once foolish, disobedient, deceived, serving lusts and pleasures. But the kindness and love of God our Savior toward man appeared, not by works of righteousness which they had done, but according to His mercy He saved them, by the washing of regeneration and renewing of the Holy Ghost. He urges that they maintain good works for necessary uses, that they be not unfruitful. He warns to avoid foolish questions, genealogies, and contentions about the law. He closes with personal greetings and instructions about Zenas the lawyer and Apollos.

In summary, Titus is a short pastoral letter in which Paul instructs his companion Titus on how to organize and lead the church in Crete. He emphasizes the importance of sound doctrine, proper church leadership, and godly behavior for all age groups. The letter highlights salvation as coming through God’s mercy, not works, and calls believers to devote themselves to good works as a response to that grace.
