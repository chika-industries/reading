Here is a summary of the next book, **1 Samuel**, based on the provided text.

The First Book of Samuel marks a major transition in Israel's history: the end of the period of the judges and the beginning of the monarchy. It records the rise of the prophet Samuel, the anointing and failure of Israel's first king (Saul), and the anointing of David, a man after God's own heart, who would succeed him.

**Part 1: Samuel: The Last Judge (Chapters 1-8)**
- **Hannah's Prayer and Samuel's Birth:** A barren woman named Hannah prays fervently at Shiloh, vowing that if God gives her a son, she will dedicate him to the Lord. God answers her prayer, and she names him Samuel. After he is weaned, she brings him to the temple to serve under Eli the priest.
- **Eli's Wicked Sons:** Eli's sons, Hophni and Phinehas, are corrupt priests who treat the Lord's offerings with contempt and commit immorality. Eli rebukes them too gently, and God declares judgment on his house.
- **The Lord Calls Samuel:** As a boy, Samuel hears a voice calling him in the night. After three times, Eli realizes it is the Lord and tells Samuel to respond. The Lord reveals to Samuel that He will judge Eli's house.
- **The Ark Captured and Returned:** Israel goes to battle against the Philistines and is defeated. They bring the Ark of the Covenant into camp, but the Philistines capture it. Eli's sons are killed, and upon hearing the news, Eli falls backward, breaks his neck, and dies. The Philistines place the Ark in the temple of their god Dagon, but the Lord afflicts them with tumors. They return the Ark on a cart drawn by milk cows.
- **Samuel Judges Israel:** Samuel calls Israel to repentance at Mizpeh. The Philistines attack, but the Lord thunders against them, and Israel prevails. Samuel judges Israel faithfully all his days, but in his old age, he appoints his corrupt sons as judges, and the people reject them.

**Part 2: Israel Demands a King (Chapters 8-12)**
- **The People's Request:** The elders of Israel demand that Samuel appoint a king to judge them "like all the nations." Samuel is displeased, and the Lord tells him they have not rejected Samuel but have rejected Him as King.
- **Samuel's Warning:** Samuel warns the people what a king will do—take their sons for war, their daughters for service, their fields and vineyards for his servants, and tax them heavily. The people refuse to listen and insist on a king.
- **Saul Chosen as King:** A tall, handsome Benjamite named Saul, son of Kish, is sent to find his father's lost donkeys. He consults Samuel, who anoints him privately. Later, Saul is publicly chosen by lot at Mizpeh and acclaimed as king.
- **Saul's First Victory:** When the Ammonites threaten Jabesh-gilead, Saul gathers an army and defeats them, proving his leadership and silencing the opposition.

**Part 3: The Reign and Rejection of King Saul (Chapters 13-15)**
- **Saul's First Disobedience:** Facing a massive Philistine army, Saul grows impatient waiting for Samuel to offer sacrifices. He presumes to offer the burnt offering himself. Samuel arrives and rebukes him, declaring his kingdom will not continue and God has sought "a man after his own heart."
- **Jonathan's Heroism:** Saul's son Jonathan, with only his armor-bearer, attacks a Philistine outpost, causing a panic. The Lord delivers the Philistines, and Israel wins a great victory.
- **Saul's Rash Vow:** During the battle, Saul places the people under a foolish oath, cursing anyone who eats before evening. Jonathan, unaware, eats honey and is nearly executed by his father, but the people rescue him.
- **The Amalekite Campaign:** God commands Saul to utterly destroy the Amalekites and all they have. Saul defeats them but spares their king, Agag, and the best of the livestock, claiming they were for sacrifices.
- **Saul Rejected as King:** Samuel confronts Saul, declaring that "to obey is better than sacrifice." Because Saul has rejected the word of the Lord, the Lord has rejected him from being king. Samuel kills Agag himself and never sees Saul again, though he mourns for him.

**Part 4: The Rise of David and the Fall of Saul (Chapters 16-31)**
- **David Anointed:** The Lord sends Samuel to Bethlehem to anoint one of Jesse's sons as the next king. Jesse presents his older sons, but the Lord rejects them, looking on the heart. The youngest, David, a shepherd boy, is brought in, and Samuel anoints him. The Spirit of the Lord comes upon David from that day forward, and the Spirit departs from Saul.
- **David Serves Saul:** An evil spirit troubles Saul. His servants recommend a skilled harpist, and David is brought to the court. Saul loves him greatly and makes him his armor-bearer. David plays the harp to soothe Saul when the evil spirit comes upon him.
- **David and Goliath:** The Philistines gather for war, and their champion, a giant named Goliath of Gath, defies the armies of Israel for forty days. David, visiting the camp, hears the challenge and is indignant. Armed only with a sling and five stones, he faces Goliath, declaring "the battle is the Lord's." He strikes the giant with a stone and cuts off his head with Goliath's own sword, routing the Philistines.
- **Saul's Jealousy:** David becomes a national hero. Women sing, "Saul hath slain his thousands, and David his ten thousands." Saul becomes intensely jealous and fears David. He tries to pin David to the wall with a spear, and David flees.
- **David as an Outcast:** David becomes a fugitive, gathering a band of loyal men. He spares Saul's life twice when he has the opportunity to kill him, refusing to touch "the Lord's anointed." Jonathan, Saul's son, makes a covenant with David, loving him as his own soul.
- **Saul's Downfall:** The Philistines gather against Israel. Saul, terrified and receiving no answer from the Lord, consults a medium at En-dor, whom he had previously banned. The spirit of Samuel appears and tells Saul that because of his disobedience, the Lord will give Israel into Philistine hands and he and his sons will die.
- **The Death of Saul:** In the battle on Mount Gilboa, Israel is defeated. Saul's sons, including Jonathan, are killed. Saul, badly wounded, falls on his sword to avoid capture. Thus, the first king of Israel dies in disgrace.

The book ends with the Philistines triumphing and David mourning the death of his beloved friend Jonathan and the king, Saul, in a beautiful lament, "The Song of the Bow."
