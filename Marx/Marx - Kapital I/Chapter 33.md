Here is a summary of Chapter 33 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 33: The Modern Theory of Colonization

In this final chapter of Volume 1, Marx turns to the colonies—specifically, European settlements in North America, Australia, and elsewhere. He argues that the colonies reveal the **true nature of capitalist production** by showing what happens when the conditions necessary for capitalism are absent. The "modern theory of colonization" is the theory that capitalists themselves developed to explain why their ventures in the colonies kept failing—and how to fix the problem.

#### The Failure of Capitalism in the Colonies

When European capitalists tried to establish capitalist production in the colonies, they ran into an unexpected problem: **workers refused to become permanent wage-labourers**. In colonies where land was abundant and freely available (e.g., North America, Australia), workers could simply leave their jobs, take up a piece of land, and become independent farmers. The capitalist could not keep them tied to the factory or plantation.

This undermined the most basic condition of capitalism: a **propertyless working class** that has no choice but to sell its labour-power. In the colonies, workers had an alternative. They would work for wages only long enough to save a little money, then they would buy or claim land and become self-sufficient producers. The capitalist thus faced a constant labour shortage and high turnover.

#### The "Modern Theory" of Colonization

The capitalists and their economists (e.g., Edward Gibbon Wakefield) responded by developing a **theory of colonization**—a practical guide to creating capitalism in the colonies. Wakefield, in particular, argued that the problem was the availability of cheap or free land. His solution was to **artificially raise the price of land** so that workers could not afford to become independent farmers.

By making land expensive, the state would force workers to remain in the wage-labour force. They would have to work for capitalists for a long period (perhaps their whole lives) before they could afford land. In the meantime, the capitalists would have a stable, exploitable workforce.

#### The Key Elements of Wakefield's System

Wakefield's "systematic colonization" included:

- **Land sold at a "sufficient price"** —high enough to prevent workers from quickly leaving wage-labour.
- **The proceeds used to subsidize immigration** of more workers, ensuring a constant supply of labour.
- **Colonial government management** to enforce these conditions, including laws against squatting (occupying unused land) and limits on land grants.

This system was implemented in Australia, New Zealand, and elsewhere. It was explicitly designed to **recreate in the colonies the conditions of the mother country**: a landless proletariat forced to sell its labour-power to capital.

#### What the Colonies Reveal About Capitalism

Marx argues that the colonies are a **crucial experiment**. They show that capitalism is not a natural or universal system. It requires specific, artificially created conditions:

- **The separation of the worker from the means of production** (land, tools).
- **The concentration of the means of production in the hands of a capitalist class**.
- **The absence of any alternative livelihood** for the worker.

In the mother countries (England, Europe), these conditions were created by centuries of primitive accumulation—expropriation, violence, and state coercion. In the colonies, where land was abundant, these conditions did not exist. So the capitalists had to use the state to **manufacture** them: to make land artificially scarce, to force workers into wage-labour, and to prevent them from escaping.

#### The Irony of Bourgeois Economics

Marx concludes with a sharp irony. In Europe, bourgeois economists celebrated the "free" wage-labourer and claimed that capitalism was the natural outcome of thrift, freedom, and individual initiative. But in the colonies, faced with the absence of their system's preconditions, the same capitalists demanded **state intervention**—the artificial raising of land prices, restrictions on settlement, and the forced creation of a proletariat.

> **"Wakefield discovered that in the colonies, property in money, means of subsistence, machines, and other means of production does not yet stamp a man as a capitalist if the essential complement—the wage-worker—is missing. He discovered that capital is not a thing but a social relation between persons, mediated by things."**

The modern theory of colonization thus **unwittingly confirms Marx's own theory**: capital is not a thing but a social relation. It requires a specific class structure: a class of propertyless workers and a class of capitalists who own the means of production. Where that structure does not exist, it must be created by force or fraud.

#### Conclusion

This chapter serves as a powerful capstone to Volume 1. By examining the colonies, Marx demonstrates that capitalism is not a natural or eternal system but a historically specific one, dependent on the violent expropriation of the direct producers. The "modern theory of colonization" is a confession by the capitalists themselves that their system cannot survive without the state's help to maintain the class structure. And it confirms that **the secret of primitive accumulation** is not thrift or industry but expropriation, violence, and the artificial creation of a propertyless working class.
