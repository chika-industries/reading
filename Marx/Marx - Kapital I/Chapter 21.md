Here is a summary of Chapter 21 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 21: Piece-Wages

In this chapter, Marx examines **piece-wages**, the second major form of wages, where the worker is paid not for the time worked but for each unit of output (per piece, per dozen, per yard, etc.). He shows that piece-wage is not a fundamentally different form from time-wage but rather a **modified form** of it, with its own specific mechanisms for increasing exploitation and disciplining the workforce.

#### Piece-Wage as a Converted Form of Time-Wage

Piece-wage appears to be the price of the *product* of labour, as if the capitalist is buying the finished commodity directly from the worker. But in reality, piece-wage is simply a disguised form of time-wage. The price per piece is calculated by determining the average time required to produce one piece and then multiplying that by the hourly wage.

For example, if the normal working day is 12 hours, the daily value of labour-power is 3 shillings, and the worker produces 24 pieces in a day, then the price per piece is 3 shillings / 24 = 1½ pence. The piece-wage therefore expresses the same relationship as the time-wage, but it appears to reward the worker directly for his output.

#### The Advantages of Piece-Wages for Capital

Piece-wages offer several significant advantages to capital over time-wages:

1.  **Quality Control:** Since the worker is only paid for pieces that meet the required standard, the capitalist shifts the cost of defective work onto the worker. The worker must ensure quality, or he works for nothing.

2.  **Intensification of Labour:** Under piece-wages, the worker has a direct incentive to work faster and more intensely. The more he produces, the more he earns. This leads to a natural intensification of labour, often without the need for direct supervision. The worker becomes his own overseer, driving himself to exhaustion.

3.  **Increased Surplus-Value:** Because piece-wages encourage the worker to work harder and longer, they tend to increase the length and intensity of the working day beyond normal limits. The worker, eager to earn more, may voluntarily extend his working hours or skip breaks. This allows capital to extract more surplus labour without formally lengthening the working day.

4.  **Leveling of Wages:** Piece-wages tend to equalize the earnings of workers of different skill levels over time. A slower worker earns less; a faster worker earns more. But the average wage per piece is calculated on the average productivity. This creates a constant pressure on slower workers to catch up, and it allows capital to dismiss less productive workers without penalty.

5.  **Reduction of Supervision Costs:** Since the piece-wage system largely supervises itself (the worker's own desire for earnings drives productivity), the capitalist can reduce the number of overseers and managers. This saves on constant capital and increases the rate of profit.

#### The Disadvantages and Limits of Piece-Wages for Capital

Piece-wages are not an unmixed blessing for capital. Marx identifies several potential drawbacks:

- **Incentive to Lower Quality:** If the worker is paid only by quantity, there is a temptation to rush and produce shoddy work. Capital must therefore impose strict quality controls, which reintroduce supervision costs.
- **Difficulty in Calculating the Standard:** Establishing the "normal" output per day (the basis for the piece rate) is a constant source of conflict. Workers will try to set a high rate per piece; capitalists will try to set a low rate. This leads to a perpetual struggle over the "price of labour."
- **The "Speed-Up" and Its Consequences:** Once workers begin producing more than the normal output, the capitalist will lower the piece rate, arguing that the "normal" output has increased. This is a classic capitalist tactic: workers who work harder and faster end up being paid less per piece, as the rate is constantly adjusted downward. This fuels resentment and class struggle.

#### Piece-Wages and the Division of the Working Day

Under piece-wages, the distinction between necessary labour and surplus labour becomes even more obscured than under time-wages. The worker sees a direct connection between his effort and his earnings. He may believe that he is being paid for his labour, not for his labour-power. The fact that part of his working day is unpaid is hidden because his total earnings appear to be the sum of the piece rates for all the pieces he produces.

In reality, the piece rate is set so that the worker must produce a certain number of pieces just to cover the value of his labour-power (the necessary labour). Any pieces beyond that are produced during surplus labour time and represent surplus-value. But the worker experiences this not as unpaid labour but as the reward for his extra effort.

#### The Historical Role of Piece-Wages

Piece-wages played a historically significant role in the development of capitalism. They were particularly important in:

- **Agriculture (harvesting):** Paying by the bushel or by the acre incentivized speed and intensity.
- **Mining:** Paying by the ton of coal or ore extracted.
- **Domestic industry (outwork):** Where workers laboured in their own homes, piece-wages were the dominant form, as supervision was impossible.

However, piece-wages also became a tool for the intensification of exploitation and the degradation of working conditions. Marx notes that the struggle over piece rates is often more intense than over time-wages, because the connection between effort and pay is so direct.

#### The Relationship between Time-Wages and Piece-Wages

Marx concludes that piece-wage is simply a **modified form** of time-wage. The price per piece is derived from the value of labour-power and the normal working day. But once established, piece-wage takes on a life of its own. It appears to give the worker a direct interest in his own exploitation, as he voluntarily intensifies his labour to increase his earnings, thereby increasing the surplus-value extracted by capital.

In summary, Chapter 21 demonstrates that piece-wages, far from being a fair or transparent form of payment, are a sophisticated mechanism for increasing exploitation. They transfer the cost of supervision to the worker, encourage self-intensification, and obscure the division of the working day into paid and unpaid labour. Piece-wages represent the culmination of the wage form's tendency to hide the reality of capitalist exploitation behind the appearance of a fair day's pay for a fair day's work.
