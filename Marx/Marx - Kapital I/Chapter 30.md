Here is a summary of Chapter 30 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 30: Impact of the Agricultural Revolution on Industry. The Creation of a Home Market for Industrial Capital.

In this chapter, Marx explains how the transformation of agriculture—the expropriation of the peasantry, the rise of capitalist farming, and the increase in productivity—created the **domestic market** for industrial capital. The agricultural revolution did not just free up labour; it also turned that labour into buyers of manufactured goods.

#### The Twofold Creation of the Home Market

Marx identifies two ways the agricultural revolution created a home market for industry:

1.  **The expropriated peasants became consumers.** Before their dispossession, peasants produced most of what they needed themselves (food, clothing, tools, furniture). They were largely self-sufficient. After being driven off the land, they became wage-labourers who had to buy everything they consumed. Their wages were spent on manufactured goods—textiles, tools, household items, etc. The peasant who once spun his own yarn now bought cloth from the factory.

2.  **The capitalist farmers and landlords became buyers of means of production.** Capitalist farming required investment: improved ploughs, threshing machines, fertilizers, better buildings, etc. These were produced by industry. The landlords, enriched by higher rents, also demanded luxury goods, which stimulated the production of finer manufactured articles. Thus, industry gained a market not only in necessities for workers but also in means of production and luxuries for the rural rich.

#### The Destruction of Domestic Industry

The agricultural revolution also destroyed the rural **domestic industries** that had coexisted with peasant agriculture. When peasants lost their land, they lost the raw materials (e.g., flax for spinning, wool for weaving) and the time to produce their own goods. The capitalist farmer, focused on profit, had no interest in maintaining household production. The spinning wheel and the handloom were abandoned in favour of factory-made goods.

This destruction was not incidental; it was a deliberate effect of competition. Factory-made textiles were cheaper and more uniform than homespun cloth. The peasant who tried to keep his old ways found that he could no longer compete. He was forced to sell his labour and buy his necessities with money.

#### The Creation of a Class of Wage-Labourers

The home market for industrial capital depended on the existence of a **propertyless class that had to buy its means of subsistence**. This class was created by the expropriation of the peasantry. Without a large population of wage-labourers, there would be no demand for the mass of cheap commodities produced by factories. The factory system and the propertyless worker were born together.

Marx emphasizes that this market was not created by simple population growth or by rising incomes, but by a **violent restructuring of social relations**. The same process that robbed the peasant of his land forced him into the factory and turned him into a consumer of factory products.

#### The Role of Colonial Markets

While the home market was crucial, Marx also notes that colonial markets played a role in the early development of industrial capital. The colonies provided raw materials (cotton, sugar, tobacco) and served as outlets for manufactured goods. But the home market, created by the agricultural revolution, was the foundation. Without a mass of domestic workers who had been separated from their means of production and were forced to buy everything, the factory system could not have sustained itself.

#### Conclusion

The agricultural revolution and the industrial revolution were not separate events but two sides of the same process. The expropriation of the peasantry created the **domestic market** for industrial capital by turning self-sufficient producers into dependent consumers. The capitalist farmer and landlord provided demand for means of production and luxuries. And the destruction of domestic industry cleared the field for the factory. Thus, primitive accumulation in agriculture directly enabled primitive accumulation in industry.

This chapter sets the stage for Chapter 31, where Marx will examine the **genesis of the industrial capitalist**—the figure who emerged to take advantage of this newly created home market and labour force.
