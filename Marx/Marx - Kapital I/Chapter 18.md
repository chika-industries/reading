Here is a summary of Chapter 19 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 19: The Transformation of the Value (and Respectively the Price) of Labour-Power into Wages

This chapter begins Part Six, which deals with wages. Here Marx makes a crucial analytical move: he shows how the *value* of labour-power, which he has already established, appears in the everyday world of capitalism as *wages*. This appearance is deceptive and systematically conceals the reality of exploitation.

#### The Surface Appearance vs. the Underlying Reality

On the surface of capitalist society, wages appear to be the **price of labour**. It seems that the worker sells his labour to the capitalist, and the wage is what the capitalist pays for that labour. This is how both workers and capitalists instinctively understand the transaction. Moreover, this appearance seems to be confirmed by everyday experience: the worker works a certain number of hours, and at the end of the day (or week) he receives a sum of money that appears proportional to the duration of his labour.

However, Marx has already demonstrated that what the worker actually sells is not his labour but his **labour-power**. Labour itself is the *use* of that power; it is the activity that creates value. Labour cannot have a value because it is the substance and measure of value. To say that labour has a value is like saying that gravity has weight—it is a category mistake.

#### The Illusion of Wages

Marx argues that the wage form *necessarily* conceals the division of the working day into necessary and surplus labour. When the worker receives wages for a day's work, it appears as if the whole day's labour is paid for. The wage seems to represent the price of the entire labour performed.

In reality, the wage is the price of labour-power, which is only a part of the value the worker produces. The rest is surplus-value appropriated by the capitalist without any apparent equivalent. But because the wage is paid *after* the labour is performed, and because it appears as a payment for the work done, the exploitation is hidden. The unpaid portion of labour seems to be paid for as well.

#### Why This Illusion Is Inevitable

Marx gives several reasons why this illusion is not merely a mistake but a necessary appearance of the capitalist mode of production:

1.  **The Exchange Relation Itself:** In the market, the worker and capitalist meet as free owners of commodities: the worker owns his labour-power, the capitalist owns money. The exchange appears as a simple exchange of equivalents, hiding the unequal relation that unfolds in production.
2.  **The Form of Payment:** Wages are usually paid after the labour is performed, which makes it seem like payment for the labour itself.
3.  **The Variation of Wages with Hours Worked:** If the worker works longer, he receives more wages. This reinforces the idea that he is being paid for each hour of labour, rather than for a day's capacity to labour whose value is fixed.
4.  **The Fluctuation of Wages with the Market:** Wages rise and fall with supply and demand, further obscuring the underlying value of labour-power and making it appear as if labour itself is a commodity with a fluctuating price.

#### The Consequences of This Illusion

The transformation of the value of labour-power into wages has profound ideological and political consequences:

- **It conceals exploitation.** If the worker appears to be paid for the whole of his labour, then surplus-value disappears from view. Profit seems to arise not from unpaid labour but from something else—the "productivity" of capital, risk, abstinence, etc.
- **It makes class struggle appear as a struggle over wages.** Workers and capitalists fight over the level of wages, but this fight obscures the deeper struggle over the division of the working day between necessary and surplus labour.
- **It naturalizes the capitalist relation.** The wage form makes it seem natural that the worker works for a wage, just as it seems natural that other commodities are bought and sold. The historical and social specificity of wage-labour is effaced.

Marx concludes by noting that classical political economy (Smith, Ricardo) never succeeded in breaking through this illusion. They analyzed the value of labour-power (calling it the "value of labour") but then inconsistently treated wages as the price of labour. This confusion was systematized by later "vulgar" economists, who took the appearance for reality and elevated it into a dogma.

In summary, Chapter 19 shows that the wage form is not an innocent representation but a necessary mystification. It turns the value of labour-power into the price of labour, thereby hiding the origin of surplus-value and making capitalist exploitation invisible at the very point where it is most directly experienced by the worker.
