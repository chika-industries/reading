Here is a summary of Chapter 22 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 22: National Differences in Wages

In this chapter, Marx examines why wage levels differ from one country to another. He argues that a direct comparison of money wages across countries is misleading because wages depend on the value of labour-power, which itself varies with productivity, intensity of labour, and historical and social conditions.

#### The Problem of Comparing Wages Across Countries

At first glance, it might seem that higher wages in one country indicate a better standard of living for workers. But Marx shows that this is not necessarily true. To compare wages internationally, we must consider:

- The **value of money** (prices of commodities) in each country.
- The **length and intensity** of the working day.
- The **productivity of labour**.
- The **historical and moral elements** that determine the value of labour-power (what counts as a "necessary" standard of living).

A higher nominal wage may be accompanied by higher prices, a longer working day, or greater intensity, leaving the worker no better off—or even worse off—than a worker in a lower-wage country.

#### The Role of Productivity and Intensity

Marx argues that the value of labour-power is determined by the labour-time required to produce the worker's means of subsistence. If labour productivity is higher in one country, the value of those means of subsistence will be lower, *ceteris paribus*. This tends to reduce the value of labour-power and thus the wage.

However, if labour is more *intense* in one country (workers expend more energy per hour), then more value is created per hour. This can raise the value of labour-power if the intensity affects the production of wage-goods. But intensity also increases the rate of exploitation.

In practice, more developed capitalist countries tend to have both higher productivity and higher intensity of labour. The net effect on wages is complex and cannot be read off from the money wage alone.

#### The "Historical and Moral Element"

Marx repeats his point that the value of labour-power includes a "historical and moral element." What counts as a necessary standard of living (food, housing, clothing, education, leisure) varies from country to country and changes over time. In richer, more developed capitalist countries, the working class has typically won a higher standard of living as part of the value of labour-power. This means that even if the *rate* of exploitation is similar, the *mass* of wages (and the real standard of living) may be higher.

#### The Tendency Toward Equalization (and Its Limits)

Under free trade and capitalist competition, there is a tendency toward the equalization of profit rates across countries, but not toward the equalization of wages. Capital flows to where profits are highest, which can raise wages in the short term. But labour mobility is limited by language, culture, laws, and migration barriers. As a result, large and persistent national differences in wages can exist.

Moreover, Marx notes that the most advanced capitalist country (England in his time) often has the highest *nominal* wages, but also the highest intensity and longest effective working day (within legal limits). The English worker may be paid more in money but exploited just as heavily as a worker in a less developed country.

#### The Fallacy of Comparing Wage Rates in Isolation

Marx criticizes bourgeois economists who point to higher wages in rich countries as proof that capitalism benefits workers everywhere, without considering the countervailing factors of intensity, prices, and the value of labour-power. He also criticizes those who argue that low wages in poor countries give them an unfair competitive advantage; in fact, low wages often go hand in hand with low productivity, so unit labour costs may be similar.

#### Conclusion

National differences in wages arise from:
- Differences in the value of labour-power (due to productivity and historical standards).
- Differences in the intensity of labour.
- Differences in the length of the working day.
- Differences in the prices of commodities.

A higher money wage does not necessarily mean a higher real wage or a lower rate of exploitation. To understand the true position of workers in different countries, one must analyze the underlying value relations, not just the surface appearances of wage rates.

This chapter serves as a bridge between the analysis of wages in general and the next part of *Capital*, which deals with the accumulation of capital and its effects on the working class.
