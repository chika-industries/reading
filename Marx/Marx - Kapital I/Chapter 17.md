Here is a summary of Chapter 17 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 17: Changes of Magnitude in the Price of Labour-Power and in Surplus-Value

In this chapter, Marx examines how the value (and price) of labour-power and the mass of surplus-value are affected by changes in three key variables: the **length of the working day**, the **intensity of labour**, and the **productivity of labour**. He assumes that labour-power is bought and sold at its value (i.e., wages are not systematically driven below subsistence), and he analyzes the various combinations and their effects.

#### The Three Factors

Marx identifies three fundamental factors that determine the relative magnitudes of the value of labour-power and surplus-value:

1.  **The Length of the Working Day:** The total duration of labour (e.g., 10 hours, 12 hours).
2.  **The Intensity of Labour:** The degree of exertion, the amount of labour expended in a given time. More intense labour creates more value in the same period.
3.  **The Productivity of Labour:** The output produced per unit of time, determined by the level of development of the forces of production. Greater productivity reduces the value of individual commodities.

Changes in any of these factors will alter the division of the working day into necessary labour (which reproduces wages) and surplus labour (which produces surplus-value). Marx analyzes four possible cases.

#### I. Length of Working Day and Intensity Constant; Productivity Variable

This is the most important case, as it reflects the normal operation of capitalist development.

- If productivity rises, the value of commodities falls. Since the value of labour-power is determined by the value of the means of subsistence, the **value of labour-power falls**. The necessary labour-time shrinks, and surplus labour-time expands proportionally. Both the *rate* and the *mass* of surplus-value increase.
- If productivity falls (e.g., due to crop failures), the opposite occurs: the value of labour-power rises, necessary labour-time expands, and surplus-value shrinks.

Crucially, Marx notes that a rise in productivity, while increasing surplus-value, does not necessarily imply a fall in the *real wage* (the quantity of means of subsistence the worker receives). In fact, real wages can rise *at the same time* as the value of labour-power falls, provided productivity has increased enough. For example:

> "The value of labour-power does not fall, and consequently surplus-value does not rise, in the same proportion as productivity rises... If the productivity of labour doubles, the value of a given quantity of means of subsistence falls by half. The value of labour-power, therefore, also falls by half... But the quantity of means of subsistence the worker receives may remain the same, or even increase, while its value falls."

This is a crucial point against simplistic notions of "immiseration." Marx is not arguing that real wages necessarily fall, but that the *value* of labour-power falls relative to the value it creates, increasing the rate of exploitation even as living standards improve.

#### II. Length of Working Day and Productivity Constant; Intensity Variable

If the intensity of labour increases, more value is created in the same time.

- If intensity rises uniformly across all industries that produce wage-goods, then the value of labour-power will rise (because more value is required to reproduce the worker, even if his physical consumption is the same). However, surplus-value will rise *even more*, because the total value created increases faster than the value of labour-power.
- The relative proportions shift in favour of surplus-value, even as the absolute value of labour-power (and thus the potential for real wages) may also increase.

This case became increasingly important after the Factory Acts limited the working day. Capital responded by intensifying labour, achieving the same effect as lengthening the day without technically violating the law.

#### III. Productivity and Intensity Constant; Length of Working Day Variable

This is the case of absolute surplus-value, analyzed in Part Three.

- If the working day is lengthened, necessary labour-time remains the same, but surplus labour-time expands. Both the rate and mass of surplus-value increase.
- If the working day is shortened (e.g., by factory legislation), surplus-value falls unless productivity or intensity increases to compensate.

#### IV. Simultaneous Variations in Duration, Productivity and Intensity

In reality, all three factors vary simultaneously. Marx outlines the general principles:

- Any combination is possible, and the effects can be calculated by treating each variation sequentially.
- Two factors may vary in opposite directions, partially or wholly cancelling each other out.
- The most common pattern in capitalist development is: **lengthening (or intensifying) the working day combined with increasing productivity**. This maximizes the extraction of surplus-value.

#### The Law of the Determination of the Value of Labour-Power

Marx concludes by restating the fundamental law:

> **The value of labour-power is determined by the value of the means of subsistence required for the maintenance and reproduction of the labourer.**

This value is not fixed; it varies with productivity, intensity, and the historical and moral conditions of each society. However, within a given society at a given time, it has a definite magnitude. The struggle between capital and labour over the division of the working day and the level of wages is a struggle over how this given magnitude is determined in practice.

In summary, Chapter 17 provides a rigorous analytical framework for understanding the complex, dynamic relationship between wages and profits. It demonstrates that exploitation is not a simple matter of "low wages" but a structural relation that persists and intensifies even as living standards rise, through the mechanisms of productivity and intensity. The worker's share of the value he creates tends to fall, even as his absolute consumption may rise.
