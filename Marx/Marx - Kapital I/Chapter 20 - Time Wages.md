Here is a summary of Chapter 20 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 20: Time-Wages

In this chapter, Marx begins his concrete analysis of the actual forms wages take in capitalist society. The most basic form is **time-wages**, where the worker is paid for a given period of time—an hour, a day, a week, etc. Marx shows how this apparently simple form conceals complex relationships and contains within itself the potential for the extraction of surplus-value in ways that are not immediately obvious.

#### The Basic Form: The Price of Labour-Power for a Given Time

The daily (or weekly) value of labour-power is determined by the labour-time necessary to produce the worker's means of subsistence. If that value is, say, 3 shillings for a 12-hour working day, then the price of an hour of labour-time is 3 shillings / 12 = 3 pence. This is the **nominal price** of an hour of labour.

However, Marx immediately points out that this simple calculation is deceptive. The worker does not sell his labour by the hour; he sells his labour-power for a day. The capitalist has the right to use that labour-power for the agreed period. If the working day is extended beyond the normal length (e.g., from 12 to 15 hours), the worker may receive overtime pay, but the rate per hour may fall, and the extraction of surplus-value increases.

#### The Relationship between Time-Wage, Working Day, and Price of Labour-Power

Marx analyzes three key variables:

1.  **The total working day (length).**
2.  **The amount of labour performed in that day (intensity).**
3.  **The money wage (the daily or weekly sum).**

From these, the **price of labour** (wage per hour) can be calculated. However, Marx shows that the same daily wage can correspond to very different rates of exploitation depending on the length and intensity of the working day.

For example:
- If the daily wage is 3 shillings and the working day is 12 hours, the hourly wage is 3 pence.
- If the working day is extended to 15 hours for the same daily wage, the hourly wage falls to 2.4 pence. The worker is now working longer for the same daily pay, so the rate of surplus-value has increased (more unpaid labour).

Thus, a **fall in the price of labour** (hourly wage) can occur without any reduction in the daily wage, simply by lengthening the working day. Conversely, a rise in the daily wage may be illusory if the working day is extended proportionally.

#### The Struggle over Time-Wages

The history of capitalist production is, in part, a history of the struggle over the relationship between the daily wage and the length of the working day. Capital has a constant tendency to:

- **Prolong the working day** while keeping the daily wage the same, thereby reducing the hourly wage and increasing surplus-value.
- **Intensify labour** (making the worker expend more energy per hour) without increasing the daily wage, which has the same effect as prolonging the day.
- **Use overtime and short-time** to manipulate the wage. Overtime may be paid at a higher rate, but this often serves to justify an even lower base rate. Short-time (reducing hours) can drive the worker to accept worse conditions to make up lost earnings.

Workers, through trade unions and factory legislation, fight to establish a **normal working day** and a **normal price of labour**—i.e., to ensure that the daily wage corresponds to the value of labour-power for a standard day's work.

#### The Deceptive Appearance of Time-Wages

The time-wage form, like the wage form in general, conceals exploitation. It makes it appear that the worker is paid for each hour of labour, when in fact he is paid for the use of his labour-power for a period of time. The unpaid portion of the working day (surplus labour) is hidden because the wage seems to cover the whole day.

Moreover, the time-wage form allows capital to reduce the **price of labour** (hourly rate) without reducing the **nominal daily wage**. This is a key mechanism for increasing the rate of surplus-value while maintaining the appearance of a constant or even rising daily wage.

#### The Limits of Time-Wages

Time-wages, however, are an incomplete form. They still presuppose that the worker is employed for a definite period and that the quality and quantity of labour are somehow measured. But in many industries, especially those where the product is individual or where supervision is difficult, capital prefers **piece-wages**, which Marx will analyze in the next chapter. Piece-wages appear to pay the worker for each unit of output, thus seeming even more directly to be the "price of labour." But Marx will show that piece-wages are merely a modified form of time-wages, with their own specific mechanisms of exploitation.

In summary, Chapter 20 demonstrates that time-wages, the most basic form of wages, are not a simple or neutral payment for hours worked. They are a form of the value of labour-power that systematically conceals the division of the working day into necessary and surplus labour. Capital manipulates the length and intensity of the working day to reduce the price of labour while maintaining or even increasing the nominal daily wage. The struggle over time-wages is therefore a struggle over the very limits of exploitation.
