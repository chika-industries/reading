Here is a summary of Chapter 25 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 25: The General Law of Capitalist Accumulation

This is one of the most famous and politically charged chapters in *Capital*. Marx analyzes how the accumulation of capital affects the condition of the working class. He argues that accumulation necessarily produces a **relative surplus population** (an "industrial reserve army") that keeps wages in check and guarantees capital a ready supply of exploitable labour. The chapter is divided into five sections.

#### 1. A Growing Demand for Labour-Power Accompanies Accumulation if the Composition of Capital Remains the Same

Marx begins with the simplest case: suppose the **composition of capital** (the ratio of constant to variable capital) remains unchanged. That is, each new increment of capital employs the same proportion of workers to machinery and raw materials as before. In this case, accumulation directly increases the demand for labour-power.

If the supply of workers is sufficient, wages may rise temporarily. But Marx argues that rising wages do not fundamentally threaten capital, because:
- Higher wages may simply mean that workers are getting a slightly larger share of the value they produce, but surplus-value may still be high.
- If wages rise too much, accumulation slows down, reducing the demand for labour and bringing wages back down.
- Therefore, even in this "best-case" scenario for workers, the effect is only temporary. The mechanism of accumulation itself regulates the labour market.

Crucially, Marx dismisses the Malthusian theory that wages are determined by population growth. It is not the absolute number of workers that matters, but the ratio of demand to supply created by accumulation.

#### 2. A Relative Diminution of the Variable Part of Capital Occurs in the Course of Accumulation

In reality, the composition of capital does not remain constant. As capitalism develops, the organic composition of capital (the ratio of constant to variable capital) **rises**. More machinery, raw materials, and fixed capital are used per worker. This is driven by:
- Competition, which forces capitalists to adopt labour-saving technologies.
- The drive for relative surplus-value, which requires increasing productivity.

As a result, the **variable capital** (the part spent on wages) grows more slowly than the total capital, and may even decline in relative (and sometimes absolute) terms. Even when total capital grows rapidly, the demand for labour may grow only slowly, stagnate, or even fall.

#### 3. The Progressive Production of a Relative Surplus Population (Industrial Reserve Army)

This is the core of the chapter. Because the demand for labour does not keep pace with accumulation, capitalism constantly produces a **relative surplus population**—workers who are superfluous to the needs of capital at the current level of accumulation. This "industrial reserve army" has several functions:

- **It provides a ready supply of labour** for periods of rapid expansion (booms).
- **It exerts downward pressure on wages** even when employment is high, because workers know they can be replaced by the unemployed.
- **It disciplines the employed workers**, making them more docile and less likely to resist exploitation.
- **It is a condition of capitalist accumulation itself.** Without a reserve army, wages would rise, profits would fall, and accumulation would grind to a halt.

The reserve army exists in three forms:
1.  **Floating:** Workers who are sometimes employed, sometimes unemployed (e.g., in cyclical downturns).
2.  **Latent:** Workers from agriculture or other sectors who are not yet fully integrated into industrial capitalism but are available to be drawn in when needed.
3.  **Stagnant:** Workers who are only irregularly employed (e.g., casual labourers, outworkers) and live in chronic poverty.

#### 4. Different Forms of the Relative Surplus Population. The General Law of Capitalist Accumulation

Marx now states his **general law**:

> **The greater the social wealth, the greater the relative surplus population, and the greater the official pauperism. The working population produces both the accumulation of capital and the means by which it is itself made relatively superfluous; and it does this to an extent that is always increasing.**

In other words: the more successful capitalism is at accumulating wealth, the larger the reserve army of labour becomes. This is not an accident; it is a necessary product of the capitalist mode of production. The relative surplus population is the lever of capitalist accumulation.

The chapter also introduces the concept of **pauperism** (the lowest layer of the reserve army), which includes:
- Those able to work but unable to find employment.
- Orphans and pauper children.
- The "demoralized and ragged" (the lumpenproletariat).
- The disabled, the elderly, and others unable to work.

Pauperism is the "hospital of the reserve army" and a condition of the reproduction of capital.

#### 5. Illustrations of the General Law (England 1846–1866)

Marx provides extensive empirical evidence from English official statistics to demonstrate his law in action. He shows that:

- **Despite enormous increases in wealth and capital,** the condition of large sections of the working class worsened or stagnated.
- **Wages rose in some periods** (especially the 1850s and early 1860s), but so did the number of paupers and the relative surplus population.
- **Agricultural labourers** were among the worst off, with low wages, long hours, and terrible living conditions.
- **Ireland** provided a dramatic example: depopulation (due to famine and emigration) did not improve the lot of those who remained; instead, it concentrated land ownership and intensified exploitation.

Marx concludes with a devastating summary:

> **Accumulation of wealth at one pole is, therefore, at the same time accumulation of misery, agony of toil, slavery, ignorance, brutality, mental degradation, at the opposite pole, i.e., on the side of the class that produces its own product as capital.**

This is the **general law of capitalist accumulation**: capital grows, the reserve army grows, and the working class, even when it experiences temporary improvements, remains in a condition of structural subordination and relative deprivation.

#### Significance

This chapter directly challenges the idea that capitalism can ever deliver general prosperity to the working class. Marx does not deny that real wages can rise, but he insists that such rises are limited, temporary, and accompanied by a growing reserve army and increasing inequality. The "immiseration" he describes is not necessarily absolute (though it can be) but **relative**—the worker's position worsens compared to the capitalist's, and the threat of unemployment and poverty is ever-present.

The general law is not a prediction of universal starvation but an analysis of the structural dynamics of capitalism. It explains why, even in periods of growth, poverty and inequality persist and are reproduced.
