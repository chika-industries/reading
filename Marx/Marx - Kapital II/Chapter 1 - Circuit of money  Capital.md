# Summary of Chapter 1: The Circuit of Money Capital

## Overview

This chapter analyzes the first of three forms of capital's circuit: money capital (M → C... P... C' → M'). The circuit consists of three stages through which capital passes in its circular movement.

## The Three Stages

### First Stage: M → C (Money to Commodities)

The capitalist appears as a buyer in the commodity and labor markets, transforming money into commodities. These commodities are of two types:
- **Means of production (MP)** - purchased from other capitalists
- **Labor-power (L)** - purchased from workers

Thus, M → C breaks down into M → L and M → MP. The quantity of means of production purchased must be sufficient to absorb the labor-power bought. This act transforms money capital into productive capital (P).

The purchase of labor-power is crucial because the worker sells not labor itself but *labor-power*, whose value is less than the value it creates. This difference is the source of surplus-value.

### Second Stage: Function of Productive Capital (P)

After purchase, capital enters production. The capitalist consumes the purchased commodities productively. Labor-power is applied to means of production, resulting in a commodity (C') of greater value than the elements that entered production. Capital-value now exists as productive capital, and the result is an expanded commodity-capital (C' = C + c, where c is surplus-value).

### Third Stage: C' → M' (Commodities to Money)

The capitalist returns to market as a seller, converting the expanded commodity-value into money. This act realizes both the original capital-value and the surplus-value. The formula becomes C' → M' (where M' = M + m, the original money plus surplus-value).

## The Circuit as a Whole

The complete circuit is **M → C... P... C' → M'**. Key characteristics:

1. **Money-capital** is the starting point and return point - capital returns as augmented money-value
2. **Production** (P) interrupts circulation and serves as the means of self-expansion
3. The circuit expresses the driving motive of capitalism: money begetting more money

## Important Distinctions

- **Constant vs. variable capital**: Means of production (constant) transfer value; labor-power (variable) creates new value and surplus-value
- **Industrial capital** is the only form where capital both appropriates *and creates* surplus-value
- The circuit is a unity of production and circulation - capital exists simultaneously in different forms (money, productive, commodity-capital) at different points

## Prerequisites

For this circuit to operate on a social scale, there must exist:
- A class of wage-laborers separated from means of production
- Developed commodity production and circulation
- Money functioning as capital, not merely as medium of exchange
