# Summary of Chapter 12: Changes in Depreciation Policy

This chapter examines how companies can manipulate reported profits by altering depreciation methods, useful economic lives, or residual values—without even needing to change formal accounting policy. Because depreciation is a non‑cash charge, extending asset lives or switching methods can immediately boost profits, creating a treadmill effect where further growth becomes harder to sustain.

## Why Depreciation Matters

Under SSAP 12, depreciation is “the measure of the wearing out, consumption or other reduction in the useful life of a fixed asset.” Management has considerable discretion in choosing:

- Depreciation method (straight‑line, reducing balance, unit of production, etc.)
- Useful economic life
- Residual value

A change in method is allowed only if it “better represents the company’s results and financial position,” but changes in useful life do not even require disclosure as a change in accounting policy—they can be buried in the accounting policy note or detected only by comparing notes year‑on‑year.

## BAA – Lengthening Asset Lives

BAA made two significant changes in useful lives over a short period:

| Asset Category | 1988 Life | 1989 Life | 1990 Life |
|----------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Terminals | 16 years | 30 years | 50 years |
| Runways | 23.5 years | 40 years | 100 years |

**The effect:** At the same time, BAA was undertaking massive capital expenditure: Stansted terminal (£395m), Heathrow Terminal 3 (£110m), runway resurfacing, and other projects. By extending depreciation lives, the annual charge on these new assets was dramatically reduced.

The 1990 Report estimated that if the changes had not been introduced, profits would have been **£8.6m lower**—just 3.4% of pre‑tax profit that year, but the impact grows with each new asset commissioned.

**The runway example:** Extending runway life from 23.5 to 100 years raises the question: will BAA still derive economic benefit from aircraft using its runways in 2091? At the time, powered flight had existed for less than 100 years.

## Cable & Wireless – Method and Life Changes

### Unit of Production Method

Cable & Wireless changed depreciation for major network developments to a “unit of usage” basis. Instead of a fixed annual charge, depreciation is calculated as:

> Network usage ÷ Estimated total usage over the network’s life

This smooths the charge, preventing high early‑year depreciation before usage builds up. The risk: if total usage is overestimated, early years are under‑depreciated, leaving an overvalued asset on the balance sheet that may eventually need a large write‑down.

### Lengthening Digital Exchange Lives

Digital exchange lives were extended from **10 to 15 years**. This change was not highlighted in the Cable & Wireless accounts; it appeared only in the subsidiary Mercury’s notes.

**The effect:** A 10‑year life gives a 10% depreciation charge per year; a 15‑year life gives 6.7%—a **one‑third reduction** in the annual charge on all digital exchanges.

## How to Spot Changes

### 1. Compare Accounting Policy Notes Year‑on‑Year

Changes in depreciation lives do not count as a change in accounting policy, so they are not highlighted. Investors must compare the current year’s policy note with the previous year’s to detect shifts.

### 2. Calculate Depreciation as a Percentage of Gross Assets

A simple screen: depreciation charge ÷ average gross fixed assets. A declining percentage over time suggests life extensions or method changes.

| Cable & Wireless | 1985 | 1986 | 1987 | 1988 | 1989 | 1990 |
|------------------|------|------|------|------|------|------|
| Depreciation (£m) | 92 | 88 | 89 | 100 | 119 | 196 |
| Average gross assets (£m) | 1,036 | 1,182 | 1,307 | 1,489 | 1,953 | 2,806 |
| Depreciation/Assets (%) | 8.9 | 7.4 | 6.8 | 6.7 | 6.1 | 7.0 |

Between 1985 and 1990, the percentage fell from 8.9% to 7.0%, despite heavy investment in digital exchanges that would normally increase the proportion of assets being depreciated.

### 3. Compare with Industry Peers

| Company | 1986 | 1987 | 1988 | 1989 | 1990 |
|---------|------|------|------|------|------|
| British Telecom | 7.5 | 8.8 | 9.3 | 8.9 | 9.4 |
| Cable & Wireless | 7.4 | 6.8 | 6.7 | 6.1 | 7.0 |
| Racal Telecom | 10.5 | 10.6 | 11.1 | 10.0 | 9.9 |
| US Regional Bells (avg) | ~7.2 | ~7.9 | ~7.9 | ~7.8 | ~7.6 |

Cable & Wireless’s 7.0% in 1990 was notably below the UK industry (BT 9.4%, Racal 9.9%) and even below the US regional Bells, which were regulated and had older networks.

## The Treadmill Effect

Changes in depreciation policy create a **one‑off benefit** that boosts profits in the year of change and for several years thereafter. To maintain reported profit growth, a company must either:

- Keep extending lives (which becomes harder as assets approach the end of their extended lives)
- Increase organic earnings growth to replace the benefit

If investors extrapolate past profit growth without adjusting for depreciation changes, they may overvalue the shares. Cable & Wireless’s reported five‑year profit growth (1985–90) was **16.7% p.a.** ; after adjusting for depreciation changes, the underlying growth was **14.3% p.a.** —a significant difference for valuation.

## Key Insight

Changes in depreciation policy are among the most subtle and effective ways to inflate reported profits. Because they are not always disclosed as “changes in accounting policy,” they can escape notice. Investors should:

1. Compare depreciation policies year‑on‑year, looking for extended lives or new methods.
2. Calculate depreciation as a percentage of gross assets over time.
3. Benchmark against industry competitors.

A company that repeatedly extends asset lives is effectively borrowing profit from the future—and eventually the bill will come due, either through lower profits or a large write‑down when the assets are finally disposed of.
