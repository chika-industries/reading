# Summary of Chapter 3: The 1980s – Decade of the Deal

This chapter sets the scene for understanding creative accounting by examining the merger and acquisition boom of the 1980s, which created both the opportunities and the pressures that led to widespread accounting manipulation.

## The Environment: Liberalisation and Laissez-Faire

The 1980s saw a perfect storm of conditions for takeover activity:
- Economic growth
- A raging bull market
- Easy credit supply

In the UK, additional factors fuelled the boom:
- **End of exchange controls** (enabling cross-border investment)
- **"Big Bang"** deregulation of the London Stock Exchange, which created integrated securities houses with corporate finance departments eager to justify their existence by hatching deals

## The Statistics

The scale of M&A activity exploded compared to the previous decade:

## Fashionable Themes (Dubious Then, Disastrous in Hindsight)

**"One-stop" Financial Shopping:** The concept of financial supermarkets where consumers could buy everything from mortgages to insurance. This drove British & Commonwealth's expansion and the rush by insurers and building societies to buy estate agents. The concept ignored that clearing banks had already failed at this model. The Prudential later lost £350m selling its estate agency chain.

**1992 and the Single European Market:** Used to hype takeovers, though UK companies were more obsessed with US acquisitions than European opportunities.

**Unbundling:** Sir James Goldsmith's term for asset stripping. Lord Hanson perfected it with Imperial Group (1986). The catch: growth through unbundling becomes addictive—each successful deal requires a bigger next deal to maintain the growth pattern, leading to creative accounting practices.

**MBOs and LBOs:** Major UK deals included Magnet, Lowndes Queensway, and Woolworths. Timing was crucial—the first two crashed on rising interest rates and falling demand. The Bricom buyout from British & Commonwealth (£359m, June 1988) proved pivotal: it removed most tangible assets from B&C's balance sheet, leaving only dubious goodwill, which offered no support when Atlantic Computers collapsed.

## The Hangover

**Who paid the bill?**
- **Successful companies** that overpaid in the boom (leading to 1991 rights issues)
- **Shareholders** of failed companies (total losses at B&C, Dominion International, UTC, LIT, Burns Anderson)
- **Property developers** (Polly Peck, Brent Walker, Coloroll)
- **Maxwell** (highly geared US acquisitions pushed the company into administration)

**Human cost:** Tarnished reputations and broken careers for the "whizz-kids" (Gunn, Goldie, Ashcroft, Nadir, Maxwell). In the US, Ivan Boesky and Michael Milken faced prison. In the UK, Ernest Saunders (former Guinness chief) saw his career destroyed after the Distillers bid.

## Key Insight

The takeover boom created not only the deals themselves but also a series of accounting techniques associated with acquisitions and disposals that could be used to boost profits artificially. These techniques—which form the subject of Part II—became essential for companies needing to maintain the appearance of growth as deals got bigger and the pressures mounted.
