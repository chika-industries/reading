# Summary of Chapter 17: Survival Techniques in the Accounting Jungle

This concluding chapter provides practical advice for investors navigating the complexities of creative accounting. Smith distills the lessons from earlier chapters into a set of actionable techniques to help readers separate genuine financial strength from accounting illusion—and, ultimately, to protect their investments.

## 1. Read the Accounts Backwards

Annual reports are carefully crafted to impress. Starting at the front yields glossy photographs, the Chairman’s Statement (invariably optimistic), and a review of operations before reaching the accounts themselves.

**Better approach:** Begin at the back and work forward.
- The **last notes** often contain the most critical information—contingent liabilities, pension fund details, and resolutions for the AGM (which may reveal directors’ powers to allot shares).
- Work through notes on **pension funding**—calculate the proportion of net asset value represented by prepayment assets, and assess whether pension credits artificially inflate profits or interest cover.
- Scrutinise **interest notes** for capitalisation; recalculate interest cover including capitalised interest.
- By the time you reach the profit and loss account and balance sheet, you will have already performed the calculations needed to see the company’s true health.

## 2. Read the Accounting Policies – and Compare

The accounting policies note is vital. Changes in policy must be considered for their profit impact—even if they fall within GAAP and receive a clean audit opinion.

**Crucial point:** Changes in depreciation lives do not count as “changes in accounting policy” and may not be highlighted. Always compare the current year’s policy note with the previous year’s to detect such shifts.

## 3. Screen the Accounts Using Various Filters

Simple ratio‑based screens can raise questions that lead to deeper investigation.

**Interest rate screen:** Compare net interest income/expense with average net cash/debt (from opening and closing balance sheets). An implied rate far below market rates (or net interest income despite net debt) suggests currency mismatching or other treasury speculation.

**Tax charge screen:** The Inland Revenue has far more information than an investor. A tax rate significantly below the statutory rate (e.g., 33% in the UK at the time) indicates that the Revenue views taxable profits as lower than accounting profits. Low tax charges featured prominently in companies such as Polly Peck, Stakis, and Costain before their difficulties.

**Depreciation screen:** Calculate depreciation as a percentage of gross assets over several years. A declining trend often signals hidden life extensions. MTM’s depreciation fell from 6.2% of gross assets in 1986 to 2.6% in 1990—a warning that accounting policies were becoming aggressive.

## 4. Watch Out for Transfers Between Balance Sheet and Profit and Loss Account

Companies often boost reported profits at the expense of the balance sheet. Look for:
- **Reclassifications** of assets (e.g., development properties moved to investment properties) before write‑downs, so the loss goes to reserves rather than the P&L.
- **Currency mismatching** where interest income goes to profits but exchange losses go to reserves.
- **Acquisition provisions** that absorb future costs, allowing those costs to bypass the P&L.

**A simple check:** Calculate Net Asset Value (NAV) per share over a ten‑year period. If NAV is static or falling while profits surge, creative accounting is likely at work.

The ASB’s proposed **statement of total recognised gains and losses** will help—it captures all movements in reserves, revealing the true change in net worth. In Fisons’ 1990 accounts, earnings per share were 26.3p, but total recognised losses per share were 21p after accounting for goodwill write‑offs and currency losses.

## 5. Cash is King!

Profits can be manufactured; cash cannot. Lack of cash—not lack of profits—causes businesses to fail.

**The fallacy of “profit plus depreciation” as cash flow:** Adding depreciation back to profits ignores changes in working capital, capital expenditure, and provisions. A much better measure is the **cash flow statement** introduced by FRS 1, which separates:
- Operating activities
- Investing activities (capex, acquisitions, disposals)
- Financing activities (share issues, new borrowing, repayments)

**Bass 1991 example:**
- Trading profit + depreciation = £788m
- But after working capital changes, provisions utilised, and capital expenditure, cash flow before financing was only **£35m**—far below the £788m that a naive “profit plus depreciation” calculation would suggest.

**Cash flow per share** can be compared with earnings per share over time to see whether reported earnings are actually being converted into cash.

## 6. If in Doubt, Don’t Invest

Investors are on their own. Auditors will not warn about techniques that comply with GAAP. Stock market analysts are often mesmerised by EPS and may ignore the underlying quality of earnings.

**The only effective sanction:** Choose not to invest. Ask questions directly to the company—at AGMs, in writing to the finance director, or through your stockbroker. If the answers are unsatisfactory, walk away.

The principle applies to investing as it does to betting:

> “Not to bet until the odds be considered fair, reasonable or completely in the favour of the backer is an advantage which must never be surrendered. The bookmaker has to lay odds all the time for each and every race—but the backer can choose if and when to bet.”  
> *– Braddocks Complete Guide to Horse Race Selection and Betting*

## Key Insight

The accounting jungle is navigable—but only with vigilance. By reading accounts backwards, scrutinising policies, using simple screens, tracking transfers between balance sheet and P&L, focusing on cash, and exercising the discipline to avoid doubtful investments, investors can protect themselves from the worst excesses of creative accounting. The ultimate lesson is stark but simple: when in doubt, don’t invest.
