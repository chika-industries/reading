# Summary of Chapter 2: How Could It Happen?

This chapter examines four major corporate collapses from the 1990-91 recession, demonstrating how companies reporting record profits could suddenly become insolvent. Each case illustrates different creative accounting techniques that masked underlying problems.

## Polly Peck International

**The Fall:** On September 3, 1990, Polly Peck reported impressive interim profits of £110.5m (up from £64.4m), with a market capitalization of £1.05bn and shareholders' funds of £932.7m. Just over two weeks later, shares were suspended after plunging from 457p to 108p. By October, administrators were appointed, with liquidation studies suggesting a £384m deficit for shareholders.

**Key Questions:** How could a darling of the stock market with nearly £1bn in reported shareholders' funds become insolvent within weeks? (The technique involved—currency mismatching—is covered in Chapter 15.)

## British & Commonwealth

**The Scale:** At its July 1987 peak, B&C was valued at £1.85bn, making it the UK's 46th largest company. By April 1990, shares were suspended at 53p.

**The Cause:** The primary problem was Atlantic Computers, acquired in September 1988. Atlantic's "Flexlease" contracts created massive contingent liabilities not shown in the accounts—obligations to meet lease payments when customers exercised options to return equipment. B&C attempted to "ring-fence" Atlantic, but the strategy failed, and B&C entered administration in June 1990. (Relevant techniques: acquisition accounting and contingent liabilities—Chapters 3-6 and 9.)

## Coloroll

**The Peak:** Valued at £424m in June 1988, Coloroll's last profit announcement (November 1989) showed profits halved to £10m, with stated gearing of 75% (far below reality). Shares were suspended in June 1990. (Relevant techniques: acquisition accounting and off-balance sheet finance—Chapters 3-6 and 8.)

## Maxwell Communications Corporation

**The Most Dramatic Case:** Maxwell received seven "blobs" in the original Accounting for Growth health check—one of only three companies with such a high score.

**The Methods Revealed After His Death:**

1. **Pledging assets then selling them** without delivering to lenders as security (e.g., Berlitz language school sold after being pledged)

2. **Plundering pension funds**—diverting £350m from Mirror Group Newspapers' pension fund by transferring assets to private Maxwell companies, then pledging them as loan security

3. **Share support operations**—using £130m from private companies to buy MCC shares, effectively funding purchase of own shares (illegal), and selling put options to Goldman Sachs to indirectly support the price

4. **Simply taking cash**—£43m "lent" from MGN to private companies after flotation, supposedly for gilt investment that never occurred

## Conclusion

These collapses were not isolated incidents but resulted from creative accounting and financial engineering. The chapter sets up Parts II and III, which will examine the specific techniques used:

- Polly Peck → Currency mismatching (Chapter 15)
- British & Commonwealth → Acquisition/disposal techniques (Chapters 3-6) and contingent liabilities (Chapter 9)
- Coloroll → Acquisition/disposal techniques (Chapters 3-6)
- Maxwell → Multiple techniques across the spectrum

The key message: all techniques described in the book were compliant with UK Generally Accepted Accounting Practice (GAAP) and received clean audit reports at the time. The question is whether users of accounts received sufficiently clear information to make informed judgements.
