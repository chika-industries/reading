# Summary of Chapter 9: Contingent Liabilities

This chapter explores contingent liabilities—potential obligations that may arise in the future—and how they can be used to conceal serious risks from investors. Because contingent liabilities do not form part of the double‑entry bookkeeping system, they are easily hidden and have been at the heart of some of the largest corporate collapses.

## What Is a Contingent Liability?

Under SSAP 18, a contingency is “a condition which exists at the balance sheet date where the outcome will be confirmed only on the occurrence or non‑occurrence of one or more uncertain future events.” Unlike normal accounting entries, there is no “equal and opposite” asset or liability recorded; they exist entirely in the notes—often in a simple card index, relying on directors’ assurances.

## Common Types

- **Guarantees of subsidiary overdrafts** – holding companies guarantee bank borrowings of subsidiaries.
- **Performance bonds** – builders provide bonds to ensure work meets required standards; if the bond is called, the company is liable.
- **Discounted bills / factored debts with recourse** – if the customer does not pay, the original seller remains liable.

## Dangerous Examples

### Coloroll – Selling with Recourse

When Coloroll sold its cloth and clothing divisions to an MBO called Response Group, part of the consideration was **£7.5m** of redeemable preference shares and **£14.25m** of loan notes. Coloroll then sold these securities “with recourse”—meaning it remained liable if the MBO failed. When Response hit financial trouble in January 1990, Coloroll had to honour the guarantees, turning the contingent liability into a cash outflow of **£22m**. The same pattern appeared with the sale of Homfray Carpets Australia.

### Allied Lyons – Hidden Currency Losses

Allied Lyons’ 1990 accounts showed a seemingly routine contingent liabilities note: guarantees, bills discounted, and VAT group liability. Nothing disclosed the massive foreign exchange transactions undertaken by group treasury that eventually cost the company **£147m** and led to the resignation of the Finance Director. Because contingent liabilities do not require double‑entry booking, there was no mechanism to force disclosure.

### British & Commonwealth – Atlantic Computers

The most devastating example. B&C acquired Atlantic Computers, a computer leasing company whose core product was the “Flexlease” contract. Atlantic acted as an intermediary between banks (who provided lease finance) and users.

**The Flexlease trap:**
- Users had a “Flex” option to return equipment after three years if they leased new, more expensive equipment.
- They also had a “Walk” option to return equipment after five years with no further obligation.
- Atlantic remained liable to the banks for the full original lease payments. It covered those liabilities partly by selling returned equipment and partly by writing new leases.

The system worked like a pyramid: each new lease generated income to cover the old liabilities, but the contingent liabilities (Atlantic’s exposure to banks) grew relentlessly. At no point did Atlantic or B&C show these exposures in the accounts as contingent liabilities. The first investors heard of the problem was after Easter 1990, when B&C announced it was putting Atlantic into administration and writing off **£550m**.

**Why the auditors didn’t catch it:** Contingent liabilities are not part of the double‑entry trial balance. Auditors must rely on management representations. The failure to disclose the contingent liabilities—even though B&C had full access to Atlantic’s books when it acquired the company—meant the true risks were invisible until it was too late.

## The Aftermath

B&C tried to “ring‑fence” Atlantic and rely on limited liability, but the collapse triggered defaults on bond covenants and a crisis of confidence. When the Securities and Investment Board removed B&C’s merchant banking licence, the final liquidity crisis hit, and B&C entered administration on **3 June 1990**.

## Key Insight

Contingent liabilities are particularly dangerous because:
1. They require no double‑entry booking, so they can be hidden in a card file.
2. They are disclosed only in a note, often at the back of the accounts, where they can be easily overlooked.
3. They rely entirely on directors’ honesty—and the directors may not even understand the full exposure.

When a company sells an asset but guarantees the purchaser’s loans, or structures leases that leave it on the hook for huge future payments, the balance sheet will look healthy while the company is slowly being destroyed. Investors must scrutinise the contingent liabilities note as if their investment depends on it—because often it does.
