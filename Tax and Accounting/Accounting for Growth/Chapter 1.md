# Summary of Chapter 1: Introduction

The first chapter introduces the central paradox that motivated the book: how companies can report profits yet still go bankrupt. Terry Smith opens with a recession-era quip—"A time when money is returned to its rightful owners"—before addressing the question he's frequently faced throughout his finance career: "XYZ company went bust last week, but it was making profits. How can that happen?"

## Key Points

**The Brentford Nylons Example (1976):** This early case illustrates that creative accounting is nothing new. The retailer collapsed despite reporting £130,000 in profits—profits that included a £550,000 special credit from a supplier claim and a £180,000 regional development grant.

**The 1990-91 Recession Parallel:** Similar phenomena occurred with major corporate collapses of companies reporting "profits" shortly before failure, including:
- Polly Peck
- British & Commonwealth
- BCCI
- Maxwell Communications

**Origin of the Report:** Smith co-authored the original "Accounting for Growth" report with Richard Hannah at UBS Phillips & Drew in early 1991. The title is a deliberate pun—questioning whether 1980s profit growth resulted from genuine economic performance or "accounting sleight of hand."

**The "Blob Guide":** The report introduced a simple checklist tracking 11 accounting techniques across major UK companies. Remarkably, this unsophisticated measure proved highly predictive:

| Companies with 5+ techniques | 1991 Share Performance |
|------------------------------|------------------------|
| LEP | -90% |
| Maxwell | -100% (suspended) |
| ASDA | -66% |
| British Aerospace | -44% |
| Burton | -43% |
| (Exceptions: Dixons +40%, Next +234%) |

**Core Thesis:** The key distinction investors must grasp is between **profit** (an accountant's opinion/guess based on "true and fair view") and **cash** (a fact). Cash ultimately determines whether a business survives or fails.

**Book Structure:**
- Parts II-III: Detailed examination of creative accounting techniques with real company examples
- Part IV: Updated "Major Companies Accounting Health Check" and "Survival Techniques in the Accounting Jungle"
