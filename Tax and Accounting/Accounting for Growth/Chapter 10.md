# Summary of Chapter 10: Capitalisation of Costs

This chapter examines how companies can convert expenses into assets through capitalisation—a legitimate accounting practice under the Companies Act 1985, but one that can significantly distort reported profits and financial health. The most common form is capitalising interest, particularly in property development, but the technique extends to research and development, product registration costs, and even more unusual items.

## How Capitalisation Works

Capitalisation takes an item that would otherwise be an expense (reducing profit) and instead classifies it as an asset on the balance sheet. The expense is then released gradually (e.g., through depreciation or amortisation) over future periods—or sometimes not at all.

## Capitalising Interest

### Broadwell Land (1989)

The profit and loss account showed:
- Operating profit: **£6.96m**
- Interest payable: **£148,270**
- Net interest receivable: **£96,189** → interest cover appeared comfortable

But Note 5 revealed the truth:

| Item | £ |
|------|---|
| Actual interest payable | 3,892,684 |
| Less: Interest capitalised | (2,215,024) |
| Less: Interest in cost of sales | (1,270,195) |
| Less: Interest capitalised in investment properties | (152,886) |
| Less: Interest capitalised in other fixed assets | (107,309) |
| Interest in P&L | **148,270** |

**True interest cover:**
- Net interest payable (ignoring capitalisation): £3,892,684 – £244,459 = **£3,649,225**
- Operating profit / true interest = £6,959,314 / £3,649,225 = **1.9 times**

A company that appeared to have no net interest cost actually had dangerously thin cover. Broadwell Land went into administrative receivership when property profits collapsed.

A study of 45 companies that went into receivership in 1989–90 found that **60%** had interest cover below three times in their final accounts. Examples of true interest cover (after adding back capitalised interest):

## Capitalising Other Costs

### Research and Development

SSAP 13 permits capitalisation of development expenditure. Contrasting treatments:

**SmithKline Beecham (1989):** All R&D expensed (£390m), only laboratory buildings capitalised.

**Rockwood (1988):** Capitalised patents and development costs of **£957,000**—for a freight forwarding and security company, not a high-tech business. Rockwood later went into receivership.

### Unusual Capitalisations

**Corton Beach (1990):** Capitalised interest on acquisitions until the acquired company was “substantially integrated”—a novel interpretation that deferred interest costs.

**Musterlin (1989):** A publisher capitalised “book properties” (publication rights) as tangible fixed assets worth **£6.8m** out of total tangible assets of £11.6m.

**Sharp & Law (1988):** A shopfitting company capitalised computer software products intended for external sale.

**Cable & Wireless (1990):** Capitalised some employee costs—a rare practice for a major company.

### The MTM Case (1992)

MTM, a speciality chemicals group, announced that its 1991 results would be substantially below expectations and delayed. The reason: auditors wanted to change accounting policies relating to capitalisation of product registration and development costs. The result:

- 1991 pre-tax loss: **£20.5m**
- 1990 results restated to reduce profit from previously reported to **£7.9m**

The share price collapsed from 289p to 97p within a month—a stark reminder that capitalisation policies can mask underlying weakness until auditors force a correction.

## Key Insight

Capitalisation of costs, especially interest, is a legitimate accounting practice but one that requires careful scrutiny. Investors must:

1. **Add back capitalised interest** when calculating interest cover—this is what bankers do.
2. **Compare capitalisation policies** across competitors; inconsistencies often signal aggressive accounting.
3. **Watch for “unusual” capitalisations** (book properties, employee costs, acquisition interest) that stretch the concept beyond normal practice.
4. **Recognise that capitalised costs create future depreciation charges**—the benefit is temporary; the asset remains on the balance sheet.

As the MTM case shows, when auditors eventually force a change, the impact on profits can be devastating. Companies that rely heavily on capitalisation to inflate earnings are often hiding deeper problems.
