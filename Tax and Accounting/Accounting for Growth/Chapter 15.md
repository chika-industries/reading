# Summary of Chapter 15: Currency Mismatching

This chapter explores how companies can generate apparent profits by borrowing in one currency and holding deposits in another, exploiting interest rate differentials—while the corresponding exchange losses are hidden in reserves. The technique can flatter reported earnings but creates hidden balance‑sheet risks that often prove catastrophic.

## Why Mismatch?

The incentive is simple: if a company can borrow in a low‑interest currency (e.g., US dollars) and deposit in a high‑interest currency (e.g., sterling), the interest margin flows through the profit and loss account as net interest income—even if the company has no net cash of its own.

**Example (1991 figures):**
- Sterling LIBOR: 12.8%
- US prime rate: 8.9%
- A company borrows $193m, converts to £100m, and deposits in sterling.
- Six months’ interest: £6.4m received, £4.8m paid → £1.6m net interest income.

But exchange rates move. If sterling weakens (fewer dollars per pound), the dollar loan becomes larger in sterling terms when the transaction is unwound.

## The Accounting Asymmetry

Under SSAP 20:

- **Interest income/expense** from the mismatch goes through the **profit and loss account**.
- **Exchange gains/losses** on monetary assets and liabilities are treated differently:
  - If the foreign currency borrowing is used to finance a foreign currency investment, exchange differences go to **reserves** (balance sheet).
  - Otherwise, they go to the **profit and loss account**.

This creates a dangerous asymmetry: the interest gain boosts reported profits, while the exchange loss—often much larger—can be hidden in reserves, making the company appear more profitable than its true economic position.

## Polly Peck – A Dramatic Example

Polly Peck’s 1989 accounts showed:
- A £10.5m provision for unrealised currency losses in the P&L
- Exchange adjustments taken directly to reserves: **£44.7m** in 1989 and **£170.3m** in 1988

The massive reserve debits masked the true cost of currency mismatching. When the Turkish lira and other currencies moved against the group, the balance sheet absorbed the losses while profits continued to be reported—until the whole structure collapsed.

## The “Double Whammy” – Gearing and Goodwill

Currency mismatching can worsen a company’s financial ratios in two ways:

1. **Gearing rises** – the sterling value of foreign currency debt increases while reserves fall.
2. **Goodwill effect** – if the debt financed an acquisition with capitalised goodwill, the goodwill also rises in sterling terms, further eroding tangible net asset value.

**Reckitt & Colman (1988–89)**
- Goodwill from foreign acquisitions increased due to exchange movements.
- Gearing rose from 11.6% to 15.6% partly because of currency translation effects, even though trading remained strong.

## How to Spot Currency Mismatching

### 1. Compare Net Interest with Average Net Debt

A simple but powerful screen:

- Calculate average net debt = (opening net debt + closing net debt) ÷ 2
- Divide net interest paid (or received) by average net debt to get an implied interest rate.

If the implied rate is far below market rates (or the company shows net interest income despite having net debt), mismatching may be present.

**1991 examples – companies with suspiciously low implied interest rates:**

| Company | Net Interest (£m) | Average Net Debt (£m) | Implied Rate |
|---------|-------------------|------------------------|--------------|
| Grand Met | (198) | 2,748 | (7.2%) |
| Fisons | (10) | 144 | (6.9%) |
| Blue Circle | (23) | 373.5 | (6.2%) |
| Courtaulds | (5) | 221.5 | (2.3%) |
| Storehouse | 0.5 | 20.0 | 2.5% |

A negative implied rate (net interest receivable while having net debt) is a red flag.

**Companies with unusually high returns on net cash:**

| Company | Net Interest Income (£m) | Average Net Cash (£m) | Implied Rate |
|---------|--------------------------|----------------------|--------------|
| Hanson | 178 | 454.5 | 39.2% |
| GEC | 104 | 286.0 | 36.4% |
| TI Group | 8.0 | 6.5 | 123% |

These extraordinary returns suggest currency mismatching or other treasury activities.

### 2. Look for Large Gross Borrowings and Deposits

A balance sheet that shows both substantial short‑term borrowings and substantial cash balances is a tell‑tale sign. Even if net debt is low, the company may be mismatching currencies.

**Hanson (1991)**
- Cash at bank: £7,765m
- Borrowings: £7,415m
- US dollar operations: net borrowings of £2,721m with negative shareholders’ funds
- Sterling operations: net cash of £3,710m
- Net interest income: £188m (14% of pre‑tax profit)

## Why Mismatching Is Dangerous

- **Interest rate differentials exist for a reason** – they reflect expected exchange rate movements. The interest gain is often dwarfed by the exchange loss when currencies revert.
- **Profit is recorded now; loss is deferred** – and may be hidden in reserves until it becomes impossible to ignore.
- **Banks look through the accounting** – they consider the total exposure, not just the reported gearing. When exchange rates move against the company, bankers may demand additional collateral or repayment.

## Key Insight

Currency mismatching is a form of speculation dressed as treasury management. It allows companies to report higher profits than their underlying operations generate, while concealing the corresponding risks in reserves. The simple screen of comparing net interest with average net debt can often reveal whether a company is playing this game.

Investors should be wary of any company that consistently earns a much higher return on its cash than market interest rates, or pays a much lower rate on its debt than the prevailing cost of borrowing. When the currency moves the wrong way, the losses—hidden for years—can suddenly crystallise and destroy shareholder value.
