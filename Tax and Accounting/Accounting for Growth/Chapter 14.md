# Summary of Chapter 14: Pension Fund Accounting

This chapter explores how companies account for pension fund surpluses—a phenomenon that became widespread in the 1980s as investment returns outstripped wage inflation. The introduction of SSAP 24 in 1988 aimed to bring consistency, but significant discretion remains, allowing companies to use pension surpluses to boost profits or flatter balance sheets in ways that investors must scrutinise carefully.

## How a Surplus Arises

A pension fund surplus occurs when the actuarial valuation of assets exceeds the estimated liabilities to members. This typically results from:
- Investment returns higher than anticipated
- Wage increases lower than expected
- Reductions in headcount (which reduce future liability growth)

During the 1980s bull market, UK equities delivered exceptional returns, while wage inflation moderated. By the early 1990s, a Bacon & Woodrow survey of 100 major companies showed a median funding level of 125% of liabilities—only six disclosed a funding level below 100%.

## The Actuarial Assumptions

The valuation depends heavily on assumptions about:
- Investment return
- Salary increases
- Pension indexation

The critical factor is the **net investment return** (investment return minus salary increases). In the survey, most companies assumed a net return of around 2%, but actual experience was much higher.

## SSAP 24 – Accounting for Pension Costs

Before SSAP 24, most companies charged pension costs on a **cash basis** (actual contributions paid). This caused profits to fluctuate sharply when contributions were reduced after a surplus emerged.

SSAP 24 introduced an **accruals basis**: the pension cost in the P&L should reflect the expected cost of benefits earned during the period, regardless of actual contributions. A surplus can be accounted for in two main ways.

### 1. Spreading the Surplus

The surplus is spread over the **remaining service life** of the scheme’s members (typically 6–15 years). Three common spreading methods produce very different profit impacts:

| Method | Effect |
|--------|--------|
| Straight line | Front‑loads the benefit, giving larger early credits |
| Fixed amount (mortgage) | Level annual credit including interest |
| Percentage of pay | Smaller early credits, larger later |

Companies are not required to disclose which method they use, making comparisons impossible.

**Example – WH Smith (1991)**
- Regular cost: £18.2m
- Variation from regular cost (spreading surplus): £29.4m
- Result: **negative pension cost** of £11.2m (a credit to P&L)

This credit represented **nearly 50%** of WH Smith’s pre‑tax profits. The table in the chapter shows the credit declining sharply over the following years, turning into a charge by year five.

### 2. Prior Year Adjustment

Instead of spreading, a company may recognise the surplus immediately as a **prepayment asset** on the balance sheet, with a corresponding prior year adjustment to reserves. This was permitted on adoption of SSAP 24 and can have dramatic effects on ratios.

**Williams Holdings (1990)**
- Pension surplus included in debtors: £72.8m out of total debtors £225m
- Net asset value per share: 106.6p (reported) vs. 83.0p (excluding surplus)
- Current ratio: 1.4 vs. 1.1

**Babcock International (1991)**
- Pension surplus: £33.3m
- Net asset value: £70.2m (reported) vs. £36.9m (excluding surplus) – **nearly half** of reported NAV came from the pension prepayment

## Negative Pension Cost

When the amortised surplus exceeds the regular cost, a **negative pension cost** (credit) appears in the P&L. This is a non‑cash item—the pension fund does not pay the company. Counting it as normal earnings can mislead investors about cash generation.

## Interest and Investment Income on the Surplus

When a prepayment asset is created, SSAP 24 permits **notional interest** on that asset to be credited to the P&L. Some companies credit this against interest payable, artificially improving interest cover.

**Courtaulds Textiles (1990)**
- Pension credit reduced interest payable by £4.4m
- Reported interest cover: 4.4×
- Cover after removing pension credit: 3.2×—still safe, but the effect was material.

## The Danger of Uncertainty

Pension surpluses are not permanent assets. They can disappear due to:
- Falling investment returns
- Rising wage inflation
- Legislation (e.g., Social Security Act 1990 – mandatory indexation of pensions)
- Legal rulings (e.g., the Barber case – equal treatment of men and women)

**National Power** raised a £67m provision before privatisation to cover the potential past‑service liability from the Barber judgement.

**Boots** incorporated benefit improvements into its zero pension cost, anticipating the Social Security Act and Barber requirements, showing that future legislation can quickly erode a surplus.

## Key Insight

Pension fund surpluses have become a significant source of reported profits and balance sheet strength for many UK companies. However:
- The benefit is **non‑cash** (except in rare cases where cash is actually refunded)
- The methods of spreading surpluses are **subjective and vary widely**
- Surpluses can **disappear quickly** with changes in assumptions or legislation
- **Interest cover** can be artificially inflated if pension credits are netted against interest

Investors should:
1. **Exclude pension credits** from operating profit when assessing interest cover and cash generation
2. **Identify prepayment assets** and assess their impact on net asset value and gearing
3. **Consider the sustainability** of the benefit—a large credit today may become a charge tomorrow
4. **Watch for changes in actuarial assumptions** that could alter the reported surplus

As the chapter concludes, pension surpluses are often a “great illusion”—real in accounting terms, but far less certain than cash or tangible assets.
