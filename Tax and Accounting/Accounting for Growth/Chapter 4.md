# Summary of Chapter 4: The Pre-Acquisition Write-Down

This chapter examines one of the most powerful techniques for manipulating profits through acquisition accounting: writing down the value of acquired assets and creating provisions at the time of purchase.

## Basic Principles

When a company acquires another, it must bring the subsidiary's assets into the group accounts at **"fair value"** rather than their previous book value. The difference between the purchase price and the fair value of net assets acquired becomes **goodwill**, which must be accounted for.

**The Critical Point:** While fair value adjustments are legitimate, they create opportunities for abuse—particularly through provisions against asset values or for reorganisation costs.

## Stock and Debtor Write-Downs

### Coloroll's Acquisition of John Crowther (1988)

This example demonstrates how aggressive write-downs work:

- Coloroll acquired Crowther for **£213m**
- Crowther had net assets of **£70m** before acquisition
- Normal goodwill would be: £213m - £70m = **£145m**

**However**, Coloroll made additional write-downs totalling **£75m**:

| Item | £m |
|------|-----|
| Incidental costs | 11 |
| Stock write-down | 6 |
| Debtor write-down | 5 |
| Redundancy costs | 7 |
| Relocation costs | 13 |
| Other | 33 |
| **Total** | **75** |

The result: goodwill written off of **£224m**—**more than the total acquisition cost**.

**Why this matters:** These write-downs absorb costs at the expense of the balance sheet, thereby **boosting future profits**. When stock is later sold for more than its written-down value, or when reorganisation costs are covered by provisions rather than the P&L, profits appear higher than they should.

Coloroll also created **£56.5m in provisions** "arising on acquisitions." In 1989, **£52m of these provisions were utilised**—compared with pre-tax profits of just **£55.5m**. Without these provisions, profits would have been almost wiped out.

## Fixed Asset Write-Downs

### Tiphook's Acquisition of Sea Containers (1990)

- Acquisition cost: **£350m**
- Goodwill written off: **£139m**
- The acquired containers (average age 6 years) were written down as if Tiphook had owned them and depreciated them over 15 years to 15% residual value

**The benefit:** A depreciation saving of approximately **£15.4m per year** over the remaining 9-year life of the assets—boosting profits at the balance sheet's expense.

## Subsequent Amendments to Fair Value ("A Second Bite at the Cherry")

### TI Group's Additional Goodwill Adjustments

TI made acquisitions in 1987-88, then in 1989 added **£59.3m** in further goodwill adjustments—including **£4.2m** for John Crane, acquired **two and a half years earlier**.

The justification: "identification of additional investment opportunities to improve international competitiveness through major rationalisation."

**The significance:** This represents 22% of the original provisional fair values. By taking these costs through the balance sheet rather than the P&L, TI protected its profits. Note 27 shows that without these provisions to utilise, 1989 profits would have been **£58.5m lower** and 1990 profits **£30.3m lower**.

## What Harm Does It Do?

### Charterhall (1989)

- Acquired Lennards footwear and Corah plc for **£40m**
- This included goodwill of **£49m**—the assets had tangible value of **minus £9m**
- After raising provisions of **£16.1m**, shareholders' funds of **£77.6m** were outweighed by intangibles (**£34.5m**) and borrowings (**£84.2m**)

When profits disappeared and the Australian parent (Westmex) went into liquidation, Charterhall entered administrative receivership on **21 December 1990**.

**The lesson:** Creative acquisition accounting isn't just about presentation—it can mask the complete absence of tangible asset backing, leaving lenders unnerved and companies vulnerable when profits falter.
