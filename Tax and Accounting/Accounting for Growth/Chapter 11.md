# Summary of Chapter 11: Brand Accounting

This chapter examines one of the most controversial and inconsistent practices to emerge from the 1980s takeover boom: placing values on brands and recording them as intangible assets on the balance sheet. The practice arose from takeovers in the food, drink, and consumer goods industries, where brands often represented the primary value of an acquired business.

## Origins: Of Bids and Deals

Brand accounting emerged from two related pressures:

1. **Takeover defence** – Companies like Rowntree argued that predators were acquiring valuable brands “on the cheap” because those brands were not reflected in balance sheet net asset values. When Nestlé and Jacobs Suchard bid for Rowntree in 1988, the share price rose from 477p to the winning bid of 1075p—a premium partly justified by brand value.

2. **Acquisition accounting** – When a company paid a high price for a business with few tangible assets, the resulting goodwill could be enormous. By reclassifying part of that purchase price as “brands” rather than goodwill, the acquirer could avoid writing off goodwill against reserves or amortising it through the profit and loss account.

## Grand Metropolitan’s Pioneering Policy

Grand Met’s accounting policy became a model for many:

> “Significant brands, acquired since 1985, the value of which is not expected to diminish in the foreseeable future, are recorded in the balance sheet as fixed intangible assets. No amortisation is provided… the value is reviewed annually… and the cost written down as an exceptional item where permanent diminution has occurred.”

At 30 September 1990, Grand Met’s brands were included at **£2,317m**. Shareholders’ funds were **£3,427m** and net borrowings **£2,888m**—so published gearing was 84%. If brands were written off, gearing would rise to **260%**.

| Grand Met | Pillsbury | 86% |
| Cadbury Schweppes | Trebor | 75% |

Without brand accounting, these acquisitions would have produced huge goodwill write‑offs that would either deplete reserves or create a long‑term P&L charge.

## ED52 and the Push for Restriction

ED52 (Accounting for Intangible Fixed Assets) took a cautious view. An intangible asset should be recognised in the balance sheet only if:

1. Historical costs associated with the asset are known or ascertainable.
2. The characteristics can be clearly distinguished from goodwill and other assets.
3. Its cost can be measured independently of goodwill and other assets.

These criteria effectively barred capitalising “home‑grown” brands (like those created internally) and made it difficult to justify capitalising acquired brands unless strict conditions were met.

## Ranks Hovis McDougall – The Exception

RHM went further than anyone, capitalising **both acquired and internally created brands** at “current cost”. In 1989 it carried **£740m** of brands on its balance sheet, valued with the help of Interbrand Group. This was directly contrary to the spirit of ED52, which rejected home‑grown brand capitalisation.

## Interbrand’s Valuation Method

Interbrand calculated brand value using a two‑step approach:

1. **Brand earnings** – derived from profit before tax, minus profits from own‑label manufacture, weighted over the last three years (most recent year half weight, previous two years quarter each).

2. **Multiple applied** – based on a score out of 100 assessing brand strength in seven areas:
   - Leadership (market share and influence)
   - Stability (resistance to competitive attacks)
   - Market (consumer vs. industrial, fashion risk)
   - Internationality
   - Trend (long‑term earnings direction)
   - Investment (advertising spend and quality)
   - Protection (trademarks, patents)

The result was subjective and could easily lead to overstatement.

The lack of consistency was glaring:
- **Unilever** – capitalised no brands, despite having many.
- **Forte** – did not capitalise its name, while **Ladbroke** capitalised Hilton.
- **Brewers** – none followed Guinness’s lead.

## The Blackpool FC Analogy

Michael Renshall, Chairman of the Accounting Standards Committee, offered a memorable critique. He compared brand accounting to buying Blackpool Football Club in 1953, when it won the FA Cup. “There would have been a lot of goodwill in the price someone would have paid then,” he said. “But it is obvious now that the goodwill did not have an indefinite life.” (Blackpool had since fallen to Division Three.)

## WPP’s Stretched Approach

WPP capitalised corporate brand names based on “the present value of notional royalty savings” and “estimates of profits attributable to brand loyalty.” No depreciation was provided because, in the directors’ opinion, the brands did not have a finite economic life. Critics argued that almost any acquired company could be said to have an indefinite life if that phrase were interpreted so loosely.

## Key Insight

Brand accounting was never driven by a consistent accounting standard; it was a pragmatic response to takeover pressures and the awkwardness of goodwill. The result is a patchwork where some companies capitalise acquired brands, some capitalise home‑grown brands, and others do nothing. Comparisons between companies are virtually impossible.

The fundamental problem remains: unlike tangible assets, brands are not separable from the business in most cases, their value is highly subjective, and they almost certainly do have a finite useful life—even if that life is long. Investors should treat brand values on the balance sheet with great scepticism, focusing instead on the cash flows and profitability that those brands generate, rather than accepting them as unquestioned assets.
