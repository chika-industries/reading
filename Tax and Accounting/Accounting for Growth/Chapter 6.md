# Summary of Chapter 6: Deferred Consideration

This chapter examines "earn-outs"—acquisition structures where part of the purchase price is deferred and contingent on the acquired company's future performance. While popular in the 1980s, particularly among advertising agencies and people-based businesses, these arrangements created hidden time bombs in company balance sheets.

## How Deferred Consideration Works

In a typical earn-out, the acquirer makes an upfront payment, with further payments (in cash or shares) based on a multiple of future profits of the acquired company.

**Perceived advantages:**
1. **Limited downside risk**—if the acquired business performs badly, future payments adjust downward
2. **Retaining key people**—ties in vendors whose talents are crucial to the business
3. **Immediate earnings enhancement**—the acquired company's profits are consolidated at once, but additional consideration is paid later

## The Hidden Dangers

### Saatchi & Saatchi (1989)

Saatchi's accounts showed contingent liabilities for deferred consideration of up to **£119.5m**, payable through 1995. At the time, the group already had:
- Negative net worth of **£264.2m** (after £434.6m goodwill write-off)
- Bank loans and overdrafts of **£121.5m**

Any payment of these deferred liabilities would have further increased gearing and worsened the balance sheet.

### WPP Group

WPP made over 30 small acquisitions through earn-outs, with maximum future payments around **£130m**. The payment schedule (assuming 15% post-tax profit growth):

| Year | Shares (£m) | Cash (£m) | Total (£m) |
|------|-------------|-----------|------------|
| 1991 | 12 | 19 | 31 |
| 1992 | 6 | 10 | 16 |
| 1993 | 17 | 17 | 34 |
| 1994 | 12 | 33 | 45 |
| 1995 | 2 | 3 | 5 |
| **Total** | **49** | **82** | **131** |

**The devastating effect of a falling share price:**

When WPP's share price collapsed from **700p** (early 1990) to **48p** (early 1992), the number of shares needed to satisfy the £49m share-based consideration exploded:

- At 700p: **7.0 million shares**
- At 48p: **102.0 million shares**

With only 43 million shares in issue at the time, this would require increasing shares outstanding by nearly **2½ times**—causing massive dilution of earnings per share and making a maintained dividend impossible.

### Cray Electronics

Cray made numerous acquisitions, all for shares and virtually all on deferred consideration. The warning signs were there:
- Capitalised R&D
- Merger accounting
- Property profits
- Increasing share outflow as deferred consideration became payable

After the Chairman and Chief Executive resigned, 1989 figures were restated from **£17.03m** pre-tax profit to just **£1.33m**.

### FKB Group

The most extreme example. Note 25 of the 1989 accounts showed:

| | £m |
|------|-----|
| Maximum deferred consideration liability | 58.8 |
| Shareholders' funds | 4.7 |
| Goodwill already written off | (40.1) |

A liability 12.5 times larger than shareholders' funds. FKB was pushed into administration by these commitments.

## Why It Matters

1. **Cash flow risk**—If deferred consideration is payable in cash, the acquirer must have sufficient funds when payments fall due, regardless of trading conditions

2. **Dilution risk**—If payable in shares at a fixed value, a falling share price dramatically increases the number of shares required, diluting existing shareholders

3. **Dividend pressure**—More shares mean higher dividend costs, potentially forcing dividend cuts or elimination (as happened with WPP)

4. **Hidden leverage**—These commitments often weren't shown as liabilities on the balance sheet, only disclosed in notes as contingent liabilities

## Key Insight

Deferred consideration arrangements that look attractive in a rising market become lethal when share prices fall or trading deteriorates. The "immediate earnings enhancement" is illusory—it represents borrowing future profits at an unknown cost. Companies that built themselves through earn-outs (Saatchi, WPP, Cray, FKB) all faced severe consequences when the bill came due.

The WPP share price chart (Figure 6.1) tells the story: from a high of 1095p in 1987 to a low of 33p in 1991—a stark illustration of how creative acquisition structures can unravel when the market turns.
