# Summary of Chapter 7: Extraordinary and Exceptional Items

This chapter examines one of the most debated areas in accounting: the classification of items as either "exceptional" (above the line, affecting Earnings Per Share) or "extraordinary" (below the line, bypassing EPS). The distinction matters enormously because EPS is "the single most important figure affecting the analysts' and hence the market's view" of a company.

## The Definitions (SSAP 6)

**Extraordinary items:** Material items from events or transactions *outside* ordinary activities, expected not to recur frequently or regularly.

**Exceptional items:** Material items from events or transactions *within* ordinary activities, requiring separate disclosure due to size or incidence.

The theory seems clear, but practice reveals enormous variation.

## Inconsistent Treatment

### Reckitt & Colman (1988)
Showed an **extraordinary gain** including profits from disposal of businesses and major sites.

### British Aerospace (1989)
Showed an **exceptional profit** on sale of investments in Daf NV and Istel Holdings.

Same type of transaction, completely different treatment.

### RHM's "Extraordinary" Closures

Ranks Hovis McDougall reported extraordinary closure costs with remarkable frequency:

| | 1984 | 1985 | 1986 | 1987 | 1988 | 1989 | 1990 | 1991 |

Over eight years, these "extraordinary" costs averaged **22%** of reported net profits—a clear abuse of the English language if not SSAP 6.

## The Midland Bank LDC Provisions (1987)

The most famous change in treatment involved Midland Bank's provisions for bad debts to Less Developed Countries.

**The background:** UK clearing banks had lent heavily to LDCs. By 1987, led by Citibank, they began raising provisions to 25-30% of exposure.

**Midland's controversial move:** At interim results, Midland placed its **£653m** LDC provision **below the line** as an extraordinary item. This defied logic—bad debts are a normal part of banking, and Midland had plenty of experience with them (Crocker National Bank in California).

**Why it mattered:** If taken above the line, Midland's dividend would have been uncovered. The company felt vulnerable to predators (Hanson bought a stake, Hongkong Bank circled, even Saatchi & Saatchi made a bid approach).

**The outcome:** By the final accounts, under pressure, Midland moved the provision **above the line**, disclosing a loss before tax of **£505m** and loss per share of **125.3p**—though the dividend was held at 20.1p, reinforced by the Hongkong Bank capital injection.

## Extraordinary Items and Company Failures

A striking pattern emerges: companies using extraordinary debits heavily often failed soon after.

| Company | Year | Extraordinary Items | Pre-tax Profit | Outcome |
|---------|------|---------------------|----------------|---------|
| Arley Holdings | 1989 | (£0.97m) | £0.63m | Receivership 27.11.90 |
| Bestwood | 1988 | (£5.34m) | £2.61m | Receivership 24.4.90 |
| A Goldberg & Sons | 1988 | (£0.76m) | £3.23m | Receivership 8.6.90 |
| Reliant | 1989 | (£0.54m) | £1.94m | Receivership 2.11.90 |

In every case, extraordinary debits substantially exceeded trading profits—and all four companies failed.

## The Future: ASB Changes

The Accounting Standards Board (1991 Exposure Draft) proposed restricting extraordinary items to:
- Expropriation of assets (confiscation or nationalisation)
- Fundamental changes in taxation basis

Redundancy, reorganisation costs, disposal profits/losses, write-downs, provisions—all would move **above the line**, making EPS much more volatile.

**Impact on the shipping and transport sector:**

| Company | Pre-tax (£m) | Extraordinaries (£m) | Ratio | Historic PER | Adjusted PER |
|---------|--------------|---------------------|-------|---------------|--------------|
| AB Ports | 164 | (43) | -26% | 11.1 | 24.3 |
| Ocean | 133 | (33) | -25% | 12.1 | 12.1 |
| LEP | 74 | (11) | -15% | 9.1 | 12.8 |
| NFC | 255 | (6) | -2% | 11.9 | 12.3 |
| P&O | 955 | 85 | +9% | 13.8 | 12.7 |

AB Ports' extraordinary costs (redundancies from abolishing the Dock Labour Scheme) would become exceptional, dramatically raising its effective P/E ratio.

## Key Insight

The distinction between exceptional and extraordinary items has been used to shield EPS from negative impacts while sometimes including positive ones. The ASB's changes will force all significant items above the line, making earnings more volatile but also more honest. Investors who ignored "below the line" items often missed warning signs of impending failure.
