# Summary of Chapter 8: Off Balance Sheet Finance

This chapter examines techniques used to keep debt and assets off a company's balance sheet, thereby reducing reported gearing and creating a misleading picture of financial health. The usual aim is to make the company appear less leveraged than it truly is.

## What Is Off Balance Sheet Finance?

**Definition:** "The funding or refinancing of a company's operations in such a way that, under legal requirements and existing accounting conventions, some or all of the finance may not be shown on its balance sheet."

The key principle emerging from accounting standards (ICEAW Technical Release 603, ED42, ED49) is that financial statements should reflect the **substance** rather than the **legal form** of transactions.

## The "Quasi-Subsidiary" Structure

The most common technique involves creating a company that the parent controls but which falls outside the Companies Act definition of a subsidiary—usually through complex share structures that circumvent the 50% equity holding rule. Such companies are accounted for as **associates** (showing only the net investment) rather than **subsidiaries** (showing all assets and liabilities).

## LEP Group: Sale of St Paul's Vista (1988)

**The transaction:**
- LEP sold a development property (St Paul's Vista) for **£120m** to St Paul's Vista Limited—an associate with a complex share structure where LEP had an **effective 90% interest**
- The property had a net book value of **£111.3m**, creating a profit of **£8.7m**
- This profit was credited to group pre-tax profits as an exceptional gain
- Because the Inland Revenue treated it as an intra-group transaction, no tax was payable—**£8.7m flowed directly to earnings**

**The benefits to LEP:**
- Removed debt from the published balance sheet
- More than **doubled reported EPS** in 1988
- Enabled **£2.86m** from revaluation reserve to become distributable as dividends

**The ultimate outcome:**
The transaction was eventually reversed, bringing the property (Swiss Bank House) back on balance sheet. LEP's 1990 accounts restated 1989 net borrowings from **£214.7m** to **£333.7m**. By 1991:

| Period | Operating Profit | Pre-tax Profit |
|--------|------------------|----------------|
| Six months to June 1991 | £20.1m | £1.9m |
| Full year 1990 | £55.7m | £29.4m |

Gearing exceeded 200%, the dividend was passed, and by October 1991 LEP was forced into a refinancing agreement owing **£470m** of debt. The share price collapsed from 187p to 7.5p.

## Burton Group: Hall & Sons Ltd (later High Street Property Investments Ltd)

**The structure (1986):**
- Burton sold and leased back **£100m** of properties with **£70m** of borrowings to Hall & Sons Ltd
- Burton's equity interest was only **50%** (through "B" ordinary shares), but it also held all of the far more numerous **£1 Preference Shares**
- No profit was booked (book and market value were equal), but the borrowings were taken **off balance sheet**

**The effect on apparent gearing:**
The finance quasi-subsidiaries (BG Holdings, Burton Group Personal Accounts, Welbeck Finance) conducted Burton's credit card business—an important part of operations contributing **£22.2m** to pre-tax profit in 1986. Their combined borrowings of **£428m** compared with Burton's shareholders' funds of **£440.5m** and consolidated balance sheet borrowings of just **£218.3m**.

**The reckoning (1991):**
Under a new Finance Director, Burton consolidated High Street Property Investments as a subsidiary. Gearing before the change: **24.3%**. Gearing after: **43.1%** —virtually halved by the off-balance sheet structure.

## Joint Ventures: Rush & Tompkins (1989)

**The structure:**
- Net investment in property development joint ventures: **£7.78m** (shown in stocks)
- Additional working capital provided: **£17m** (included in debtors)
- **£89m** of "non-recourse or limited recourse" bank facilities provided to these joint ventures

**The danger:**
These loans were not shown in Rush & Tompkins' consolidated balance sheet, where bank borrowings of **£25m** compared with shareholders' funds of **£35m**. But "non-recourse" proved a slippery concept—when Rush & Tompkins went into receivership in May 1990, it reportedly owed over **£300m**, not £25m.

## Dissimilar Activities

SSAP 14 allows exclusion of subsidiaries with "dissimilar activities" from consolidation. Burton Group exploited this to keep its finance subsidiaries off balance sheet—despite their business being integral to Burton's operations and contributing significantly to profits.

## Regulatory Changes

**Companies Act 1989** introduced a new definition of subsidiary undertaking based on **de facto control** rather than just equity holding:
- Majority of voting rights
- Power to appoint/remove majority of the board
- Control of voting rights through agreement
- Ability to direct operating and financial policies
- Participating interest with dominant influence

**ED49** requires quasi-subsidiaries to be included in consolidated accounts, defining them as vehicles that are "directly or indirectly a source of benefits or risks for the reporting enterprise."

## Key Insight

Off-balance sheet finance doesn't reduce a company's actual debt—it merely hides it. The LEP, Burton, and Rush & Tompkins examples show that when the structures unravel, the true debt levels emerge, often with catastrophic consequences. The regulatory changes (Companies Act 1989 and ED49) have made these structures much harder to maintain, but investors must still be vigilant: if a transaction seems too good to be true—selling an asset while retaining control, or keeping significant borrowings off the balance sheet—it probably is.
