# Summary of Chapter 13: Convertibles with Put Options and AMPS

This chapter examines complex financial instruments that proliferated in the 1980s, particularly convertible bonds with put options (premium puts) and Auction Market Preferred Shares (AMPS). These instruments offered companies cheap-looking financing but often carried hidden future liabilities that were not properly accounted for—until the bills came due, sometimes with disastrous consequences.

## Convertibles with Put Options

A convertible bond with a put option gives the holder the right to sell the bond back to the issuer at a specified premium on a future date. The combination of a low coupon and a redemption premium creates a yield to redemption that reflects the true cost of borrowing—but many companies failed to provide for the premium in their accounts, showing only the low coupon in interest payable.

### London International Group (LIG) – A Typical Structure

In March 1981, LIG issued £50m of 4½% convertible bonds when UK base rate was around 10%. Terms:
- Conversion price: 452p (share price then 284p – a 59% premium)
- Put option: holders could require redemption on 25 March 1992 at 120.70% of principal
- Combined with the 4½% coupon, this gave a redemption yield of 8.53%

**The accounting problem:** LIG initially showed only the 4½% coupon in interest payable, ignoring the redemption premium. When it became clear that conversion would not happen (share price was 297p in early 1992), the company had to make a belated provision.

In the 1991 rights issue document, LIG admitted:
- A £2.3m supplementary interest charge would be included in interest payable
- An exceptional item of £5.8m would provide for supplementary interest from 1987 to 1990

The bonds had funded acquisitions in Italy and Spain; the profits from those acquisitions had been shown in full while the true cost of funding was hidden for years.

### Saatchi & Saatchi – A Near‑Fatal Outcome

Saatchi issued £176.5m of Convertible Europreference shares due to be redeemed for £211m in July 1992. By then the company was in such difficulty that this liability alone could have pushed it into receivership. Instead, Europreference shareholders were offered ordinary shares, taking 64% of the enlarged capital and massively diluting existing shareholders. The share price collapse illustrated the severity of the problem.

### Tesco – The Correct Approach

Tesco issued convertible bonds convertible at 174p or redeemable at 127.625% of principal in 1992. Unlike many others, Tesco:
- Charged the full effective interest rate (including amortisation of the redemption premium) to the profit and loss account each year
- Restated prior years to reflect this more prudent treatment
- Eventually, because the share price performed well, bondholders converted and the unused redemption premium was written back to reserves

## Rolling Puts – Postponing the Inevitable

Some companies tried to extend the redemption date to buy time for conversion. LIG attempted this in May 1989, moving the put date from March 1992 to March 1997 with a higher premium (155.09% of principal) to maintain an 8.53% yield. It did not solve the underlying problem of inadequate provisioning or cash availability.

## Auction Market Preferred Shares (AMPS)

AMPS are preference shares with dividends set by periodic auctions. They offered companies the appearance of equity capital while often being effectively debt, especially when backed by letters of credit.

### The Beazer Example

Beazer issued $101m of AMPS backed by a letter of credit from National Westminster Bank. If the company were wound up, NatWest would repay AMPS holders—effectively moving the AMPS from equity into debt. The AMPS were shown as minority interest, but their variable rate could rise at auction just when the company was under stress. When the building recession hit, Beazer was forced into a rescue takeover by Hanson.

## Key Insight

Convertibles with put options and AMPS were classic examples of financial alchemy—instruments that appeared to offer cheap financing or equity-like capital but concealed real future costs. The failure to account for the true effective interest rate (by amortising redemption premiums) or to recognise the substance of AMPS as debt rather than equity allowed companies to report higher profits and lower gearing than was prudent.

When the market turned, the hidden liabilities surfaced. Some companies (like Tesco) navigated safely because they had accounted prudently. Others (like Saatchi, LIG, Beazer, and Ratners) faced near‑collapse, dilution, or takeover. Investors should treat any instrument that promises cheap financing in a high‑interest‑rate environment with extreme scepticism and examine whether the full effective cost is being charged against profits.
