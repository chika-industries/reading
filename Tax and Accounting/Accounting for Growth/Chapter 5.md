# Summary of Chapter 5: Disposals

This chapter examines how companies can manipulate profits through the timing and classification of gains from selling assets or subsidiaries. The key distinction is whether disposal profits are taken "above the line" (included in earnings per share) or "below the line" (treated as extraordinary items).

## Disposal of Fixed Assets

Companies have significant discretion in how they classify profits from asset sales, leading to major inconsistencies even within the same industry.

### Ladbroke vs. P&O: Contrasting Treatments

**Ladbroke Group (1990):** Sold investment properties for £86.2m. The profit was taken **above the line** (included in operating profit), enhancing reported earnings.

**P&O:** Accounting policy explicitly states that "capital profits on the sale of these properties... are recognised in **extraordinary items**" (below the line). Over three years, P&O excluded substantial gains from EPS:

| Year | Property Profits (£m) |
|------|----------------------|
| 1988 | 48.9 |
| 1989 | 25.5 |
| 1990 | 25.0 |

### Retail Sector Variation

A KPMG study of 34 retail companies found dramatic variation in how property profits were treated:

| Treatment | Number of Companies |
|-----------|--------------------|
| Exceptional item | 5 |
| Part of "other operating income" | 4 |
| "Other item" or other income | 4 |
| Separately disclosed on P&L face | 3 |
| Extraordinary item | 3 |
| Part of ordinary activities (notes) | 4 |
| No mention | 11 |

**WH Smith (1989):** Property profits (£5.6m) taken **above the line** as part of operating profit.

**Asda (1990):** Property profits (£88.8m) from refinancing the Gateway acquisition treated as an **extraordinary item** (below the line).

## Company Disposals

### Tootal's Phased Sale of Da Gama Textile Company

Tootal sold its 49.8% stake in Da Gama for approximately £25m, payable in five tranches. Da Gama contributed the lion's share of Tootal's £8.5m related company income—an outright sale would have caused a serious profit shortfall.

**The "elegant" solution:**
- **1988/89:** First tranche profit (£3.4m) booked to pre-tax profits
- **Also 1988/89:** Full year's related company contribution from the 49.8% stake
- Together, these accounted for **around 25%** of Tootal's reported pre-tax profits

A more prudent approach would have treated the entire disposal profit as extraordinary or treated the deferred proceeds as a long-term debtor.

## Deconsolidation on Disposal

Some companies deconsolidate subsidiaries at the announcement of a sale rather than at completion—particularly useful when the subsidiary is **loss-making**.

### TSB/Target Group (1990)

TSB decided to dispose of Target early in its 1989/90 financial year. At the half-year, it **did not consolidate** Target's results, which showed:

| Period | Target Result |
|--------|---------------|
| First half 1989/90 | £17m loss |
| First half 1988/89 | £3m profit |

**The effect on TSB's reported profits:**

| | Reported | Restated (including Target) |
|---------------|----------|-----------------------------|
| Pre-tax profits | £175m | £158m |
| Growth | +7% | -5% |

A useful smoothing technique at a time when TSB was under pressure post-flotation.

### Midland Bank/EAB (1984)

Midland reduced its representation on European American Bancorp's board and reclassified its 20% holding from an **associate** (where share of profits/losses go through P&L) to a **trade investment** (only dividends affect P&L).

**The result:** EAB's £20m loss (Midland's share) was taken to **reserves** (balance sheet) rather than the profit and loss account—protecting reported profits while the balance sheet suffered.

## Two That Failed

### Charterhall (1989)

Pre-tax profit of **£13.1m** included disposal profits of **£8.5m** from selling properties and petroleum interests. Removing these reduced interest cover from **3.1 times** to **1.7 times**—a critical difference in a company where goodwill had already eroded the tangible asset base. Charterhall entered administrative receivership on 21 December 1990.

### Leading Leisure (1989)

Reported operating profit of **£11.9m** included **£10m** from selling properties to Duchy Parklands Limited—a company 50%-owned by Leading Leisure and financed by it.

**The reality check:**

| | As Reported | Adjusted* |
|---------------|-------------|-----------|
| Operating profit | £11.9m | £1.9m |
| Interest payable | £4.9m | £11.3m |
| Interest cover | 2.5x | 0.5x |

*Operating profit less property sale; interest payable ignoring capitalised interest

Leading Leisure went into administrative receivership on 9 November 1990.

## Key Insight

The treatment of disposal profits is highly subjective and inconsistent. Profits from selling assets or subsidiaries have little to do with ongoing trading performance and should be evaluated separately. When companies rely on such gains to bolster reported profits—particularly through sales to related parties—it often signals underlying weakness.
