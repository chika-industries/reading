Here is a summary of Articles 1 through 5 of the U.S.-Japan Tax Treaty (signed November 6, 2003):

### Article 1: General Scope
- **Applicability:** The treaty applies to residents of one or both of the U.S. and Japan, unless stated otherwise.
- **Non-Discrimination:** The treaty cannot restrict benefits already available under a country’s domestic laws or other international agreements.
- **Dispute Resolution:** Interpretation or application disputes are resolved exclusively via the Mutual Agreement Procedure (Article 25), not trade agreements like GATS.
- **Saving Clause:** The U.S. generally retains the right to tax its citizens and residents as if the treaty hadn't come into effect.
- **Exit Tax:** The U.S. can tax former citizens/long-term residents for 10 years following loss of status if the loss was for tax avoidance purposes.

### Article 2: Taxes Covered
- **Japan:** National income tax and corporation tax.
- **United States:** Federal income taxes under the Internal Revenue Code (excluding social security taxes).
- **Future Taxes:** The treaty also applies to substantially similar taxes enacted after the signature date.

### Article 3: General Definitions
- **Japan:** Includes territorial sea and areas where Japan has jurisdiction for tax purposes.
- **United States:** Includes states, D.C., and territorial sea, but excludes possessions (e.g., Puerto Rico, Guam).
- **Person:** Includes individuals, companies, and any other body of persons.
- **Company:** Any body corporate or entity treated as such for tax purposes.
- **International Traffic:** Transport by ship/aircraft except when solely between places in the other state.
- **Interpretation:** For undefined terms, the meaning under the tax laws of the state applying the treaty prevails (unless context requires otherwise).

### Article 4: Resident
- **Definition:** A person liable to tax in a state due to domicile, residence, citizenship, place of incorporation, etc. Includes the state itself, political subdivisions, pension funds, and tax-exempt charitable organizations. It excludes those taxed only on local income (source basis).
- **Dual-Resident Individuals (Tie-Breaker):** For individuals who qualify as residents of both countries, status is determined by:
    1.  Permanent home (where available);
    2.  Center of vital interests (closer personal/economic relations);
    3.  Habitual abode;
    4.  Nationality;
    5.  Mutual agreement (if all else fails).
- **Dual-Resident Entities (Non-Individuals):** Competent authorities must determine residency by mutual agreement; otherwise, the entity is not entitled to treaty benefits.

### Article 5: Permanent Establishment (PE)
- **Definition:** A fixed place of business through which business is wholly or partly carried on (e.g., branch, office, factory, mine).
- **Time Threshold:** A construction, installation, or exploration project constitutes a PE only if it lasts more than 12 months.
- **Exceptions (Preparatory/Auxiliary):** PE status does **not** arise from facilities used solely for storage, display, delivery, purchasing, or collecting information.
- **Dependent Agents:** A person (not an independent agent) with authority to conclude contracts in the enterprise’s name creates a PE.
- **Independent Agents:** Using a broker or independent agent does **not** create a PE if they are acting in the ordinary course of their business.
- **Subsidiaries:** A subsidiary does not automatically constitute a PE of its parent company.
