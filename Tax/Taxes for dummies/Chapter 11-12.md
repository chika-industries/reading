### Chapter 11: Itemized Deductions: Schedule A
This chapter is a deep dive into Schedule A, the form used by taxpayers who choose to itemize their deductions instead of taking the standard deduction.

- **Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing:** It reminds readers that you should only itemize if your total allowable expenses exceed your standard deduction amount. - **Medical and Dental Expenses (Lines 1-4):** You can deduct unreimbursed medical and dental expenses that exceed **7.5% of your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)** . It provides a comprehensive list of qualifying expenses (e.g., doctor visits, prescriptions, insurance premiums, long-term care, travel for medical care, weight-loss programs for specific diseases) and explicitly lists non-deductible items (e.g., cosmetic surgery, health club dues, over-the-counter medicines).

- **Interest You Paid (Lines 8-10):**

    - **Investment Interest:** Interest paid on money borrowed to invest (e.g., margin loans) is deductible, but only up to the amount of your net investment income.

### Chapter 12: Interest and Dividend Income: Form 1040, Schedule B
This chapter explains Schedule B, which is required if your taxable interest or ordinary dividend income exceeds $1,500.

- **Part I: Interest Income:**
    - It explains how to read **Form 1099-INT** and **Form 1099-OID**, which report taxable interest and original issue discount (like from zero-coupon bonds).
    - It covers the rules for reporting interest from U.S. Savings Bonds (EE and I bonds), including the option to defer reporting interest until the bond is cashed in.
    - It discusses adjustments for bond premiums and market discounts, which can affect your taxable interest and your cost basis in the bond.
    - **Line 3 (U.S. Savings Bond Interest Exclusion):** It explains the rules for excluding interest from Series EE and I bonds if the proceeds are used to pay for qualified higher education expenses, subject to income limits (phase-out begins at $83,200 for singles, $124,800 for joint filers in 2021).
    - It touches on the complex rules for **interest-free loans** and the concept of "imputed interest."
- **Part II: Dividend Income:**
    - It explains **Form 1099-DIV**, distinguishing between **ordinary dividends** (taxed at regular rates) and **qualified dividends** (taxed at the lower capital gains rates of 0%, 15%, or 20%).
    - To be "qualified," the dividends must be paid by a U.S. corporation or a qualified foreign corporation, and you must have held the stock for more than 60 days during the 121-day period surrounding the ex-dividend date.
    - It clarifies that dividends from money market funds, REITs, and tax-exempt corporations do not qualify for the lower rates.
- **Part III: Foreign Accounts and Trusts:**
    - This section contains important questions about foreign financial accounts.
    - **Line 7a:** You must check "Yes" if you had a financial interest in or signature authority over a foreign account. If the total value of all foreign accounts exceeded $10,000, you are required to file a separate **FinCEN Form 114 (FBAR)** electronically.
    - **Line 8:** You must check "Yes" if you are a beneficiary of a foreign trust, which requires filing additional complex forms like Form 3520.
