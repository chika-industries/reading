### Summary of Chapter 16: Protect Your Wealth from Pirates, Predators, and Other Plaintiffs

This chapter addresses a critical companion to tax planning: asset protection. The author argues that the best time to build a strategy to protect your wealth from lawsuits is at the same time you are building your strategy to protect it from the government (taxes).

- **True Freedom and Its Risks:** The author defines true freedom as having enough wealth that you don't have to work. He shares a personal story about a business partnership that went bad, resulting in a long, expensive court battle and the loss of 75% of that practice's value. The lesson learned was that even with good legal agreements, you are vulnerable if you don't maintain physical and operational control.
- **RULE #14: You must maintain control of your assets at all times and in all circumstances.** This is the most important rule of asset protection. If you don't have control, a plaintiff or a bad partner can take it from you.
- **Three Goals of Asset Protection:**
    1.  **Prevent a lawsuit.** (Best outcome)
    2.  **Stay under the radar.** (Less likely to be sued)
    3.  **Win any lawsuit.** (Expensive and time-consuming, even if you win)
    The chapter focuses on Goal #1—prevention—because even winning a lawsuit can be financially and emotionally devastating.
- **Follow the Money:** Plaintiffs and their lawyers sue because they want your money. If you can structure your assets so that it's clear they cannot get paid even if they win, lawyers (especially those working on contingency in the U.S.) will likely not take the case.
- **The Right Entity for Protection:** Just as with taxes, the entity you choose is critical. The protection rules are often different from the tax rules, so your tax advisor and attorney must work together.
    - **Trusts:** Excellent for protection. If the beneficiary is different from the grantor, creditors generally cannot access the trust assets.
    - **General Partnerships:** "Awful." You are liable for everything your partners and employees do. You can be in one without even knowing it.
    - **Limited Partnerships:** Good for limited partners (they can only lose their investment). The general partner is still exposed, but that can be a corporation with few assets.
    - **Corporations:** Good for protecting personal assets from company lawsuits. As a shareholder, you are generally not personally liable.
    - **Limited Liability Companies (LLCs):** The "absolute best" for protecting business and investment assets.
- **The Power of the Charging Order:** This is the superpower of an LLC. If you are personally sued, a plaintiff cannot take your ownership in an LLC, cannot vote your shares, and cannot force the sale of assets. They can only get a "charging order," which is a right to any *distributions* made to you. As the manager, you can simply choose not to make any distributions while the lawsuit is pending. The plaintiff gets nothing, and their lawyer doesn't get paid. Lawyers know this and tend not to sue people who own assets in strong-charging-order states like Nevada and Wyoming.
- **Key Strategy #16:** The tax strategy is to **include asset protection planning when you create your tax strategy.** The author warns that many CPAs (Certified Public Accountants) "Cannot Protect Assets." They may recommend entities based on tax benefits alone, ignoring weak asset protection laws in certain states. The correct process is: (1) Work with a good tax advisor to create an entity structure that maximizes tax benefits. (2) Have that structure reviewed by an asset protection attorney to ensure it also provides maximum legal protection. This prevents costly mistakes and ensures you are protected from both the IRS and plaintiffs.
