Here are the summaries for Articles 16 through 25 of the U.S.-Japan Tax Treaty.

### Article 16: Entertainers and Sportsmen
- **Taxing Right:** Income derived by a resident of one country (e.g., a Japanese musician) from their personal activities as an entertainer or sportsman in the other country (e.g., a concert in the U.S.) **may be taxed in that other country**, even if the performance does not constitute a permanent establishment (overriding the normal rules of Articles 7 and 14).
- **De Minimis Exception:** This source-country taxation does **not** apply if the gross receipts (including reimbursed expenses) from the activities do not exceed **$10,000 (or equivalent in Yen)** for the taxable year.
- **Star Companies:** If the income from the performance accrues not to the individual but to another person (e.g., a loan-out corporation), that income may still be taxed in the country where the performance takes place, unless the performance contract allows that other person to designate the individual performer.

### Article 17: Pensions and Annuities
- **Pensions (Private):** Pensions and other similar remuneration (including social security payments) beneficially owned by a resident of a Contracting State shall be taxable **only in that State**.
- **Annuities:** Annuities derived and beneficially owned by an individual resident of a Contracting State are taxable **only in that State**.
- **Alimony/Child Support:** Periodic payments made under a written separation agreement or divorce decree by a resident of one State to a resident of the other are taxable **only in the payer's State of residence**. However, they are **taxable in neither State** if the payer is not entitled to a deduction for the payments.

### Article 18: Government Service
- **Salaries (Active Service):** Salaries (other than pensions) paid by a Government (or political subdivision) to an individual for governmental services are taxable **only in that State**.
    - *Exception:* They are taxable **only in the other State** if the services are rendered there and the individual is a resident of that other State *and* is a national of that other State (or did not become a resident solely for the purpose of rendering the services).
- **Pensions (Government):** Pensions paid by, or out of funds created by, a Government for services rendered are taxable **only in that State**.
    - *Exception:* They are taxable **only in the other State** if the individual is a resident *and* a national of that other State.
- **Business Activities:** These rules do not apply if the services are rendered in connection with a business carried on by the Government (e.g., a state-owned railway); instead, the normal rules for private employment (Articles 14-17) apply.

### Article 19: Students and Business Apprentices
- **Exemption:** Payments received by a student or business apprentice who is, or was immediately before visiting, a resident of one State (e.g., Japan) and is present in the other State (e.g., the U.S.) for education or training are **exempt from tax in the visited State**, provided the payments are made from outside that visited State.
- **Time Limit for Apprentices:** For a business apprentice, this exemption applies only for a period not exceeding **one year** from the date training begins.

### Article 20: Professors, Teachers, and Researchers
- **Exemption:** An individual who visits one State temporarily to teach or conduct research at a university or other educational institution, and who remains a resident of the other State, shall be **exempt from tax in the visited State** on remuneration for such teaching or research.
- **Time Limit:** The exemption applies for a period not exceeding **two years** from the date of arrival.
- **Private Benefit Exclusion:** The exemption does **not** apply to income from research undertaken primarily for the private benefit of a specific person(s).

### Article 21: Other Income
- **General Rule:** Items of income beneficially owned by a resident of a Contracting State, wherever arising, that are **not dealt with in the previous articles** (e.g., Article 6-20) shall be taxable **only in that State** (the residence state).
- **PE Exception:** If the beneficial owner has a PE in the other State and the right or property generating the income is effectively connected with that PE, then Article 7 (Business Profits) applies instead.
- **Excess Income:** If a special relationship causes the amount of "other income" to exceed an arm's length amount, the excess may be taxed in the source country at a rate of **5%** .
- **Anti-Abuse:** Similar to the royalty article, a resident cannot be considered the beneficial owner if the payment is structured to pass through them to a third person not entitled to treaty benefits.

### Article 22: Limitation on Benefits (LOB)
- **Purpose:** This anti-treaty-shopping provision ensures that only qualified residents of a Contracting State receive treaty benefits. A resident must meet specific criteria to be considered a "qualified person."
- **Qualified Persons:** A resident is entitled to treaty benefits automatically if it is:
    - **(a)** An individual;
    - **(b)** The Contracting State itself or its political subdivisions;
    - **(c)** A publicly-traded company (with its shares regularly traded on a recognized stock exchange);
    - **(d)** A tax-exempt organization (pension fund or charitable entity);
    - **(e)** A tax-exempt organization described in Article 4(1)(c) (religious, charitable, etc.), provided more than half of its beneficiaries are residents of either State.
- **Ownership/Base Erosion Test:** A person not meeting the above criteria may still qualify if:
    - **(f)(i)** At least 50% of the person is owned by qualified persons (individuals, public companies, etc.); **and**
    - **(f)(ii)** Less than 50% of the person's gross income is paid out to persons who are not residents of either State (the "base erosion" test).
- **Active Trade or Business Test:** Even if not a qualified person, a resident may still get benefits for a specific item of income if it is engaged in the active conduct of a trade or business in its home State and the income is derived in connection with that business.
- **Discretionary Relief:** If a resident fails all the above tests, the competent authority may still grant benefits if it determines that the establishment or maintenance of the resident did not have as one of its principal purposes the obtaining of treaty benefits.

### Article 23: Relief from Double Taxation
- **Japan (Credit Method):** Japan allows a resident a credit against Japanese tax for the amount of U.S. tax payable on income derived from the U.S. (subject to limits). For dividends paid to a Japanese company owning at least 10% of the payer, Japan also allows an **indirect credit** (a credit for the underlying U.S. tax paid by the payer on its profits).
- **United States (Credit Method):** The U.S. allows a citizen or resident a credit against U.S. tax for Japanese tax paid. For a U.S. company receiving dividends from a Japanese company in which it owns at least 10% of the voting stock, the U.S. allows an **indirect credit** for the Japanese tax paid by the payer on its profits.
- **Special Rule for U.S. Citizens:** Where the U.S. taxes a U.S. citizen who is a resident of Japan, Japan must limit its credit to the amount of U.S. tax that would be imposed on a non-citizen resident of Japan. The U.S. then provides a credit for the remaining Japanese tax.

### Article 24: Non-Discrimination
- **Protection for Nationals:** Nationals of one State cannot be subjected to more burdensome taxation in the other State than nationals of that other State in the same circumstances.
- **Protection for Permanent Establishments:** The taxation of a PE in one State shall not be less favorable than the taxation of enterprises of that State carrying on the same activities.
- **Deductions:** Interest, royalties, and other disbursements paid by a resident of one State to a resident of the other shall be deductible under the same conditions as if paid to a resident of the first State (arm's length standards apply).
- **Owned Enterprises:** Enterprises of one State that are owned by residents of the other State shall not be subjected to more burdensome taxation than similar enterprises of the first State.
- **Scope:** These provisions apply to taxes of every kind and description, not just the income taxes covered by the treaty.

### Article 25: Mutual Agreement Procedure (MAP)
- **Taxpayer Remedy:** Where a person considers that the actions of one or both States result in taxation not in accordance with the treaty, they may present their case to the competent authority of their State of residence (or nationality) within **three years** of the first notification of the action.
- **Resolution:** The competent authority shall endeavor to resolve the case by mutual agreement with the competent authority of the other State to avoid taxation not in accordance with the treaty.
- **Implementation:** Any agreement reached shall be implemented notwithstanding any time limits or procedural limitations in the domestic law of the Contracting States.
- **General Consultation:** The competent authorities may also consult to resolve difficulties or doubts regarding the interpretation or application of the treaty, including attribution of income, characterization of items, and advance pricing arrangements.
