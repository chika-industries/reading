# Summary of Buffett's Eleventh Year (1967)

## The Performance: Another Strong Year, But...

The Dow gained 19.0% (including dividends). The Partnership gained **35.9%** before allocation to the general partner. Limited partners received **28.4%**.

Cumulative since 1957: Partnership up **1606.9%** vs. Dow up **165.3%**. Annual compounded rate for limited partners: **23.9%** over eleven years.

But Buffett was measured in his enthusiasm. He noted that 1967 was a year when "it rained gold" for many investors. "Probably a greater percentage of participants in the securities markets did substantially better than the Dow last year than in virtually any year in history." His guess: at least 95% of investment companies beat the Dow, many by substantial margins.

> "It was a year when profits achieved were in inverse proportion to age – and I am in the geriatric ward, philosophically."

## The October 9, 1967 Letter: A Major Shift

This is one of the most important letters Buffett ever wrote. He announced a fundamental change in his goals and approach.

**The old goal:** Ten percentage points per annum better than the Dow.

**The new goal:** "The lesser of 9% per annum or a five percentage point advantage over the Dow."

In other words: if the Dow averages -2% over five years, he'd hope for +3%. If the Dow averages +12%, he'd hope for only +9% (not +22%).

Why the change? Four factors:

**1. The market environment had changed.** Quantitative bargains had "virtually disappeared." He attributed this to twenty years of constant combing without an economic convulsion like the 1930s, the rise of takeover bids, and the exploding ranks of security analysts.

> "The result has been the virtual disappearance of the bargain issue as determined quantitatively – and thereby of our bread and butter."

**2. Hyperactive markets.** The intensified interest in investment performance had created "a hyper-reactive pattern of market behavior against which my analytical techniques have limited value."

He saw speculation on an increasing scale, with time horizons shrinking to quarterly, monthly, even more frequently. "The payoff for superior short-term performance has become enormous."

> "I will not abandon a previous approach whose logic I understand even though it may mean foregoing large and apparently easy profits, to embrace an approach which I don't fully understand, have not practiced successfully and which, possibly, could lead to substantial permanent loss of capital."

**3. Increased size.** With $65 million in assets, his capital was "something of a drag on performance" – though he believed it was the least significant factor.

**4. Personal motivation (the most important).**

> "When I started the partnership I set the motor that regulated the treadmill at 'ten points better than the Dow.' I was younger, poorer and probably more competitive."

Now, at 37, his personal circumstances had changed. He wanted to allow for "considerable non-economic activity." He gave an example: continuing to own a satisfactory but far-from-spectacular controlled business because he liked the people, even if alternative investments offered higher returns.

> "More money would be made buying businesses at attractive prices, then reselling them. However, it may be more enjoyable (particularly when the personal value of incremental capital is less) to continue to own them and hopefully improve their performance."

He ended with a plea for partners to consider whether they wanted to stay, given his reduced objectives. He would continue to keep virtually all his own capital in BPL.

## The Berkshire Hathaway and Diversified Retailing Update

Both controlled companies made important acquisitions in 1967. Diversified Retailing (80% owned) acquired Associated Cotton Shops. Berkshire Hathaway acquired National Indemnity Company and National Fire & Marine Insurance Company.

> "These acquisitions couldn't be more gratifying. Everything was as advertised or better."

The principal selling executives, Ben Rosner and Jack Ringwalt, "have continued to do a superb job (the only kind they know)."

But he was honest about Berkshire's textile operations: "I don't presently foresee any loss in underlying values. I similarly see no prospect of a good return on the assets employed in the textile business."

## The Workout Disaster of 1967

Workouts, which had been a reliable category, turned sour. On an average investment of $17.2 million, the overall gain was only **$153,273** – a return of 0.89%. He called it "the worst performance in history" for that category.

> "The streets were filled with upset applecarts – our applecarts – during 1967."

The lesson: even a reliable category can have terrible years, especially when only a few situations are held.

## How His Mind Works (1967 Edition)

**He distinguishes between quantitative and qualitative insights – and admits where his best gains came from:**

> "The really sensational ideas I have had over the years have been heavily weighted toward the qualitative side where I have had a 'high-probability insight.' This is what causes the cash register to really sing."

But he notes that such insights are rare. "The more sure money tends to be made on the obvious quantitative decisions." The problem: those quantitative bargains had dried up.

**He is willing to lower his goals rather than compromise his principles:**

He could have joined the speculative frenzy. He didn't. He reduced his targets instead. That takes intellectual integrity.

**He recognizes the conflict between short-term and long-term, and chooses long-term:**

Even though his new goals would make BPL less attractive to performance-chasing investors, he stated them clearly and gave partners an out.

**He values people and enjoyment over maximizing every last point:**

> "When I am dealing with people I like, in businesses I find stimulating (what business isn't?), and achieving worthwhile overall returns on capital employed (say, 10-12%), it seems foolish to rush from situation to situation to earn a few more percentage points."

This is the Buffett who would later hold Coca-Cola and GEICO for decades, not because they were the highest-returning possibilities every year, but because they were good businesses run by good people.

## The Jokes

The "geriatric ward" line about his age (37) is classic self-deprecation.

The "chrzaszcz" (Polish May bug) reappears – he clearly loved that one.

His line about the investment management business swinging "from excessive lethargy to acute hypertension" is a perfect diagnosis.

And the quote from the investment manager who said securities "must be studied in a minute-by-minute program" – Buffett's response: "Wow! This sort of stuff makes me feel guilty when I go out for a Pepsi."

## The Key Quote

> "I would rather sustain the penalties resulting from over-conservatism than face the consequences of error, perhaps with permanent capital loss, resulting from the adoption of a 'New Era' philosophy where trees really do grow to the sky."

He wrote that in 1959. He was still living by it in 1967. And he would keep living by it for the rest of his life.

But the quote that best captures the 1967 letter's essence:

> "I will not abandon a previous approach whose logic I understand even though it may mean foregoing large and apparently easy profits, to embrace an approach which I don't fully understand, have not practiced successfully and which, possibly, could lead to substantial permanent loss of capital."

That is Buffett's intellectual honesty on full display. He knew what he didn't know. And he refused to pretend otherwise, even when "easy profits" were available to those who did.
