# Analysis of the 1977 Berkshire Hathaway Letter

## The Performance: 19% Return on Equity

Operating earnings: **$21,904,000** or **$22.54 per share** – "moderately better than anticipated."

But Buffett immediately redirects attention to the metric that matters most to him: **return on beginning equity capital of 19%** – "slightly better than last year and above both our own long-term average and that of American industry in aggregate."

He takes a direct swipe at companies that tout "record earnings per share" without acknowledging the capital added:

> "Most companies define 'record' earnings as a new high in earnings per share. Since businesses customarily add from year to year to their equity base, we find nothing particularly noteworthy in a management performance combining, say, a 10% increase in equity capital and a 5% increase in earnings per share. After all, even a totally dormant savings account will produce steadily rising interest earnings each year because of compounding."

This is a classic Buffett insight. Growth in earnings per share that merely matches the growth in equity is not performance – it's just compounding at the same rate. Real performance means earning more on the new capital than the old capital earned.

**Outlook for 1978:** Beginning equity up 23%, insurance underwriting margins expected to turn down. Still, "reasonably good year" expected with per‑share operating earnings up somewhat.

## Textile Operations: A Poor Year, But They Stay

> "The textile business again had a very poor year in 1977. We have mistakenly predicted better results in each of the last two years. This may say something about our forecasting abilities, the nature of the textile industry, or both."

**The fundamental question from shareholders:** Why remain in textiles when returns are so low?

Buffett gives three reasons:

1. **Employment responsibility** – The mills are among the largest employers in New Bedford and Manchester, with an aging workforce possessing non‑transferable skills. The workers and unions have shown "unusual understanding and effort" to keep the operation viable.

2. **Historical contribution** – Ken Chace's efforts after 1965 generated capital from the textile division that financed the acquisition and expansion of the profitable insurance operation. Textiles paid for the engine.

3. **Hope for modest profits** – With hard work and imagination, "at least modest profits" should be achievable.

This is Buffett balancing economic reality with human commitment. He is not cold‑hearted. He will not liquidate a business that employs hundreds of loyal workers just to squeeze out a few more percentage points of return elsewhere.

## Insurance Underwriting: Tailwinds vs. Headwinds

**The growth story since 1967:**
- 1967: Bought National Indemnity for $8.6 million with $22 million premium volume
- 1977: Aggregate insurance premium volume: **$151 million**
- **No additional shares issued** to achieve this growth.

Buffett lists the expansion: new companies (Cornhusker, Lakeland, Texas United, Iowa, Kansas), cash purchases (Home & Auto, Kerkling Reinsurance, Cypress), and new products (reinsurance).

But he is brutally honest about mistakes:

> "Some major mistakes have been made during the decade, both in products and personnel."

**The four major mistakes:**
1. A surety operation initiated in 1969
2. The 1973 expansion of Home & Auto into Miami, Florida (disastrous, as previously disclosed)
3. An unresolved aviation "fronting" arrangement
4. California Worker's Compensation operation (still being reorganized)

Then comes a crucial observation:

> "It is comforting to be in a business where some mistakes can be made and yet a quite satisfactory overall performance can be achieved. In a sense, this is the opposite case from our textile business where even very good management probably can average only modest results."

**The key lesson:**

> "One of the lessons your management has learned – and, unfortunately, sometimes re-learned – is the importance of being in businesses where tailwinds prevail rather than headwinds."

This is the essence of Buffett's later emphasis on "economic moats." Some industries are just harder. Insurance, despite its cycles and occasional mistakes, has tailwinds that allow good managers to prosper. Textiles, even with great managers, has headwinds that make good results unlikely.

**The cycle in 1977:** Rate increases from 1976 finally flowed through fully. But costs (economic inflation + social inflation) rise at close to 1% per month. Rates are now slowing. Underwriting margins will decline by the second half of 1978.

**Phil Liesche's group:** Extraordinary performance. As competitors withdraw, National Indemnity gains volume and profits. But when the cycle turns, "unusual managerial discipline will be required" to let business go rather than write it at foolish prices.

**Reinsurance:** Combined ratio 107.1 – still unsatisfactory, but improving. Generates high investment funds.

**Home & Auto:** John Seward turned it from near‑death to "sound, profitable, and growing."

**Homestate operation:** Five companies, $23 million volume (up from $5.5 million three years ago). All four that operated full year had combined ratios below 100. Cornhusker led at 93.8. John Ringwalt continues to excel.

**Cypress Insurance Company** (Worker's Comp, California) – purchased at yearend 1977 for cash. Milt Thornton runs a "first‑class operation." Not combined with existing California operation; they will operate independently.

**The nature of insurance:**

> "Insurance companies offer standardized policies which can be copied by anyone. Their only products are promises. ... There are no important advantages from trademarks, patents, location, corporate longevity, raw material sources, etc., and very little consumer differentiation to produce insulation from competition."

Therefore, people make all the difference. And Buffett has an exceptional group.

## Insurance Investments: The $74 Million Unrealized Gain

Insurance investments grew from $134.6 million to $252.8 million in two years. Investment income improved from $8.4 million pre‑tax in 1975 to $12.3 million in 1977.

**Realized capital gains in 1977:** $6.9 million pre‑tax.

**Unrealized gain in stocks at yearend:** approximately **$74 million** (compared to a $17 million unrealized loss at the end of 1974).

But Buffett cautions:

> "This figure ... should not be taken too seriously. Most of our large stock positions are going to be held for many years and the scorecard on our investment decisions will be provided by business results over that period, and not by prices on any given day."

**The Berkshire history digression:** In 1948, Berkshire Fine Spinning and Hathaway Manufacturing (pre‑merger) had combined earnings of almost $18 million – more than IBM's $28 million? Actually, he says IBM was $28 million, so Berkshire was smaller but still a powerhouse. But in the decade after the 1955 merger, aggregate sales of $595 million produced an aggregate loss of $10 million. By 1964, net worth had shrunk from $53 million to $22 million.

> "So much for single year snapshots as adequate portrayals of a business."

**GEICO** is the largest position: convertible preferred + common = $23.5 million cost, $43.5 million market.

**Washington Post** cost $10.6 million, market $33.4 million – already a triple. (Held "permanently" as previously stated.)

**Capital Cities Communications** – new position purchased in 1977 for $10.9 million. Earnings attributable to those shares: about $1.3 million. But only the cash dividend ($40,000 annually) shows up in operating earnings. Buffett is buying a great business with extraordinary management, but the operating earnings don't reflect the retained earnings working for him. He is patient.

**The four criteria for equity investments** (restated, slightly refined):

1. One that we can understand
2. With favorable long-term prospects
3. Operated by honest and competent people
4. Available at a very attractive price

He adds:

> "We ordinarily make no attempt to buy equities for anticipated favorable stock price behavior in the short term. In fact, if their business experience continues to satisfy us, we welcome lower market prices of stocks we own as an opportunity to acquire even more of a good thing at a better price."

**The unorthodox view on control vs. non‑control:**

> "To purchase, directly, properties such as Capital Cities owns would cost in the area of twice our cost of purchase via the stock market, and direct ownership would offer no important advantages to us. ... We would not be able to provide management in either of those respects equal to that now in place. In effect, we can obtain a better management result through non-control than control. This is an unorthodox view, but one we believe to be sound."

This is Buffett at his best. He doesn't need to control a business to benefit from its success. He can buy a small piece of an outstanding business run by outstanding people at a discount to what it would cost to buy the whole thing. And he gets their management for free.

## Banking: Gene Abegg's Succession

Illinois National Bank continues to earn about **three times the rate of most large banks** – while paying maximum rates to savers and maintaining exceptional liquidity and loan quality.

The history: Gene Abegg started the bank in 1931 with $250,000. No new capital ever contributed. Since Berkshire's purchase in 1969, the bank has paid **$20 million in dividends** – more than the purchase price many times over. 1977 earnings: $3.6 million.

> "Late last year Gene, now 80 and still running a banking operation without peer, asked that a successor be brought in."

Peter Jeffrey (formerly CEO of American National Bank of Omaha) joined as President and CEO. Gene continues as Chairman. Buffett expects continued success.

## Blue Chip Stamps: 36.5% Ownership and See's Candy

Berkshire owned about **36.5%** of Blue Chip Stamps at yearend 1977. Blue Chip had a fine year: $12.9 million operating earnings, plus $4.1 million realized securities gains.

**See's Candy** (99% owned by Blue Chip) continued its extraordinary run:

> "Since See's was purchased by Blue Chip Stamps at the beginning of 1972, pre‑tax operating earnings have grown from $4.2 million to $12.6 million with little additional capital investment. See's achieved this record while operating in an industry experiencing practically no unit growth."

That is the magic of a wonderful business with pricing power. No unit growth, but earnings tripled. See's would become a cornerstone of Buffett's thinking about "economic goodwill" and the ability to raise prices without losing customers.

**Wesco Financial** (80% owned by Blue Chip), managed by Louis Vincenti, also made good progress.

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1977 Edition)

**1. He measures management by return on equity, not earnings per share growth.**
The savings account analogy devastates the common corporate practice of touting "record earnings" without adjusting for capital added.

**2. He is willing to stay in a bad business for human reasons.**
Textiles are a headwind business, but he won't abandon the workers. This is not pure economic maximization – it's values‑driven capitalism.

**3. He distinguishes between industries with tailwinds and headwinds.**
Insurance allows mistakes and still produces good results. Textiles, even with great management, barely gets by. He wants to own businesses that are forgiving.

**4. He welcomes lower prices on businesses he likes.**
> "We welcome lower market prices of stocks we own as an opportunity to acquire even more of a good thing at a better price."

Most investors panic when prices fall. Buffett gets excited.

**5. He sees non‑control ownership as often superior to control.**
Capital Cities is the example. He gets a great management team for free, pays half the price of buying the whole company, and has no operational headaches. This is a profound insight.

**6. He obsesses about the difference between operating earnings and economic reality.**
The Capital Cities dividend is only $40,000, but his share of their earnings is $1.3 million. The retained earnings are building value that will eventually be reflected in market price and dividends. He is willing to wait.

**7. He loves businesses that grow without capital.**
See's Candy: earnings tripled with "little additional capital investment." That is the holy grail.

## The Best Lines

> "Even a totally dormant savings account will produce steadily rising interest earnings each year because of compounding."

A devastatingly simple refutation of "record earnings per share" hype.

> "One of the lessons your management has learned – and, unfortunately, sometimes re-learned – is the importance of being in businesses where tailwinds prevail rather than headwinds."

This should be printed on every business school wall.

> "We welcome lower market prices of stocks we own as an opportunity to acquire even more of a good thing at a better price."

The ultimate expression of the long‑term value investor's mindset.

> "In effect, we can obtain a better management result through non-control than control. This is an unorthodox view, but one we believe to be sound."

Buffett is humble enough to know that he cannot improve on Thomas Murphy at Capital Cities or Kay Graham at the Washington Post. So he buys a piece and gets out of the way.

## The Key Quote

> "We select our marketable equity securities in much the same way we would evaluate a business for acquisition in its entirety. We want the business to be (1) one that we can understand, (2) with favorable long-term prospects, (3) operated by honest and competent people, and (4) available at a very attractive price."

This is the Buffett investment philosophy, now fully mature. It applies whether he is buying 100% of a private company or a sliver of a public one. The analysis is identical. The only difference is the size of the piece.

But the quote that captures the letter's most important lesson might be:

> "It is comforting to be in a business where some mistakes can be made and yet a quite satisfactory overall performance can be achieved."

This is the definition of a great business: one that is forgiving. It can survive mediocre decisions and still produce good results. A bad business punishes every mistake.

## Transition Note

1977 was the year Buffett articulated his investment philosophy in its mature form. The four criteria, the tailwinds/headwinds distinction, the preference for non‑control ownership of wonderful businesses, the See's Candy triumph (through Blue Chip) – all of it was there.

The GEICO position was large and growing. The Washington Post had already tripled. Capital Cities was a new addition. And the textile business, while still a drag, was being managed for cash flow and social responsibility, not growth.

Buffett was no longer just a stock picker. He was a business analyst, a capital allocator, and a philosopher of corporate management. The letters from this period are essential reading for anyone who wants to understand not just what he did, but why.
