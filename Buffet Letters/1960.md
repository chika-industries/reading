# Summary of Buffett's Fourth Year (1960)

## The Market Picture: A Complete Reversal

In 1959, the Dow had advanced while most stocks declined. In 1960, the opposite happened. The Dow fell from 679 to 616—a loss of 9.3% (6.3% after adding back dividends). But the Utility Average gained, and Buffett guessed that about 90% of all investment companies *outperformed* the Industrial Average. Most ended the year in the range of plus or minus 5%.

On the NYSE, 653 stocks lost value while only 404 gained.

## The Results

Seven partnerships operated the full year. The overall gain: **22.8%** versus a loss of **6.3%** for the Dow. This was exactly the kind of year Buffett had been promising—declining or static market where his method could shine.

Cumulative results since 1957:

| Year | Partnership Gain | Dow Gain |
|------|------------------|----------|
| 1957 | 10.4% | -8.4% |
| 1957-58 | 55.6% | 26.9% |
| 1957-59 | 95.9% | 52.2% |
| 1957-60 | 140.6% | 42.6% |

He added a crucial caveat: four years is "entirely too short a period from which to make deductions."

## The Philosophy of Par (Golf as Metaphor)

This letter contains one of Buffett's clearest explanations of how he measures success:

> "I would consider a year in which we declined 15% and the Average 30% to be much superior to a year when both we and the Average advanced 20%."

Then the golf analogy:

> "The important thing is to be beating par; a four on a par three hole is not as good as a five on a par five hole and it is unrealistic to assume we are not going to have our share of both par three's and par five's."

He was dispensing this "dose of philosophy" because he had new partners. He wanted them to understand his objectives, his measure of attainment, and his known limitations—before they experienced a down year.

## The Sanborn Map Story (A Masterpiece of Control Investing)

This is the longest and most detailed narrative in any letter so far. It shows Buffett's mind at full throttle.

**The business:**
Sanborn Map Co. published extremely detailed maps of every US city—showing water main diameters, fire hydrant locations, roof composition, etc. For 75 years, it had a near-monopoly selling to fire insurance companies. The business was recession-proof and required no sales effort.

**The problem:**
In the early 1950s, a competitive method called "carding" ate into Sanborn's business. After-tax profits fell from over $500,000 annually in the late 1930s to under $100,000 in 1958-59. The operating business was dying.

**The strange twist:**
During the 1930s, Sanborn had started accumulating an investment portfolio. By 1958, it had about $2.5 million—half in bonds, half in stocks. The portfolio blossomed while the map business wilted.

**The absurd valuation:**
- 1938: Dow at 100-120, Sanborn stock at $110 per share. Investment portfolio value: about $20 per share. Implied valuation of the map business: **$90 per share**.
- 1958: Dow at 550, Sanborn stock at **$45 per share**. Investment portfolio value: about $65 per share. Implied valuation of the map business: **minus $20 per share**.

> "In the tremendously more vigorous climate of 1958 the same map business was evaluated at a minus $20 with the buyer of the stock unwilling to pay more than 70 cents on the dollar for the investment portfolio with the map business thrown in for nothing."

**The boardroom dysfunction:**
Of 14 directors, nine were prominent insurance men who combined owned **46 shares** out of 105,000 outstanding. The largest holding among them was ten shares. For the past decade, the insurance companies had been *only sellers* of Sanborn stock.

The company attorney held ten shares. A banker held ten shares (he saw the problem). Two top officers held about 300 shares combined. And a widow—the son of a deceased president—held about 15,000 shares.

**The opportunity:**
The son, unhappy with the business, demanded the top job, was turned down, resigned. Buffett made a bid to his mother for her block and bought it. Then, with associates, he accumulated about 24,000 shares. The three large holdings (his group plus two others) totaled 46,000 shares.

**The solution:**
Rather than a proxy fight, Buffett engineered a plan where 72% of stockholders (representing 50% of the 1,600 shareholders) exchanged their shares for portfolio securities at fair value. The map business was left with over $1.25 million in bonds as a reserve. A potential capital gains tax of over $1 million was eliminated. Remaining stockholders got higher asset value, higher earnings per share, and an increased dividend.

## How His Mind Works (1960 Edition)

**He thinks in terms of "value to a private owner" vs. "market price":**
The Sanborn story is the purest example yet. The market was valuing the map business at *negative* $20 per share while the investment portfolio alone was worth $65. That's not a mispricing—it's an absurdity.

**He understands that control unlocks value:**
Without control, he couldn't force the separation of the investment portfolio from the map business. With control (or a large enough block to influence), he could.

**He's willing to be patient—but also act decisively:**
He'd been watching Sanborn for years. When the opportunity to buy the widow's block appeared, he moved. When the plan was ready, he executed.

**He sees the human dimension clearly:**
The nine insurance directors owned almost nothing. They had no skin in the game. Their interests were not aligned with shareholders. Buffett spotted this immediately.

**He's not trying to be a hero:**
> "Such control situations may occur very infrequently. Our bread-and-butter business is buying undervalued securities and selling when the undervaluation is corrected."

Control is the exception, not the rule. But when it appears, it can be transformative.

## The Joke

The line about the map business being valued at "minus $20" is darkly funny. He's not angry—he's almost delighted by the absurdity. A business that had earned money for 75 years, with irreplaceable data worth tens of millions to reproduce, was being priced as a liability.

Also, his aside about the widow's son: "demanded the top position, was turned down, and submitted his resignation, which was accepted." Buffett doesn't editorialize, but the deadpan recitation tells you everything.

## The Key Quote

> "How could this come about?"

He asks the question, then answers it with a detailed anatomy of boardroom failure, misaligned incentives, and market stupidity. That question—*how could this come about?*—is the engine of his investing. He's constantly looking for situations where the answer reveals an opportunity.
