# Summary of Buffett's Final Letter to Partners (February 25, 1970)

## Context: The Partnership Is Over

This is not a performance letter. The partnership has been dissolved. The cash distributions (64–70% of January 1, 1969 capital) have been made, and partners have received shares of Diversified Retailing and Berkshire Hathaway. Now Buffett is offering one last service: helping partners invest their cash in tax‑exempt bonds.

He is clear about the limits: he will be available in March 1970, after that he is done.

> "I have no desire to be in the investment counseling business, directly or indirectly, and will not be available for discussion of financial matters after March 31st."

## The Mechanics of Tax‑Free Bonds

Buffett wrote a mini‑textbook on municipal bonds, showing his characteristic clarity and attention to practical detail.

**Bearer vs. registered bonds:** He recommends bearer form (coupons attached) because they trade easily. Registered bonds require conversion to sell.

**Custody accounts:** For a nominal fee ($200/year on $250,000), a bank will clip coupons and keep records.

## Taxation

The interest is free from federal income tax. For a taxpayer in a 30% bracket, a 6% tax‑free yield equals ~8.5% taxable.

**State taxes:** Nebraska has no state tax on any tax‑free bonds. New York and California tax out‑of‑state bonds but exempt in‑state issues. He defers to local tax advisors.

**Important warning:** Do not own tax‑free bonds if you have a general bank loan. The IRS can disallow interest deductions on loans used to carry tax‑exempts. "I would pay off bank loans before owning tax‑free bonds."

## Marketability: The Hidden Trap

Unlike stocks, there are hundreds of thousands of municipal issues. Many have only a handful of holders.

> "You can't set forth in the morning to buy a specific Grand Island issue of your choosing. It may not be offered at any price, anywhere."

The spread between bid and asked can reach 15% for illiquid issues. Buffett promises to avoid those. He will stick to large, marketable issues where spreads are 2–5% – acceptable for a long‑term investor.

## Specific Areas of Purchase (Buffett's Comfort Zone)

**1. Large revenue‑producing public entities** (toll roads, electric districts, water districts). High marketability, quantifiable, sometimes with favorable sinking funds.

**2. Industrial Development Authority bonds** – where a public entity holds title to a project leased to a top‑grade corporation (e.g., U.S. Steel). The credit is the corporation's, not the city's. Buffett's insurance company owned mostly these.

**3. Public Housing Authority issues** – effectively guaranteed by the U.S. government (AAA). For partners who must buy in‑state bonds, these are his default choice.

**4. State obligations** – but he explicitly avoids large cities (New York, Chicago, Philadelphia).

> "I don't have the faintest idea how to analyze a New York City ... Passing an opportunity which I don't understand – even if someone else is perceptive enough to analyze it – doesn't bother me."

## Callable Bonds: An Outrageous Contract

This section is classic Buffett – he is outraged by an unfair provision that most investors ignore.

Many bonds give the issuer the right to call (retire) the bond after 5–10 years at a small premium, even though the bond is due in 40 years. This means:

- If interest rates fall (good for the bondholder), the issuer calls the bond and the investor loses.
- If interest rates rise (bad for the bondholder), the issuer does nothing and the investor suffers.

> "Such contracts are really outrageous and exist because bond investors can't think through the implications."

He will buy only non‑callable bonds, or discount bonds where the call price is so far above cost that the call is irrelevant.

## Maturity and the Mathematics of Bonds

Buffett explains bond price math with characteristic simplicity:

- If interest rates fall from 5% to 3%, a 20‑year bond rises to ~$1,288; a 2‑year bond rises only to ~$1,020.
- If rates rise to 7%, the 20‑year bond falls to ~$792; the short bond falls to ~$981.

> "The wider the swings in interest rates and the longer the bond, the more the value can go up or down on an interim basis."

His conclusion: with the yield curve strongly positive (long bonds paying much more than short bonds), he recommends ten‑ to twenty‑five‑year maturities – but only for investors who can tolerate price fluctuations.

## Discount vs. Full Coupon Bonds

A bond purchased at a discount (e.g., $791.60 for a 5% coupon when new bonds yield 7%) gives tax‑free interest plus a capital gain at maturity. A full coupon bond (7% at par) gives only tax‑free interest.

The discount bond's capital gain is taxable. But the market usually over‑compensates for this tax, making discount bonds more attractive after‑tax – especially for individuals not in the highest brackets.

Banks have been forced out of the discount bond market by the 1969 Tax Reform Act, creating an opportunity for individuals.

> "If I can get a significantly higher effective after‑tax yield, I intend to purchase discount bonds for you."

But he will buy full coupon bonds for partners who prefer current cash yield.

## How His Mind Works (1970 Edition)

**He teaches, not just advises.**

This letter is a tutorial. He wants partners to understand bonds well enough to make their own decisions – not to blindly follow him.

**He stays within his circle of competence.**

He refuses to analyze New York City bonds because he "doesn't have the faintest idea." He sticks to what he knows: revenue bonds, industrial development bonds, and Housing Authorities.

**He is outraged by unfair contracts.**

The callable bond section is pure Buffett – he sees a provision that loads the dice against the investor, and he cannot believe that otherwise intelligent people accept it. He explains it so clearly that no one reading this letter could ever buy a callable bond again.

**He thinks mathematically about yield curves and optionality.**

He calculates the trade‑offs between short and long maturities, between discount and full coupon bonds, between current yield and future capital gains. Every decision is quantified.

**He is frugal even when advising others.**

He notes that his office expenses have barely risen. He's proud of that. He wouldn't sign a percentage lease. Frugality is a habit, not a necessity.

## The Jokes

The line about "Grand Island, Nebraska" having 75 issues outstanding – a gentle poke at obscure local bonds.

His aside about "I became an authority on the Minimum Wage Act" from his Penney's days (repeated from 1966) – self‑deprecating.

The best: After explaining the mathematics of bond price changes, he adds: "Of course, if you hold the bond straight through, you are going to get the contracted rate of interest" – the patient investor is fine.

## The Key Quote

> "I don't have the faintest idea how to analyze a New York City ... Passing an opportunity which I don't understand – even if someone else is perceptive enough to analyze it – doesn't bother me."

This is the same man who walked away from the partnership because the environment had changed. He knows what he knows, and he knows what he doesn't know. He doesn't need to win every game – only the ones he understands.

---

## Epilogue: The Partnership's Legacy

From $105,100 in May 1956 to over $100 million at dissolution. From 7 limited partners to dozens. From a bedroom office to 810 Kiewit Plaza. From 10.4% in the first year to 58.8% in the last full year.

But the real legacy is not the money. It's the method: buy value, ignore the crowd, concentrate when you're sure, be patient, be honest, and when the game changes – know when to stop.

Buffett would go on to transform Berkshire Hathaway into one of the greatest wealth‑creation machines in history. But the seeds were all planted in these letters.
