# Analysis of the 1971 Berkshire Hathaway Letter (March 13, 1972)

## A New Era: Buffett as Chairman

This is Warren Buffett's first letter to Berkshire shareholders as Chairman of the Board. The previous year's letter was signed by President Ken Chace. Now Buffett takes the pen directly. The voice is unmistakably his – clear, honest, wry, and focused on return on equity.

The headline: operating earnings (excluding capital gains) were **more than 14% of beginning shareholders' equity** – "considerably above the average of American industry."

He immediately notes that this was achieved "in the face of inadequate earnings in our textile operation," proving the wisdom of the capital redeployment strategy begun five years earlier.

But he adds a note of caution:

> "It should be realized that merely maintaining the present relatively high rate of return may well prove more difficult than was improvement from the very low levels of return which prevailed throughout most of the 1960's."

## Textile Operations: Swimming Against the Tide

The textile business continued to struggle. Inadequate gross margins, price‑sensitive fabrics, and a tough industry environment. Buffet is blunt:

> "Strong efforts to hammer down costs and a continuous search for less price‑sensitive fabrics produced only marginal profits. However, without these efforts we would have operated substantially in the red."

He praises Ken Chace and his management group for "swimming against a strong industry tide." A mild industry pickup was expected in 1972, but profitability would not be "of a dramatic nature."

Buffett's approach to textiles is consistent: run it for cash flow, don't invest heavily in expansion, and don't liquidate it just to squeeze out a few more points. The human cost matters.

## Insurance Operations: The Star, But With Warnings

The property and casualty insurance industry had an "extraordinarily good year" due to three factors: reduced auto accident frequency, sharply higher effective rates, and no major catastrophes.

But Buffett immediately flags the negative:

> "These benefits ... are not without their negative connotations."

**The cycle:** When industry underwriting is unprofitable, standard markets tighten, and producers flock to National Indemnity for non‑standard policies. That drove volume surges in 1970 and 1971. Now that underwriting is profitable, competitors are returning and cutting rates.

> "We continue to have underwriting profitability as our primary goal and this may well mean a substantial decrease in National Indemnity's direct volume during 1972."

He will not chase volume. He will shrink rather than sacrifice underwriting discipline.

**Reinsurance:** Built in just two years by George Young. Same story: rates are being cut, new competitors are forming. Buffett expects business to be "curtailed somewhat" in 1972.

> "We set no volume goals in our insurance business generally – and certainly not in reinsurance – as virtually any volume can be achieved if profitability standards are ignored."

The key: "When catastrophes occur and underwriting experience sours, we plan to have the resources available to handle the increasing volume which we will then expect to be available at proper prices." He is patient. He waits for the cycle to turn.

**The "home‑state" insurance operation:** Cornhusker Casualty (Nebraska) launched in 1970. Lakeland Fire & Casualty (Minnesota) in 1971. Texas United Insurance in 1972. These are small – about $1.5 million premium volume in 1971 – but expected to more than double in 1972. John Ringwalt (Jack's son? brother? – actually John Ringwalt, Jack's son) is in charge. Buffett praises his "hard work, imagination and intelligence."

**The Home & Automobile Insurance Company acquisition:** A highlight of 1971. Based in Chicago, writing about $7.5 million in premium volume. Run by Victor Raab, whom Buffett describes as "cut from the same cloth as Jack Ringwalt and Gene Abegg."

> "These three men have built their companies from scratch and, after selling their ownership position for cash, retain every bit of the proprietary interest and pride that they have always had."

Buffett loves founders who stay hungry after getting rich. He adds capital to Home & Auto to expand into other densely populated areas.

**Overall insurance outlook:** Volume from Home & Auto and the home‑state companies may offset declines in National Indemnity. But the investment income from the funds accumulated in 1970-1971 (when rates were high) will continue to benefit earnings. So 1972 looks "reasonably good."

## Banking Operations: Illinois National Bank

Gene Abegg's bank continued to lead the industry in earnings as a percentage of deposits. Buffett lists the impressive stats:

- Earned **over 2% after tax on average deposits**
- No borrowed funds (except occasional reserve balancing)
- Liquidity position far above average
- Loan losses far below average
- Over 50% time deposits, with consumer savings accounts receiving maximum permitted interest

> "This reflects a superb management job by Gene Abegg and Bob Kline."

The challenge: interest rates were down substantially in 1971, and the bank's deposit mix has shifted from low‑cost demand deposits to more expensive time deposits. Interest paid on deposits went from under $1.7 million in 1969 to over $2.7 million in 1971.

> "With interest rates even lower now than in 1971, the banking industry is going to have trouble achieving gains in earnings during 1972."

He expects Illinois National to need "very close cost control" just to maintain 1971 earnings.

## Financial Strategy: Strong Financing

Because of volume gains early in 1971, Berkshire recast its bank loan to provide additional capital to the insurance subsidiaries. This proved "particularly propitious" when the Home & Auto opportunity arose later in the year.

> "Our insurance and banking subsidiaries possess a fiduciary relationship with the public. We retain a fundamental belief in operating from a very strongly financed position so as to be in a position to unquestionably fulfill our responsibilities."

This is Buffett's obsession with financial strength. No leverage that could endanger policyholders or depositors.

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1972 Edition)

**1. He thinks in terms of return on equity, not earnings growth.**
14% on beginning equity is the headline. Not "earnings were up X%." He cares about efficiency of capital.

**2. He understands insurance cycles and is willing to shrink.**
Most managers would boast about volume growth. Buffett says volume may decrease in 1972 because he won't cut prices to keep it. He waits for the cycle to turn – then he'll have the capital to write business when competitors are retreating.

**3. He loves founder‑managers who retain their fire after selling.**
Jack Ringwalt, Gene Abegg, Victor Raab – all built their businesses from nothing, sold for cash, and still work with "proprietary interest and pride." Buffett knows that a manager with that mentality is worth more than any spreadsheet.

**4. He is brutally honest about textile problems.**
No sugarcoating. "Inadequate gross margins," "only marginal profits," "substantially in the red" without cost cutting. But he also won't just shut it down. He balances economic reality with human responsibility.

**5. He manages for the long term, not the quarter.**
> "When catastrophes occur and underwriting experience sours, we plan to have the resources available to handle the increasing volume which we will then expect to be available at proper prices."

He is preparing for the next down cycle – not celebrating the current up cycle.

**6. He is transparent about risks and challenges.**
Lower interest rates, time deposit mix, banking industry headwinds, textile recession – he names them all. No rose‑colored glasses.

## The Jokes (Pretty Dry, But Present)

The letter is more formal than the partnership letters, but Buffett's voice sneaks through:

- "It is a pleasure to report..." – understated, almost British.
- The description of textile efforts as "swimming against a strong industry tide" – an image, not a laugh line.
- "All in all, it is questionable whether volume added by Home & Auto, plus the 'home‑state' business in 1972, will offset possible declines" – classic Buffett: he's not trying to spin a growth story; he's telling you it might be a wash.

The best line is actually an anti‑joke: "We set no volume goals in our insurance business generally – and certainly not in reinsurance – as virtually any volume can be achieved if profitability standards are ignored." That's a quiet dagger at competitors who chase market share at any cost.

## The Key Quote

> "When catastrophes occur and underwriting experience sours, we plan to have the resources available to handle the increasing volume which we will then expect to be available at proper prices."

This is the essence of Buffett's insurance strategy: be disciplined when times are good, so you can be aggressive when times are bad. The competition retreats; you advance. Patience and financial strength allow you to buy when others are selling.

But the quote that best captures his philosophy in this letter might be:

> "We set no volume goals ... as virtually any volume can be achieved if profitability standards are ignored."

He knows what he will not do. That is more important than knowing what he will do.

## Transition from the Partnership Era

This letter is different from the partnership letters. There are no jokes about Polish May bugs, no historical anecdotes about Queen Isabella, no long asides about the Manhattan Indians. Buffett is now writing as a corporate chairman, not a money manager wooing limited partners.

But the substance is the same: return on capital, underwriting discipline, founder‑managers, financial strength, and the patience to let the cycle work for you.

The partnership letters were about picking stocks and controlling situations. The Berkshire letters are about building a permanent machine – a collection of businesses that generate cash, reinvest it wisely, and compound over decades. The scale is larger, but the mind is the same.
