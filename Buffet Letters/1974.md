# Analysis of the 1974 Berkshire Hathaway Letter

## The Performance: A Bad Year

Operating earnings: **$8,383,576** or **$8.56 per share** – a **10.3% return on beginning shareholders' equity**. This is the lowest return since 1970.

Buffett is blunt:

> "Operating results for 1974 overall were unsatisfactory due to the poor performance of our insurance business."

He had predicted a decline in the 1973 letter, but "the extent of this decline, which accelerated during the year, was a surprise."

The textile division and the bank both performed "very well" – improved results over 1973. But insurance underwriting "turned dramatically worse as the year progressed."

## The Outlook for 1975: Not Encouraging

Buffett lays out expectations with characteristic honesty:

- Textile operations: "sharply negative comparisons"
- Banking earnings: "probably a moderate decline"
- Insurance underwriting: "a large question mark" – certainly not satisfactory, "could be an extremely poor one"

The only bright spots: insurance investment income and equity in Blue Chip Stamps earnings should improve.

> "During this period we plan to continue to build financial strength and liquidity, preparing for the time when insurance rates become adequate and we can once again aggressively pursue opportunities for growth in this area."

He is not panicking. He is preparing.

## Textile Operations: Running at One-Third Capacity

The first nine months of 1974 were strong. Then the fourth quarter brought "significant weaknesses" that continued into 1975.

> "We currently are operating at about one-third of capacity. Obviously, at such levels operating losses must result."

He adjusts operations downward to avoid building inventory. The products (curtain goods) are "high on the list of deferrable purchases" when consumers are uncertain. Housing starts are low. Retailers are cutting inventories.

> "These negative trends should reverse in due course, and we are attempting to minimize losses until that time comes."

He is not closing the textile business. He is riding out the cycle.

## Insurance Underwriting: The Perfect Storm

This section is Buffett's most detailed and sobering analysis yet. He identifies four converging problems:

**1. Unintelligent competition.** The unusual profitability of recent years attracted competitors who cut rates to "inadequate" levels.

**2. Underreserving.** Many insurers are "guilty of significant underreserving of losses" – meaning they don't know their true costs. They think they're profitable when they're not.

**3. Rapid inflation.** The costs of what insurers pay (auto repair, medical payments, compensation benefits) are increasing at "about 1% per month." That's 12%+ annually.

> "With costs moving forward rapidly and prices remaining unchanged, it was not hard to predict what would happen to profit margins."

**4. Stagnant premiums.** Best's estimates that auto insurance premiums increased only about 2% in 1974 – woefully inadequate against double-digit cost inflation.

The result: profit margins turned "sharply negative."

### The Grim Prognosis

> "As this report is being written, such deterioration continues. Loss reserves for many giant companies still appear to be understated by significant amounts, which means that these competitors continue to underestimate their true costs."

He believes insurers must experience "even more devastating underwriting results" before they take appropriate pricing action. He is waiting for the pain to become unbearable for others.

### The Direct Business (National Indemnity)

An underwriting loss of approximately 4% – after several years of high profitability. Volume increased somewhat, but Buffett is "not encouraging such increases until rates are more adequate."

> "At some point in the cycle, after major insurance companies have had their fill of red ink, history indicates that we will experience an inflow of business at compensatory rates."

Phil Liesche (who succeeded Jack Ringwalt) is "a most able underwriter" running a "highly profit-oriented" operation.

### The Reinsurance Business

"Intense competition" has produced "major losses for practically every company." Berkshire's underwriting loss was "something over 12%" – "a horrendous figure, but probably little different from the average."

Worse: there was no "super disaster" to blame. "Rather, a condition of inadequate rates prevails."

George Young (reinsurance manager) has cancelled "a great many contracts where prices are totally inadequate" and is making no attempt to increase volume except where prices are right. Based on present rates, "it seems highly unlikely that the reinsurance industry generally, or we, specifically, will have a profitable year in 1975."

### The Home-State Companies (Good Progress)

Under John Ringwalt, these made "good progress." Developing a sound agency group. Expense ratios still too high but will come down with scale. The Texas problem from last year "seems to be improving." Buffett calls this "one of our most promising areas for the future."

### The Florida Disaster (A Rare Mistake)

Buffett admits a major error. Home & Automobile's expansion into Florida "proved disastrous." The underwriting loss: **over $2 million**.

> "We can't blame external insurance industry conditions for this mistake. In retrospect, it is apparent that our management simply did not have the underwriting information and the pricing knowledge necessary to be operating in the area."

They dropped out of Florida in mid-1974, but losses continued due to the term nature of contracts.

Even in Cook County (Home & Auto's traditional market), rates proved inadequate. They raised rates, but competitors didn't follow, so volume dropped.

### Buffett Still Likes Insurance

Despite the brutal year:

> "We consider the insurance business to be inherently attractive. Our overall return on capital employed in this area – even including the poor results of 1974 – remains high."

He emphasizes that they have been "realistic in the calculation of loss and administrative expense" – unlike competitors who underreserve.

## Banking Operations

Illinois National Bank had "excellent results." Earnings were "slightly better than last year" – a record. The bank maintained its position as "one of the highest earning banks in the country" relative to deposits.

The challenges: consumer time deposits (expensive) and taxable income due to loss of tax-exempt bonds. But the bank kept "extraordinary liquidity" and loan losses "averaged less than 5% of normal charge-offs."

Outlook for 1975: down somewhat due to money market conditions, but still favorable compared to practically any bank.

## Blue Chip Stamps: Increased Holdings

Berkshire increased its holdings to approximately **25.5%** of Blue Chip Stamps' outstanding shares.

Stamp sales continue at "greatly reduced levels" but management has done "an excellent job of adjusting operating costs." See's Candy Shops had "an outstanding year" with "excellent prospects."

Buffett expects Blue Chip to be "a source of continued substantial earning power."

## Merger with Diversified Retailing: Terminated

The proposed merger was terminated by the respective Boards on January 28, 1975. But Buffett adds:

> "We continue to view such a merger as eventually desirable, and hope to reopen the subject at some future time."

(It would eventually happen.)

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1974 Edition)

**1. He takes the blame for mistakes.**
The Florida disaster: "We can't blame external conditions. Our management simply did not have the underwriting information and pricing knowledge." No excuses. Just owned it.

**2. He sees the cycle clearly and waits.**
He knows that inadequate rates will eventually drive competitors out. He is building financial strength and liquidity now so he can "aggressively pursue opportunities for growth" when the cycle turns.

**3. He is willing to shrink.**
Volume increased at National Indemnity – but he's not encouraging it. He'd rather lose volume than lose money on underpriced business.

**4. He distinguishes between temporary problems and structural ones.**
Textiles are cyclical; they'll recover. Insurance pricing will eventually correct. The Florida expansion was just a bad decision – they exited.

**5. He values realistic accounting above all.**
Competitors underreserve and don't know their true costs. Berkshire does. That gives them a huge advantage when the reckoning comes.

**6. He is not depressed by a bad year.**
He states the facts. He explains the causes. He outlines the plan. He does not whine. He is冷静 and focused on the long term.

## The Best Lines

> "With costs moving forward rapidly and prices remaining unchanged, it was not hard to predict what would happen to profit margins."

A simple sentence that explains an industry-wide disaster.

> "We can't blame external insurance industry conditions for this mistake."

Rare and admirable honesty from a CEO.

> "During this period we plan to continue to build financial strength and liquidity, preparing for the time when insurance rates become adequate and we can once again aggressively pursue opportunities for growth."

This is Buffett's superpower: patience. He doesn't need to act now. He'll wait.

## The Key Quote

> "At some point in the cycle, after major insurance companies have had their fill of red ink, history indicates that we will experience an inflow of business at compensatory rates."

He has seen this before. He will see it again. He is not betting on a turnaround next quarter – he is betting on the math of the cycle. Competitors will eventually bleed enough to raise prices. When they do, Berkshire will be ready.

But the quote that best captures the letter's somber but resolute tone:

> "We have made every effort to be realistic in the calculation of loss and administrative expense."

In a year when others were kidding themselves, Buffett was counting the real cost. That discipline is the foundation of everything.

## Transition Note

1974 was the worst year since Buffett took control of Berkshire. Insurance underwriting collapsed. The Florida expansion failed. Textiles were in a deep slump. But Buffett did not panic. He did not change his philosophy. He did not chase bad business to pump up volume. He tightened up, took his lumps, and prepared for the next upswing.

This is where the legend was forged – not in the good years, but in the bad ones. Any manager can look smart in a bull market. Buffett proved his mettle when the tide went out.
