# Summary of Buffett's Seventh Year (1963)

## The Performance: Another Strong Year

The Dow gained 20.7% (including dividends). The Partnership gained **38.7%** before allocation to the general partner. Limited partners received **30.5%**.

Cumulative since 1957: Partnership up **454.5%** vs. Dow up **95.1%**. The annual compounded rate for limited partners: **22.3%** over seven years.

Buffett was quick to add perspective: "Our 17.7 margin over the Dow shown above is unattainable over any long period of time. A ten percentage point advantage would be a very satisfactory accomplishment."

## The Joys of Compounding (Mona Lisa Edition)

Buffett continued his tradition of using historical examples to teach compounding. This time: Leonardo da Vinci's *Mona Lisa*.

> "Francis I of France paid 4,000 ecus in 1540 for Leonardo da Vinci's Mona Lisa. On the off chance that a few of you have not kept track of the fluctuations of the ecu, 4,000 converted out to about $20,000."

If Francis had instead found a 6% after-tax investment, his estate would now be worth "something over $1,000,000,000,000,000.00" – a quadrillion dollars, or over 3,000 times the present national debt.

> "I trust this will end all discussion in our household about any purchase of paintings qualifying as an investment."

He then showed a table of compounding $100,000 at 4%, 8%, 12%, and 16% over 10, 20, and 30 years. The lesson: "a variation of merely a few percentage points has an enormous effect on the success of a compounding program."

## Dempster Mill: The Conclusion

The control situation that began in 1956 and turned around by Harry Bottle in 1962 reached its finale in 1963.

> "A satisfactory sale on a going concern basis of Dempster Mill Manufacturing operating assets was made about a month ago."

Buffett wrote: "Harry accounted for several extra servings of dessert by his extraordinary job." And in a nice touch: "Harry, incidentally, has made an advance payment toward becoming a limited partner in 1964—we consider this the beginning, not the end."

The Dempster saga, as detailed in the appendix, showed the transformation:

| Item | 11/30/61 | 7/31/63 (unaudited) |
|------|----------|---------------------|
| Cash | $166,000 | $89,000 |
| Marketable securities | – | $2,049,000 |
| Inventory | $4,203,000 | $890,000 |
| Net worth | $4,601,000 | $4,852,000 |

The sale of assets brought approximately book value. Combined with gains in the marketable securities portfolio, the realization was about **$80 per share**.

Buffett added: "I might mention that we think the buyers will do very well with Dempster. ... Our business is making excellent purchases—not making extraordinary sales."

## The Three Categories (Refined)

Buffett reiterated his three investment categories:

**1. Generals** – Undervalued stocks, determined primarily by quantitative standards but with attention to qualitative factors. "The issues lack glamour or market sponsorship." They move with the Dow.

**2. Workouts** – Securities with a timetable from corporate actions (mergers, liquidations, reorganizations). "We wait until we can read it in the paper." Risk is something upsetting the applecart, not the market.

**3. Controls** – Rarities where Buffett either controls the company or takes a very large position. "Once control is achieved, the value of our investment is determined by the value of the enterprise, not the oftentimes irrationalities of the marketplace."

He added an appendix with detailed case studies of Texas National Petroleum (a workout) and Dempster (a control).

## The Texas National Petroleum Example (Workout)

This was a "run-of-the-mill workout" from the sale of an oil producer to Union Oil of California. Buffett bought bonds, common stock, and warrants after the deal was publicly announced. The deal took longer than expected (as usual) but paid out a little better than estimates.

Key lesson: "The deals take longer than originally projected; and the payouts tend to average a little better than estimates."

## The Dempster Appendix (Nine Morals)

Buffett listed several lessons, including:

> "Our business is one requiring patience. It has little in common with a portfolio of high-flying glamour stocks and during periods of popularity for the latter, we may appear quite stodgy."

And the crucial point about secrecy:

> "We cannot talk about our current investment operations. Such an open-mouth policy could never improve our results and in some situations could seriously hurt us. For this reason, should anyone, including partners, ask us whether we are interested in any security, we must plead the '5th Amendment.'"

## How His Mind Works (1963 Edition)

**He measures investment companies against the Dow—and finds them wanting:**
> "The advisory talent managing just the four companies shown commands annual fees of over $7 million, and this represents a very small fraction of the industry. The public batting average of this highly-paid talent indicates they achieved results slightly less favorable than the Dow."

He's not criticizing them. He's stating a fact. Most professionals can't beat an unmanaged index.

**He thinks about after-tax compounding, not tax avoidance:**
> "I am an outspoken advocate of paying large amounts of income taxes—at low rates. A tremendous number of fuzzy, confused investment decisions are rationalized through so-called 'tax considerations.'"

He argues that freezing low-basis securities just because you don't want to pay tax is "a nullification of investment management." The goal is maximum after-tax compounding, not minimum taxes.

**He prefers to let others do the work:**
> "Everything else being equal, I would much rather let others do the work. However, when an active role is necessary to optimize the employment of capital you can be sure we will not be standing in the wings."

He's not a control freak. He's happy to be passive if passive works. But he'll act when needed.

**He values operating managers as partners:**
Harry Bottle became a limited partner. Buffett didn't just hire him—he brought him into the family.

## The Jokes

The Mona Lisa story is the best: "I trust this will end all discussion in our household about any purchase of paintings qualifying as an investment." He's mocking the idea that art is an investment—while using it to teach compounding.

Also, his aside about the "vicuna coat" reappears, and his note that "we have never had to bring a single man from the outside into the operation" of Berkshire (wait, that's 1965—actually in 1963 it's about Dempster, but the point stands).

## The Key Quote

> "Never count on making a good sale. Have the purchase price be so attractive that even a mediocre sale gives good results. The better sales will be the frosting on the cake."

He said this in 1962, but it's repeated in the 1963 appendix. It's worth repeating: **the profit is made when you buy, not when you sell.** That's the essence of value investing.
