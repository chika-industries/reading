# Analysis of the 1972 Berkshire Hathaway Letter (March 16, 1973)

## The Performance: A Stunning 19.8% Return on Equity

Operating earnings reached **19.8% of beginning shareholders' equity** – a "highly satisfactory" result. Buffett notes that every major line of business improved, but insurance underwriting saw the most dramatic gains.

He immediately tempers enthusiasm:

> "Due to an unusual convergence of favorable factors – diminishing auto accident frequency, moderating accident severity, and an absence of major catastrophes – underwriting profit margins achieved a level far above averages of the past or expectations of the future."

He expects a "modest decrease" in 1973 earnings.

## The Eight-Year Scorecard (Since Buffett Took Control)

Buffett provides a stunning summary of what he has accomplished since May 1965, when his partnership assumed policy control:

- **1964 fiscal year-end shareholders' equity:** $22,138,753
- **No additional equity capital** introduced since then (no stock sales, no mergers)
- **Shares outstanding reduced by 14%** through repurchases
- **Book value per share:** from $19.46 to $69.72 – a **16.5% compounded annual increase**

> "Our 1972 operating earnings of $11,116,256 represent a return many-fold higher than would have been produced had we continued to devote our resources entirely to the textile business."

This is Buffett's capital allocation genius in numbers. He took a struggling textile company, redeployed its cash flow into insurance and banking, and multiplied shareholder value without ever asking for a dime of outside capital.

## The Three Acquisitions: All Home Runs

Buffett lists the three major acquisitions: Jack Ringwalt (National Indemnity), Gene Abegg (Illinois National Bank), Vic Raab (Home & Automobile Insurance).

> "In all three cases, the founders were major sellers and received significant proceeds in cash – and, in all three cases, the same individuals ... have continued to run the businesses with undiminished energy and imagination which have resulted in further improvement of the fine records previously established."

This is the blueprint: buy wonderful businesses run by wonderful people, give them capital, and get out of their way.

## Textile Operations: Better, But Still Cyclical

As predicted, the textile industry picked up in 1972. Ken Chace and Ralph Rigby built a strong sales organization. Manufacturing was restructured. Inventory control improved, product mix improved.

> "While the general level of profitability of the industry will always be the primary factor in determining the level of our textile earnings, we believe that our relative position within the industry has noticeably improved."

The key phrase: "relative position." He can't control the industry tide, but he can make sure Berkshire swims better than the rest. Outlook for 1973 is "good."

## Insurance Underwriting: The Paradox of Good News

This section is classic Buffett – he sees the downside of short-term success.

> "They served to swell substantially total corporate profits for 1972, but the factors which produced such profits induced exceptional amounts of new competition at what we believe to be a non-compensatory level of rates."

He would almost rather have had a less profitable year if it meant less competition. Because now, rates are being cut unsoundly.

> "Over-all, we probably would have retained better prospects for the next five years if profits had not risen so dramatically this year."

This is long-term thinking. He's not celebrating the windfall; he's mourning the inevitable reaction.

**National Indemnity:** Premium volume declined as predicted. Underwriting margins will narrow. But Jack Ringwalt and Phil Liesche "will continue to underwrite to produce a profit, although not at the level of 1972, and base our rates on long-term expectations rather than short-term hopes."

> "Although this approach has meant dips in volume from time to time in the past, it has produced excellent long-term results."

**Reinsurance:** Same story. New entrants, rate-cutting, especially in catastrophe coverage. George Young built a profitable operation, but immediate expansion "is not sensible against a background of deteriorating rates."

> "When the loss potential inherent in such exposures becomes an actuality, repricing will take place which should give us a chance to expand significantly."

He's waiting for the inevitable disasters that will drive weaker competitors out.

**Home-state operation:** Cornhusker (Nebraska) did well. Minnesota and Texas had "unsatisfactory loss ratios" on very small volume. John Ringwalt is doing an excellent job, but expense ratios are high in the development stage. They plan to expand into at least one more state in 1973.

**Home & Automobile:** Vic Raab's company continued its excellent record. In 1973, they expect to enter Florida (Dade County) and California (Los Angeles) markets with the same specialized urban auto coverage. "Vic has the managerial capacity to run a much larger operation."

## Insurance Investment Results: The Gift of High Interest Rates

Buffett notes that the dramatic premium volume gains from 1969 to 1971 coincided with "virtually record-high interest rates." They put large amounts of investable funds to work at those high rates, mostly in tax-exempt bonds.

**Investment income growth:**
- 1969: $2,025,201
- 1972: $6,755,242

The bond portfolio has "unusually good call protection" – meaning issuers cannot force early redemption. So they will benefit from those high yields for many years.

> "The lack of current premium growth, however, will moderate substantially the growth in investment income during the next several years."

Acknowledging that the easy gains are behind them.

## Banking Operations: Still Industry Leader

Illinois National Bank earned **2.2% after tax on average deposits** – remarkable given:
- 50% time deposits (expensive, consumer savings paying maximum rates)
- Strong liquid position, no money-market borrowings
- Loan charge‑off ratio only **5% of the average commercial bank**

> "This record is a direct tribute to the leadership of Gene Abegg and Bob Kline who run a bank where the owners and the depositors can both eat well and sleep well."

That's a wonderful line – combining profit and safety.

Interest paid to depositors doubled from 1969. Loans expanded 38% in 1972. But Buffett expects Illinois National will be "unlikely to increase its earnings during 1973" due to rate structures, new Federal Reserve collection procedures, and higher taxes.

## Financial: The $20 Million Long-Term Loan

On March 15, 1973, Berkshire borrowed **$20 million at 8%** from twenty institutional lenders, due 1993, with principal repayments starting 1979.

> "Periodically, we expect that there will be opportunities to achieve significant expansion in our insurance business and we intend to have the financial resources available to maximize such opportunities."

He is loading up with dry powder. Waiting for the next insurance crisis.

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1973 Edition)

**1. He measures success by return on equity, not earnings growth.**
19.8% is the headline. Book value per share compounded at 16.5% since 1965. Those are the metrics that matter.

**2. He is almost perversely counter-cyclical.**
When insurance profits are high, he warns that competition will drive down rates and future profits. When others are celebrating, he's preparing for the downturn. And when others are retreating, he'll have the capital to expand.

**3. He understands that good news can be bad news.**
> "Over-all, we probably would have retained better prospects for the next five years if profits had not risen so dramatically this year."

This is a sophisticated insight. Short-term windfalls attract competition that destroys long-term returns. He'd rather have steady, unexciting profits that keep competitors away.

**4. He loves managers who are both prudent and hungry.**
Ringwalt, Abegg, Raab – all run for profit, not volume. All are willing to shrink when necessary. All retain their entrepreneurial fire after getting rich.

**5. He thinks in terms of decades, not quarters.**
The 20-year loan at 8% is a bet that he can earn more than 8% on that capital over two decades. He's confident.

**6. He is brutally honest about problems.**
Minnesota and Texas home-state operations had "unsatisfactory loss ratios." No sugarcoating. He states the problem and explains the path to improvement.

## The Best Lines

> "Gene Abegg and Bob Kline ... run a bank where the owners and the depositors can both eat well and sleep well."

That's the perfect articulation of his philosophy: high returns AND safety. Not either/or.

> "When the loss potential inherent in such exposures becomes an actuality, repricing will take place which should give us a chance to expand significantly."

He is not hoping for disasters. He is simply noting that disasters are inevitable, and when they happen, he will be ready.

> "We set no volume goals in our insurance business generally ... as virtually any volume can be achieved if profitability standards are ignored."

This is the mantra. He repeats it because it's the most important thing his shareholders need to understand.

## The Key Quote

> "Our belief is that industry underwriting profit margins will narrow substantially in 1973 or 1974 and, in time, this may produce an environment in which our historical growth can be resumed. Unfortunately, there is a lag between deterioration of underwriting results and tempering of competition."

He sees the cycle clearly: profits attract competition, competition cuts rates, rates cause losses, losses drive out competition, then profits return. The key is to survive the down phase and have capital to deploy when others are wounded.

But the quote that best captures the letter's essence:

> "Over-all, we probably would have retained better prospects for the next five years if profits had not risen so dramatically this year."

That is a man who thinks so far ahead that today's good news looks like tomorrow's problem. It's a rare and valuable inversion.

## The Jokes (Subdued)

No laugh lines like the partnership days. The closest is the "eat well and sleep well" line about the bank – a quiet, elegant formulation. And the understated "it is a pleasure to report" opening – after 19.8% ROE, that's a bit of a deadpan.

## Transition Note

This letter shows Buffett fully in command. The partnership is gone. Berkshire is his canvas. He has assembled a collection of first-class businesses run by first-class people. The textile anchor is still there, but it no longer defines the ship.

The 16.5% compounded book value growth since 1965 is the proof. He didn't need to start a new fund. He didn't need outside capital. He just needed a vehicle – and the discipline to allocate capital where it would earn the highest return. That vehicle was Berkshire Hathaway.
