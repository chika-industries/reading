# Analysis of the 1975 Berkshire Hathaway Letter

## The Performance: A New Low

Operating earnings: **$6,713,592** or **$6.85 per share** – a **7.6% return on beginning shareholders' equity**. This is the lowest return since 1967.

Worse, a "large segment" of these earnings came from Federal income tax refunds – "which will not be available to assist performance in 1976."

Buffett's forecast from last year ("the outlook for 1975 is not encouraging") proved "distressingly accurate."

## The Outlook for 1976: Somewhat Brighter

Buffett lists the positives:
- Significantly better textile results
- Earnings from recent acquisitions (Waumbec)
- Increased equity in Blue Chip Stamps due to enlarged ownership
- At least moderate improvement in insurance underwriting

But the major variable – "by far the most difficult to predict with any feeling of confidence" – is insurance underwriting.

> "If such improvement is moderate, our overall gain in earnings in 1976 likewise will prove moderate. More significant underwriting improvement could give us a major gain in earnings."

## Textile Operations: A "V-Shaped" Depression

The first half of 1975 was "extremely depressed" – production curtailed, employment down 53% from a year earlier.

But unlike previous slumps, most textile producers cut production quickly to match orders, preventing massive inventory buildup. When retail demand revived, the rebound was rapid. Beginning midyear, business rebounded.

> "This 'V' shaped textile depression, while one of the sharpest on record, also became one of the shortest."

The fourth quarter produced an "excellent profit," bringing the full year into the black.

**Acquisition of Waumbec Mills** (April 28, 1975) – located in Manchester, New Hampshire. Made woven goods for drapery and apparel, complementing Berkshire's Home Fabrics line. Before acquisition, the company ran at "very substantial loss" with only 55% of looms operating. Losses continued for a few months after acquisition, then "outstanding efforts" by manufacturing, administrative, and sales people produced major improvements. Waumbec moved into "significant profit position."

**Outlook for 1976:** "good level of profits." But Buffett adds a crucial caveat:

> "We continue to look for ways to increase further our scale of operations while avoiding major capital investment in new fixed assets which we consider unwise, considering the relatively low returns historically earned on large scale investment in new textile equipment."

He will never throw good money after bad into textiles.

## Insurance Underwriting: The Worst Year in History

Buffett calls 1975 the "worst year in history" for the property and casualty insurance industry. "We did our share – unfortunately, even somewhat more."

**The causes:**

1. **Economic inflation** – cost of repairing humans and property far outstripped general inflation. Premium levels were set in a different cost environment.
2. **"Social" inflation** – liability concepts expanded continuously, far beyond limits contemplated when rates were established. "Adding coverage beyond what was paid for." Increased propensity to sue, mammoth jury awards for events not previously considered statistically significant.
3. **Guaranty Funds** – when insolvent companies can't pay claims, the costs are divided among remaining solvent insurers. Berkshire pays its share.

> "These trends will continue, and should moderate any optimism which otherwise might be justified by the sharply increased rates now taking effect."

**Berkshire's specific problem:** The company has a "disproportionate concentration" in exactly the lines that produced the worst results – lines that normally produce high investment income (and were therefore attractive), but have been "very disadvantageous during the past two years."

> "It well may be that we will remain positioned in the more difficult part of the insurance spectrum during the inflationary years ahead."

**Home-state operation:** The only segment with improved results. Combined ratio improved from 1974, though still at an underwriting loss. Texas United (a major problem a few years ago) won the "Chairman's Cup" for lowest loss ratio under new command of George Billing. Cornhusker (Nebraska) had a combined ratio "slightly under 100" – meaning underwriting profit. Substantial premium growth expected in 1976.

**National Indemnity (traditional business):** "Extraordinarily bad" underwriting year. Rates increased frequently but lagged loss experience. Several special programs from the early 1970s caused "significant losses" and "heavy drain on managerial time." Premium volume expected to increase in 1976 – but no guarantee of underwriting improvement.

**Reinsurance:** Same problems. Because reinsurance contract settlements lag direct business, any upturn in direct results "will precede those of the reinsurance segment."

**Home & Automobile (Chicago, Cook County only):** Experience "continued very bad" – management change in October. John Seward made President, implementing "completely revamped underwriting approach."

**Overall insurance outlook for 1976:** Substantial gain in premium volume (mostly from rate increases, not more policies). Underwriting experience "should improve – and we expect it to – but our confidence level is not high." Buffett's goal is a combined ratio below 100, but "it is unlikely to be attained during 1976."

## Insurance Investments: A Permanently Held Stock

Investment income growth was "moderate" – premium volume flat, underwriting losses reduced investable funds.

At the end of 1974, the stock portfolio had a net unrealized loss of about $17 million. By March 31, 1976, net unrealized gains were about $15 million – a swing of $32 million.

But the big news:

> "Our largest equity investment is 467,150 shares of Washington Post 'B' stock with a cost of $10.6 million, which we expect to hold permanently."

**Permanently.** That is a powerful word in Buffett's vocabulary. He is not a trader. He is an owner.

> "With this approach, stock market fluctuations are of little importance to us – except as they may provide buying opportunities – but business performance is of major importance."

He has been "delighted with progress made by practically all of the companies in which we now have significant investments."

## Banking: Eugene Abegg's Masterpiece

> "It is difficult to find adjectives to describe the performance of Eugene Abegg."

Illinois National Bank continued its outstanding record. Against average loans of about $65 million, net loan losses were **$24,000 – 0.04%.** Unusually high liquidity: obligations of the U.S. Government and its agencies due within one year amounted to about **75% of demand deposits** at year-end.

Buffett adds: "Maximum rates of interest are paid on all consumer savings instruments" – usually a drag on profitability, but Illinois National's costs have been controlled well.

The bank now pays dividends to Berkshire, which is particularly welcome because Berkshire has taxable income to offset.

## Blue Chip Stamps and the Merger

Berkshire increased its ownership of Blue Chip Stamps to approximately **31.5%** by March 1976.

The merger with Diversified Retailing was not completed, but Buffett says: "We still consider such a merger to be desirable and hope to accomplish it at some future date."

## The Ten-Year Scorecard (1965-1975)

Buffett provides a remarkable summary of what he and his team have accomplished since taking control in May 1965:

| Metric | 1964 (fiscal year-end Sept) | 1975 |
|--------|------------------------------|------|
| Net worth | $22.1 million | $92.9 million |
| Shares outstanding | 1,137,778 | 979,569 |
| Book value per share | $19.46 | $94.92 |

**Compound annual growth in book value per share: slightly over 15%** – through two brutal bear markets, an insurance industry collapse, and a textile depression.

They acquired six businesses for cash from private owners, started four others, purchased a large interest in Blue Chip Stamps, and reduced shares outstanding.

> "Our objective is a conservatively financed and highly liquid business – possessing extra margins of balance sheet strength consistent with the fiduciary obligations inherent in the banking and insurance industries – which will produce a long term rate of return on equity capital exceeding that of American industry as a whole."

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1975 Edition)

**1. He is brutally honest about bad results.**
"Distressingly accurate." "Extraordinarily bad." "Our confidence level is not high." No spin.

**2. He distinguishes between temporary and permanent problems.**
Textiles had a "V-shaped" depression – sharp but short. Insurance is more problematic: "it well may be that we will remain positioned in the more difficult part of the insurance spectrum" for years.

**3. He buys businesses, not stocks.**
The Washington Post holding will be held "permanently." He cares about business performance, not stock prices. Market fluctuations "are of little importance" except for buying opportunities.

**4. He is willing to change management when necessary.**
Home & Automobile got a new president in October after "continued very bad" experience. He doesn't cling to failing strategies or people.

**5. He thinks in terms of "social inflation" – a prescient insight.**
He identifies that liability concepts have expanded "far beyond limits contemplated when rates were established." This is decades before the term became common. He saw that jury awards and lawsuits were changing the math of insurance.

**6. He is patient.**
> "Underwriting experience should improve – and we expect it to – but our confidence level is not high."

He's not predicting a quick bounce. He's managing through the cycle, not trying to call the turn.

**7. He loves managers like Eugene Abegg.**
"Difficult to find adjectives" – this is Buffett's highest praise. A builder who runs a conservative, profitable operation with extraordinary loan quality.

## The Best Lines

> "The 'V' shaped textile depression, while one of the sharpest on record, also became one of the shortest."

A clean, memorable description.

> "We have continued to look for ways to increase further our scale of operations while avoiding major capital investment in new fixed assets which we consider unwise, considering the relatively low returns historically earned."

He will not chase growth in a low-return business. Discipline.

> "Social inflation caused the liability concept to be expanded continuously, far beyond limits contemplated when rates were established – in effect, adding coverage beyond what was paid for."

A brilliant diagnosis that most insurance executives missed at the time.

> "Our largest equity investment ... we expect to hold permanently."

That word – "permanently" – is a declaration of philosophy.

## The Key Quote

> "Stock market fluctuations are of little importance to us – except as they may provide buying opportunities – but business performance is of major importance."

This is the clearest statement yet of Buffett's separation of price from value. The market is there to serve you, not to instruct you.

But the quote that best captures the letter's spirit:

> "Our objective is a conservatively financed and highly liquid business ... which will produce a long term rate of return on equity capital exceeding that of American industry as a whole."

After the worst year since 1967, he restates the mission statement. Not "we will earn 20%." Not "we will grow at any cost." Just: be conservative, be liquid, and over the long term, beat the average American industry. That is a goal he can pursue forever.

## Transition Note

1975 was the bottom. Insurance underwriting was a disaster. Textiles had just survived the sharpest but shortest depression. But Buffett's Washington Post purchase – held "permanently" – would become one of the most famous investments in history. The 15% compounded book value growth since 1965 was still intact. And the worst was behind him.

The letters from this period are essential reading for anyone who wants to understand how Buffett thinks in a crisis. He doesn't panic. He doesn't change his philosophy. He states the facts, takes his lumps, and prepares for the eventual recovery. That is the temperament of a great investor.
