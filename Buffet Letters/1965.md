# Summary of Buffett's Ninth Year (1965)

## The Performance: A Blowout (But Anomalous)

The Dow gained 14.2% (including dividends). The Partnership gained **47.2%** before allocation to the general partner. Limited partners received **36.9%**.

Cumulative since 1957: Partnership up **943.2%** vs. Dow up **164.1%**. Annual compounded rate for limited partners: **23.9%** over nine years.

But Buffett was almost apologetic. He had predicted in the previous year's "Our Goal" section that they'd aim for a ten-point advantage over the Dow, with extremes of perhaps 25 points in a great year. Instead, they got a 33-point advantage. He called it a "freak" and said it was unlikely to be repeated.

> "Naturally, no writer likes to be publicly humiliated by such a mistake. It is unlikely to be repeated."

## The Sorrows of Compounding (A Title Change)

For years, Buffett had written about "The Joys of Compounding." Now, with the partnership having grown to $43.6 million in beginning capital, he was feeling the weight of size. He changed the title to "The Sorrows of Compounding."

> "I now feel that we are much closer to the point where increased size may prove disadvantageous."

He was not closing the partnership to existing partners' additions, but he announced: **"I intend to admit no additional partners to BPL"** – except for family members? Actually, he told Susie that if they had more children, "it is up to her to find some other partnership for them."

This was a pivotal moment. He was prioritizing performance over asset gathering.

## The Berkshire Hathaway Control Situation

This is the first appearance of the company that would become his legendary vehicle. Buffett had started buying Berkshire Hathaway stock in 1962 at **$7.60 per share** (average cost $14.86 due to heavy purchases in early 1965).

**The backstory:** Berkshire was a textile business in decline. In the postwar period, it had 11 mills and 11,000 workers, earning $29.5 million pre-tax in 1948. By the time Buffett acquired control in spring 1965, it was down to two mills and 2,300 employees.

**The pleasant surprise:** The remaining units had excellent management, led by Ken Chace. No outside managers needed.

**The valuation at year-end 1965:** Net working capital alone (before placing any value on plants and equipment) was about **$19 per share**. Buffett valued the business conservatively.

> "While a Berkshire is hardly going to be as profitable as a Xerox, Fairchild Camera or National Video in a hypertensed market, it is a very comfortable sort of thing to own."

And his West Coast philosopher's quip: "It is well to have a diet consisting of oatmeal as well as cream puffs."

## Diversification: The Noah School of Investing

Buffett introduced a new Ground Rule (#7): **"We might invest up to 40% of our net worth in a single security"** – under conditions of extremely high probability that facts and reasoning are correct, and very low probability of anything drastically changing the underlying value.

He then attacked conventional diversification:

> "Anyone owning such numbers of securities after presumably studying their investment merit (and I don't care how prestigious their labels) is following what I call the Noah School of Investing – two of everything. Such investors should be piloting arks."

He was unsparing: If good performance is even a minor objective, "any portfolio encompassing one hundred stocks is not being operated logically."

His reasoning: The hundredth stock reduces potential variance but also reduces expected return, because different opportunities have different expectations. He was willing to accept wider year-to-year swings in exchange for better long-term performance.

> "I am willing to give up quite a bit in terms of leveling of year-to-year results ... in order to achieve better overall long-term performance."

He cited his own record: margins versus the Dow ranged from 2.4 points (1958) to 33.0 points (1965). The investment funds had much narrower amplitudes – but also lower long-term returns.

Billy Rose provided the punchline: "You've got a harem of seventy girls; you don't get to know any of them very well."

## The Investment Company Comparison (Still Unflattering)

Buffett continued to track the four largest funds. Over the full period (1957-65), the Dow compounded at 11.4%, the limited partners at 23.9%, while the funds ranged from 9.4% to 10.5%. He quoted Frank Block's observation that the investment literature suffers "a yawning hiatus" on performance measurement, and that many managers "seem to prefer not to know how well or poorly they are doing."

## How His Mind Works (1965 Edition)

**He thinks in terms of mathematical expectation, not certainty:**
He explained that diversification decisions depend on the "wideness of the spread between the mathematical expectation of number one versus number eight" and the probability of really poor relative performance. He admitted it's imprecise, but insisted that's what the business is about.

**He is brutally honest about his own limitations:**
> "Frankly, operating in this field [Generals – Relatively Undervalued] is somewhat more ethereal than operating in the other three categories, and I'm just not an ethereal sort."

He also confessed: "Candor also demands I point out that during 1965 we had our worst single investment experience in the history of BPL on one idea in this group."

**He prioritizes people and relationships over a few extra points:**
> "When I am dealing with people I like, in businesses I find stimulating (what business isn't?), and achieving worthwhile overall returns on capital employed (say, 10-12%), it seems foolish to rush from situation to situation to earn a few more percentage points."

This is a major insight into his later decades. At some point, maximizing returns is less important than enjoying the work.

**He values the "two-string bow":**
A general bought cheap may either appreciate in the market or, if it doesn't, become a control situation. That optionality – the ability to force value realization if the market remains irrational – is a huge advantage.

## The Jokes

The "vicuna coat" reference is gone, but the humor is richer:

- "The medically oriented probably will interpret this entire section [on not adding new partners] as conclusive evidence that an effective antithyroid pill has been developed." (He's joking that he's finally calmed down his competitive drive.)

- The "Noah School of Investing" and the Billy Rose harem quote are both gems.

- His comment about the Dow: "Even Samson gets clipped occasionally" – referring to the fact that the eight largest companies (GM, Exxon, Sears, etc.) all lost value in the first half.

- The line about his office expansion: "We boldly announced an expansion move, encompassing an additional 227 1/4 square feet. Older partners shook their heads. I feel that our gain from operations in 1965 of $12,304,060 indicates that we did not overextend ourselves."

## The Key Quote

> "Price is what you pay. Value is what you get."

Simple. Memorable. And the entire philosophy of the partnership in seven words. He used it in the context of Berkshire Hathaway – market price irrelevant to controlling interest valuation.

But the quote that best captures his 1965 mindset might be:

> "I am willing to give up quite a bit in terms of leveling of year-to-year results ... in order to achieve better overall long-term performance."

He knew concentration would cause "an occasional very sour year." He was willing to take that risk. His partners had to be willing too – "not only in your cerebral regions, but also down in the pit of your stomach."
