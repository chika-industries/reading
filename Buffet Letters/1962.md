# Summary of Buffett's Sixth Year (1962)

## The Market: A Real Test

After years of Buffett warning that his method would shine in a declining market, 1962 delivered. The Dow fell from 731 to 652—a loss of 10.8% (7.6% after dividends). But the real action was inside the year: the Dow dipped to 535 in June before rallying. For many "growth" funds that had soared in 1959-61, the pain was severe. Fidelity Capital Fund, Putnam Growth Fund, and Wellington Equity Fund averaged a **minus 32.3%** in the first half.

Buffett's partnership? Overall gain of **13.9%** for the full year. Limited partners got **11.9%**.

He was quick to put this in perspective: "Because of a strong rally in the last few months, the general market as measured by the Dow really did not have such a frightening decline as many might think." But relative to the Dow, this was his kind of year.

## The Ground Rules (Now Codified)

For the first time, Buffett printed a clear list of "Ground Rules" that all partners had to understand before investing. These became famous:

1. **No guaranteed return** – monthly withdrawals are just that; if they don't earn 6%, it's a return of capital.
2. **If we fail to achieve 6%, monthly payments will be lowered** the following year.
3. **We talk about market values, not tax results** – the two can be very different.
4. **Performance is measured against the Dow and leading investment companies** – not absolute plus or minus.
5. **Three years is the absolute minimum for judging performance** – five years is better.
6. **I do not predict stock market or business fluctuations** – if you think that's essential, don't invest with me.
7. **What I do promise:** investments chosen on value, not popularity; absolute minimum risk of permanent capital loss through margin of safety and diversification; my family and I have virtually all our net worth in the partnership.

That last one—eating his own cooking—was non-negotiable.

## The Dempster Turnaround (Harry Bottle Arrives)

Dempster Mill Manufacturing, the control situation Buffett had acquired at ~$28 per share, was his 1962 triumph. The story is classic Buffett operating playbook.

**The problem:** Dempster had been a "general" purchased in 1956. By 1961, Buffett owned 70% (acquired at $16-25 per share, with large block at $30.25). But the business was stagnant—poor management, tough industry. For six months after taking control, he tried to work with existing management. "These efforts were completely fruitless."

**The solution:** A friend recommended Harry Bottle. On April 17, 1962, Buffett met Harry in Los Angeles, offered a deal with rewards tied to objectives. Six days later, Harry was president.

**The results (in Buffett's words):**
> "Harry is unquestionably the man of the year. Every goal we have set for Harry has been met, and all the surprises have been on the pleasant side."

What Harry accomplished:
- Inventory: from $4.2 million to $1.9 million (reducing discounts by ~$920,000)
- Cash: from $166,000 to over $1 million (plus investments)
- Liabilities: from $2.3 million to $250,000
- Administrative and selling expense: cut in half
- Factory overhead: from $6 to $4.50 per direct labor hour
- Closed five unprofitable branches, kept three good ones

By November 1962, Buffett estimated Dempster's year-end value at **$50 per share** (up from $35 at the prior year-end).

**The balance sheet transformation** (abbreviated):

| Item | Nov 30, 1961 | Nov 30, 1962 (est.) |
|------|--------------|---------------------|
| Cash | $166,000 | $1,000,000+ |
| Inventory | $4,203,000 | $1,900,000 |
| Liabilities | $2,318,000 | $250,000 |
| Value per share (est.) | $35 | $50+ |

> "The successful conversion of substantial portions of the assets of Dempster to cash, at virtually 100 cents on the dollar, has been the high point of 1962."

## The First Half Report (July 1962)

Buffett also issued a mid-year letter that showed the partnership down only 7.5% while the Dow was down 21.7%—a 14.2 percentage point advantage. He noted:

> "Our target is an approximately 1/2% decline for each 1% decline in the Dow."

He also pointed out that the so-called "growth funds" had been hammered, and that the rush of new investors into those funds after their good years meant "the poor performance this year was experienced by very many more holders than enjoyed the excellent performance of earlier years." A warning about chasing past performance.

## How His Mind Works (1962 Edition)

**He measures conservatism by down-market performance, not conventionality:**
> "If the market should be down 35% or 40% in a year (and I feel this has a high probability of occurring one year in the next ten—no one knows which one), we should be down only 15% or 20%."

He doesn't claim to avoid losses. He claims to lose less. That's his definition of conservative.

**He understands that his good years relative to the Dow come from allocation, not market timing:**
> "Our success to date in 1962 certainly does not reflect any ability on my part to guess the market (I never try), but merely reflects the fact that the high prices of generals partially forced me into other categories of investment."

He didn't predict the decline. He just couldn't find enough cheap generals, so he ended up in workouts and controls—which happened to be the right place to be.

**He is ruthlessly honest about the uniqueness of 1962:**
> "I want all partners and prospective partners to realize the results described above are distinctly abnormal and will recur infrequently, if at all."

No boasting. No promises of repetition. Just facts.

**He values operating talent as much as investment talent:**
Harry Bottle wasn't a security analyst. He was a hands-on operator who could turn around a failing business. Buffett recognized that some problems require operational skill, not just financial engineering.

## The Jokes

The line about the "vicuna coat" reappears (a reference to the 1950s scandal). But the best joke is self-deprecating:

> "We are all geared up with secretarial help, a new typewriter, etc."

He had just moved into an office and hired staff. The new typewriter was a big deal.

Also, his note about the "No prizes for being the last ones to get in the agreement" – a dry administrative joke, but it shows his impatience with paperwork.

## The Key Quote

> "Never count on making a good sale. Have the purchase price be so attractive that even a mediocre sale gives good results. The better sales will be the frosting on the cake."

This is Buffett's investment philosophy in one sentence. **The profit is made at the buy, not the sell.** If you buy cheap enough, you don't need to be a genius at selling. The margin of safety protects you even when things go wrong. The great exits are just bonus.
