# Summary of Buffett's Eighth Year (1964)

## The Performance: A Decent Year, Not a Great One

The Dow gained 18.7% (including dividends). The Partnership gained **27.8%** before allocation to the general partner. Limited partners received **22.3%**.

Buffett was candid: this 9.1 percentage point advantage over the Dow (partnership basis) and 3.6 points for limited partners was "the poorest since 1959, which was a year of roughly comparable gains for the Dow."

> "Nevertheless, I am not depressed. It was a strong year for the general market, and it is always tougher for us to outshine the Dow in such a year."

Cumulative since 1957: Partnership up **608.7%** vs. Dow up **131.3%**. Annual compounded rate for limited partners: **22.3%** over eight years.

## The Duck Analogy (Investment Companies vs. The Dow)

Buffett continued his regular comparison with the four largest investment companies, which collectively managed over $4 billion. Their results? Roughly in line with the Dow.

> "These figures continue to show that the most highly paid and respected investment management has difficulty matching the performance of an unmanaged index of blue chip stocks."

Then came the duck analogy:

> "The results of these companies in some ways resemble the activity of a duck sitting on a pond. When the water (the market) rises, the duck rises; when it falls, back goes the duck. ... The rise and fall of the lake is hardly something for him to quack about."

His point: Most investment managers take credit for results that are almost entirely a function of the market's tide. Buffett wanted to be measured by how much he *added* to that tide—the flapping of his own wings.

## The Question of Conservatism (Again)

Buffett anticipated a common objection: "Aren't those investment companies run more conservatively than the Partnership?"

He disagreed.

> "It is unquestionably true that the investment companies have their money more conventionally invested than we do. To many people conventionality is indistinguishable from conservatism. In my view, this represents erroneous thinking. Neither a conventional nor an unconventional approach, per se, is conservative."

His definition:

> "Truly conservative actions arise from intelligent hypotheses, correct facts and sound reasoning. These qualities may lead to conventional acts, but there have been many times when they have led to unorthodoxy. In some corner of the world they are probably still holding regular meetings of the Flat Earth Society."

**The objective test for conservatism:** performance in declining markets. Over the three down years in his record, the limited partners had cumulatively gained 45.0% while the Dow and the four big funds had all lost (ranging from -9.7% to -24.6%).

> "We certainly think it makes more sense than saying 'We own (regardless of price) A.T. & T., General Electric, IBM and General Motors and are therefore conservative.'"

## The Manhattan Indians (Another Compounding Lesson)

Buffett continued his historical compounding examples. This time: the 1626 sale of Manhattan Island.

> "My understanding is that [the Indians] received $24 net. For this, Minuit received 22.3 square miles which works out to about 621,688,320 square feet."

Using a $20 per square foot estimate, the current land value would be about $12.5 billion. But if the Indians had achieved only a 6.5% return on that $24, it would be $42 billion today. At 7%, $205 billion.

> "So much for that."

He then showed the compounding table again (4%, 8%, 12%, 16% over 10, 20, 30 years) and noted:

> "This table indicates the financial advantages of: (1) a long life (the Methusalah Technique); (2) a high compound rate; (3) a combination of both (especially recommended by this author)."

## The Four Categories (Expanded)

For the first time, Buffett broke his "Generals" category into two sub-categories:

**1. Generals – Private Owner Basis**
Securities selling at a substantial discount to what a private owner would pay for the whole business. "We like good management – we like a decent industry – we like a certain amount of 'ferment' in a previously dormant management or stockholder group. But, we demand value." Often has a "two strings to our bow" – either market appreciation or eventual control.

**2. Generals – Relatively Undervalued**
Securities cheap *relative* to securities of the same general quality, but not necessarily cheap on a private-owner basis. "It is important in this category, of course, that apples be compared to apples – and not to oranges."

**3. Workouts** (unchanged)

**4. Controls** (unchanged)

He noted that a new technique in the "Relatively Undervalued" category promised to "very substantially reduce the risk from an overall change in valuation standards."

## How His Mind Works (1964 Edition)

**He demands objective measurement – for others and for himself:**
> "The management of money is big business. ... After devoting strenuous efforts to objectively measuring the managements of portfolio companies, it seems strange indeed that similar examination is not applied to the portfolio managers themselves."

He insisted that partners judge him by a pre-selected yardstick (the Dow), not by whether he had a plus or minus year. And he would not change yardsticks if he fell short.

**He thinks in terms of "expectable return" not "guaranteed outcome":**
> "Long-range expectable return is the primary consideration of all of us belonging to BPL."

He was willing to state his expectations (7% Dow, 10 percentage point advantage, so ~17% for BPL, ~14% for limited partners) even though he knew "prophecy reveals far more of the frailties of the prophet than it reveals of the future."

**He is unimpressed by "swap funds" (tax-deferral schemes):**
He analyzed swap funds – investment companies created by exchanging shares to defer capital gains taxes – and showed that the three largest had performed terribly (2.6%, 1.1%, and 9.8% annual compounded vs. the Dow's 9.8%). His conclusion: "Anyone for taxes?" The point: tax avoidance often leads to poor investment decisions.

**He values alignment:**
> "My wife and I will have the largest single investment in the new partnership. ... I am inserting a provision in the partnership agreement which will prohibit the purchase by me or my family of any marketable securities. In other words, the new partnership will represent my entire investment operation in marketable securities."

No side bets. No separate accounts. His money moves exactly with his partners'.

## The Jokes

The Flat Earth Society line is classic. The Manhattan Indians story is delivered with deadpan humor – "So much for that." And his note about adding 227 1/4 square feet to the office (while Ford added 6.7 million square feet) is pure Buffett self-deprecation.

But the best joke might be his summary of the swap fund analysis: "Anyone for taxes?" – implying that paying taxes is better than lousy performance.

## The Key Quote

> "You will not be right simply because a large number of people momentarily agree with you. You will not be right simply because important people agree with you. ... You will be right, over the course of many transactions, if your hypotheses are correct, your facts are correct, and your reasoning is correct. True conservatism is only possible through knowledge and reason."

This is the intellectual foundation of everything he did. No authority, no consensus, no convention – just facts, hypotheses, and reasoning. And the willingness to be wrong in the short term.
