# Analysis of the 1973 Berkshire Hathaway Letter

## The Performance: Solid but Declining

Operating earnings: **$11,930,592** producing a **17.4% return on beginning shareholders' equity**. Earnings per share improved from $11.43 to $12.18. But return on equity fell from 19.8% in 1972 to 17.4% – a decline Buffett had forecast in the previous letter.

> "We had forecast in last year's report that such a decline was likely. Unfortunately, our forecast proved to be correct."

He expects further decline in 1974 "on our enlarged equity base."

## Management's Objective (Classic Buffett Statement)

> "Management's objective is to achieve a return on capital over the long term which averages somewhat higher than that of American industry generally – while utilizing sound accounting and debt policies."

This is the clearest articulation yet of Berkshire's goal. Not to maximize every year. Not to grow at any cost. Just to beat the average American industry return over the long term, with conservative accounting and debt.

## Textile Operations: Shortages and Price Controls

Textile demand was "unusually strong" in 1973. The problem: fiber shortages, which prevented full loom utilization. Some fiber prices "skyrocketed."

**The price control problem:** Cost of Living Council regulations prevented Berkshire from pricing many finished products at market levels, while some competitors could. But Buffett is philosophical:

> "The textile business has been highly cyclical and price controls may have served to cut down some of the hills while still leaving us with the inevitable valleys."

**Accounting change:** Because of extraordinary raw material price rises, Berkshire adopted the **LIFO** (last-in, first-out) method of inventory pricing. This matches current costs against current revenues and minimizes inventory "profits" included in reported earnings. This is conservative accounting – exactly Buffett's style.

## Insurance Operations: The Passing of a Legend

**Jack Ringwalt retired** as President of National Indemnity after founding the business in 1940. Buffett calls his record "absolutely brilliant." Phil Liesche succeeded him, and "fortunately for us, possesses the same underwriting and managerial philosophy."

**Traditional business (specialized auto and general liability):** An "exceptionally fine underwriting year" – but volume declined again. Competition was intense, and Buffett passed on matching rate cuts.

> "There currently are faint indications that some of these competitors are learning of the inadequacy of their rates (and also of their loss reserves) which may result in easing of market pressures as the year develops. If so, we may again experience volume increases."

He's waiting for the inevitable reckoning when competitors realize their rates don't cover their losses.

**Reinsurance:** The competitive conditions continued from 1972. Rates were inadequate, but Berkshire had some "substantial individual policies" that benefited results. George Young continues to build this operation.

**Home-state operation:** The "average underwriting performance was unsatisfactory." This is a rare admission of weakness. Losses occurred in several states, though Cornhusker (Nebraska) continued to do well. The companies in Minnesota, Texas, and Iowa (launched in 1973) need to "demonstrate their ability to produce underwriting profits at acceptable volume levels."

But then a bombshell:

> "Early in 1974, we plan to begin a full-scale restructuring of the home-state operation."

That's a serious move – not just incremental improvement.

**Home & Automobile (Chicago):** Vic Raab's company had an "excellent year." The expansion into Florida and California was "encouraging but very expensive in start-up costs." Buffett notes that "new ventures in the insurance business share the characteristic of most new ventures – they initially produce red ink." But he expects "very satisfactory results" once the marketing operation is established and premiums grow.

**Overall insurance outlook:** A mixed picture. Traditional business solid but shrinking. New ventures losing money (as expected). Buffett remains committed to insurance as "a most attractive area in which to employ capital."

## Blue Chip Stamps: A New Significant Holding

This section is important because Blue Chip Stamps would become a major part of Berkshire's story (and eventually be merged in).

**Holdings:** At year-end, Berkshire owned about 19% of Blue Chip Stamps. After year-end, increased to about 22.5%. A proposed merger with Diversified Retailing Company would bring that to about 38.5%.

**The accounting headache:** Blue Chip's fiscal year ended two months after Berkshire's (or ten months before, depending on how you look at it). Berkshire had a choice:
- Use audited figures from Blue Chip's prior year (ten months old) – no auditor disclaimer
- Use unaudited interim figures from Blue Chip's current year – more accurate but Peat, Marwick couldn't express an opinion on that portion

Buffett chose **current but unaudited** figures because the alternative "seemed at odds with reality." He recognized $1,008,000 of Blue Chip's earnings for 1973 (vs. $632,000 under the lagged method). This is classic Buffett: substance over form.

**Blue Chip's business:** Trading stamp business has "declined drastically." But it has important subsidiaries: See's Candy Shops and Wesco Financial (54%-owned savings and loan). Buffett expects "satisfactory earnings" related to capital employed – though much lower than if trading stamps had held up.

**Board memberships:** Buffett is on the board of Blue Chip Stamps and Wesco Financial, and Chairman of See's Candy. Operating management is "first-class, able, experienced."

## Sun Newspapers: The Pulitzer Prize

Buffett had not mentioned Sun Newspapers in annual report text since 1969 because it was "financially insignificant." But in 1973, it became significant for a different reason.

**On May 7th, Sun Newspapers won a Pulitzer Prize for local investigative reporting** – the first time a weekly had won in that category. The reporting exposed "the extraordinary contrast between decreasing services and mounting wealth" at Boys Town since Father Flanagan's death in 1948.

> "Our congratulations go to Paul Williams, Editor, and Stan Lipsey, Publisher, as well as the entire editorial staff of Sun Newspapers for their achievement, which vividly illustrated that size need not be equated with significance in publishing."

This is a lovely aside. Buffett values excellence even when it doesn't move the needle financially. And the line "size need not be equated with significance" could apply to his own investment philosophy as well.

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1973 Edition)

**1. He forecasts bad news and delivers it honestly.**
He said ROE would decline. It did. He says 1974 will be worse. He doesn't sugarcoat. This builds trust.

**2. He values consistency of philosophy over short-term performance.**
Phil Liesche was promoted because he shares Ringwalt's underwriting discipline. Buffett doesn't need a genius who will swing for the fences. He needs someone who will not take stupid risks.

**3. He is willing to take an accounting treatment that makes the auditors uncomfortable if it better reflects reality.**
Choosing unaudited but current figures over audited but stale figures – that's a man who cares about economic substance, not just a clean opinion.

**4. He will restructure losing operations rather than let them drift.**
The home-state operation is being "full-scale restructured." He tried. It didn't work. Now he's fixing it. No ego.

**5. He takes quiet pride in non-financial achievements.**
The Pulitzer Prize gets a whole paragraph. It's not about money. It's about doing good work. Buffett values that.

**6. He thinks in terms of return on equity, not earnings per share growth.**
ROE fell from 19.8% to 17.4% even though EPS rose. He's more concerned with the efficiency of capital than the absolute number.

## The Jokes (Very Dry)

The line about price controls cutting down the hills while leaving the valleys – that's a wry observation about government intervention.

The accounting explanation – "an acceptable accounting choice ... seemed at odds with reality" – is a quiet jab at accountants who prioritize auditability over truth.

The Sun Newspapers line – "size need not be equated with significance" – is a gentle self-reminder that small things can matter.

## The Key Quote

> "Management's objective is to achieve a return on capital over the long term which averages somewhat higher than that of American industry generally – while utilizing sound accounting and debt policies."

This is the mission statement. Not "maximize growth." Not "beat the S&P 500 by 10%." Just be better than average, conservatively. It's humble but ambitious. And it reflects Buffett's understanding that compounding at 15-20% over decades is extraordinary even if it's not 50% every year.

But the quote that best captures his 1973 thinking might be:

> "There currently are faint indications that some of these competitors are learning of the inadequacy of their rates (and also of their loss reserves) which may result in easing of market pressures."

He is watching competitors self-destruct. He knows that underpricing risk eventually catches up. He has the patience to wait. And the capital to act when they retreat.

## Transition Note

1973 was a year of transition. Ringwalt retired. The home-state operation failed to perform. Blue Chip Stamps became a major holding. And Buffett continued to build the machine – patient, disciplined, and willing to restructure what didn't work.

The textile business was still there, still cyclical, still not a great business. But it was no longer the story. Insurance, banking, candy (through Blue Chip), and stamps (though declining) – these were the future. And Buffett was already looking ahead to the next decade.
