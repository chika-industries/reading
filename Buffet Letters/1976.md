# Analysis of the 1976 Berkshire Hathaway Letter

## The Performance: A Dramatic Turnaround

After two dismal years, operating earnings improved significantly. In dollar terms: **$16,073,000** or **$16.47 per share** – a record.

But Buffett emphasizes return on equity as the key metric: **17.3%** on beginning shareholders' equity. This is "moderately above our long-term average and even further above the average of American industry" – though still below the 1972 record of 19.8%.

> "Last year we said the degree of progress in insurance underwriting would determine whether our gain in earnings would be 'moderate' or 'major'. As it turned out, earnings exceeded even the high end of our expectations. In large part, this was due to the outstanding efforts of Phil Liesche's managerial group at National Indemnity Company."

**Outlook for 1977:** Dollar operating earnings likely to improve somewhat, but return on equity "may decline a bit" from 1976.

## Textile Operations: A Disappointment

> "Our textile division was a significant disappointment during 1976. Earnings, measured either by return on sales or by return on capital employed, were inadequate."

Buffett gives two reasons:

1. **Industry conditions** did not measure up to expectations
2. **"Equally important were our own shortcomings."**

He details the problems with unusual candor:

> "Marketing efforts and mill capabilities were not properly matched in our new Waumbec operation. Unfavorable manufacturing cost variances were produced by improper evaluation of machinery and personnel capabilities."

But he praises Ken Chace: "as always, has been candid in reporting problems and has worked diligently to correct them. He is a pleasure to work with – even under difficult operating conditions."

**Outlook for 1977:** First quarter likely to be in the red, but by year-end textile earnings should equal or modestly exceed 1976. Buffett notes they are considering a "moderate-size acquisition" in textiles.

Yet he is realistic:

> "It should be recognized that the textile business does not offer the expectation of high returns on investment. Nevertheless, we maintain a commitment to this division – a very important source of employment in New Bedford and Manchester – and believe reasonable returns on average are possible."

## Insurance Underwriting: The Comeback

The property and casualty industry rebounded from disaster. The stockholder-owned portion had a combined ratio of 103.0 in 1976 (down from 108.3 in 1975 – lower is better, 100 is breakeven).

Berkshire is "unusually concentrated in auto lines," where stock companies improved from 113.5 to 107.4. But Berkshire's overall improvement was **even more dramatic – from 115.4 to 98.7.**

> "Our major sector ... had an outstanding year, achieving profit levels significantly better than the industry generally."

Buffett credits Phil Liesche, "aided particularly by Roland Miller in Underwriting and Bill Lyons in Claims."

**Reinsurance:** Also improved dramatically after "terrible results" in 1975, though still producing a modest underwriting loss. George Young is making "excellent progress."

**Home-state operation:** Continued excellent progress, with substantial premium growth. The combined ratio in 1976 was under 100 – meaning underwriting profit.

**Home & Automobile (Cook County, Chicago):** After a management change, John Seward "has made real progress." The 1975 combined ratio of 133.7 dropped to 109.6 in 1976 – still a loss, but moving in the right direction.

**Overall insurance outlook for 1977:** The industry may have some underwriting profit, but not necessarily Berkshire's segments. However, "we expect another significant gain in operating earnings from insurance in 1977."

## Insurance Investments: The Washington Post and a Permanent Portfolio

Buffett notes that they prefer a bond portfolio with current market value below carrying value – because that means higher rates are available on new investments.

Realized capital gains in 1976: **$9,962,000** before tax, primarily from stocks. 1977 also expected to be a year of net realized gains.

But the key is the stock portfolio: **unrealized gains of $45.7 million at year-end.** (It has since declined about $5 million as of March 21, 1977.)

> "We consider such market fluctuations from year to year relatively unimportant; ... we consider the yearly business progress of the companies in which we own stocks to be very important. And here, we have been delighted by the 1976 business performance achieved by most of our portfolio companies."

Note the **GEICO** holdings: both convertible preferred and common. This would become one of Buffett's most famous investments – and soon a major story.

**Buffett's four criteria for equity investments** (a classic statement):

1. Favorable long-term economic characteristics
2. Competent and honest management
3. Purchase price attractive when measured against value to a private owner
4. An industry with which we are familiar and whose long-term business characteristics we feel competent to judge

> "It is difficult to find investments meeting such a test, and that is one reason for our concentration of holdings. We simply can't find one hundred different securities that conform to our investment requirements."

He also notes that **Kaiser Industries** was a shorter-term holding – purchased after the company announced a distribution plan. (This shows he's not dogmatic about "permanent" holdings; he will take shorter-term special situations when they appear.)

## Banking: Eugene Abegg's Unmatched Record

> "Eugene Abegg ... continues to lead the parade among bankers – just as he has ever since he opened the bank in 1931."

The bank achieved its outstanding earnings record while:

1. Paying maximum rates on all consumer savings instruments (time deposits now over two-thirds of deposit base)
2. Maintaining outstanding liquidity (Federal funds sold plus short-term government issues approximately equal to demand deposits)
3. Avoiding high-yield second-class loans – net loan losses in 1976: **$12,000, or 0.02%** of outstanding loans – a "very tiny fraction" of industry average

> "Cost control is an important factor in the bank's success. Employment is still at about the level existing at the time of purchase in 1969 despite growth in consumer time deposits from $30 million to $90 million and considerable expansion in other activities."

That is extraordinary productivity.

## Blue Chip Stamps: Increased Interest

Berkshire increased its interest to about **33%** of Blue Chip Stamps' outstanding shares. Buffett urges shareholders to obtain Blue Chip's annual reports.

## Miscellaneous: K&W Products and the Bank Divestiture

K&W Products, a small subsidiary, performed well in its first year.

**Bank divestiture requirement:** Due to bank holding company regulations, Berkshire must divest its bank by December 31, 1980 (the letter says "less than four years remaining"). Buffett intends a spin-off of bank shares in 1980.

**Merger with Diversified Retailing:** Still hoped for, but unlikely in 1977.

## How Buffett's Mind Works (1976 Edition)

**1. He is as candid about his own mistakes as about industry problems.**
The textile division's shortcomings: "our own shortcomings." "Marketing efforts and mill capabilities were not properly matched." He doesn't blame the industry entirely.

**2. He has formalized his investment criteria.**
The four-point list for equity investments is a masterpiece of clarity. It could be printed as a wallet card for any value investor.

**3. He distinguishes between price fluctuations and business performance.**
"Unrealized gains have declined by about $5 million as this is written" – but he doesn't care. He cares about the business progress of his portfolio companies. The market will eventually follow.

**4. He is unafraid of concentration.**
"One reason for our concentration of holdings. We simply can't find one hundred different securities that conform." He'd rather have a few great ideas than many mediocre ones.

**5. He loves managers like Eugene Abegg.**
The bank's employment hasn't grown while time deposits tripled. That's efficiency. He admires it deeply.

**6. He complies with regulations without whining.**
The bank must be divested by 1980. He states it plainly and says he will do it "in a manner that minimizes disruption" – not complaining about government overreach.

## The Best Lines

> "He is a pleasure to work with – even under difficult operating conditions."

This is high praise for Ken Chace. Buffett values managers who are candid, hardworking, and pleasant – not just brilliant.

> "We simply can't find one hundred different securities that conform to our investment requirements."

A quiet rebuke to over-diversifiers.

> "Cost control is an important factor in the bank's success. Employment is still at about the level existing at the time of purchase in 1969 despite growth in consumer time deposits from $30 million to $90 million."

That single sentence explains why Abegg is a legend.

## The Key Quote

> "We select such investments on a long-term basis, weighing the same factors as would be involved in the purchase of 100% of an operating business: (1) favorable long-term economic characteristics; (2) competent and honest management; (3) purchase price attractive when measured against the yardstick of value to a private owner; and (4) an industry with which we are familiar and whose long-term business characteristics we feel competent to judge."

This is the Buffett investment philosophy boiled down to four bullets. Every Berkshire shareholder should memorize it.

But the quote that best captures the letter's triumphant tone:

> "Our own overall improvement was even more dramatic, from 115.4 to 98.7."

After two years of pain, the insurance underwriting combined ratio dropped below 100 – into profitability. The cycle had turned. And Buffett's patience and discipline had been vindicated.

## Transition Note

1976 was the comeback year. Insurance underwriting returned to profit. The stock portfolio was laden with unrealized gains. The four-point investment criteria were articulated in classic form. And Berkshire's book value continued to compound.

But hidden in the equity table was a ticking time bomb – and a massive opportunity. GEICO, the Government Employees Insurance Company, was in deep trouble. Buffett's convertible preferred and common shares would soon be tested. The 1976 letter is calm, but the storm was coming. And Buffett would respond not by selling – but by doubling down.
