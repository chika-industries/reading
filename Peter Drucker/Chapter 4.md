# Summary of Chapter 4: Knowledge Is All

## Key Concepts

**The New Reality**
- Knowledge is now the key resource in society
- Knowledge workers are the dominant group in the workforce
- Three main characteristics of the knowledge economy:
  1. **Borderlessness** – knowledge travels more easily than money
  2. **Upward mobility** – available to everyone through formal education
  3. **Potential for failure as well as success** – not everyone wins

**Information Technology’s Role**
- Allows knowledge to spread nearly instantly and become accessible to everyone
- Every institution must be globally competitive, even if local in operations
- Customers everywhere can find out what is available anywhere in the world and at what price

---

## The New Workforce

**Decline of Manual Work**
- Century ago: overwhelming majority worked with their hands (farms, domestic service, factories)
- 50 years later: manual workers dropped to ~50% of American labor force
- Today: less than 25% of American workers make living from manual jobs
- Factory workers now ~15% of total workforce (down from 35% at peak)

**Rise of Knowledge Workers**
- Term coined around 1960 by Fritz Machlup and Peter Drucker
- Now account for 1/3 of American workforce, outnumbering factory workers 2 to 1
- Within 15–20 years, likely to reach nearly 2/5 of workforce in all rich countries

**Knowledge Technologists**
- Fastest-growing segment: computer technicians, software designers, paralegals, clinical lab analysts, manufacturing technologists
- Do much manual work but based on theoretical knowledge from formal education
- See themselves as “professionals,” not workers
- Will likely become dominant social and political force in coming decades

---

## Knowledge Workers as the New Capitalists

**Own the Means of Production**
- Knowledge workers collectively own the means of production—their knowledge
- Through pension funds and mutual funds, they are also majority shareholders in many large businesses

**Specialization and Organization**
- Effective knowledge is specialized
- Knowledge workers need access to organizations that bring together diverse specialties
- They see themselves as “professionals” or “associates,” not subordinates
- The knowledge society is a society of seniors and juniors, not bosses and subordinates

---

## Women in Knowledge Work

**Knowledge Work Is Unisex**
- First modern knowledge jobs were sex-segregated: teaching (men), nursing (women)
- By 1850 teaching became unisex; today 2/5 of U.S. nursing students are men
- Knowledge workers, whatever their sex, are professionals governed by same standards and judged by same results

**Knowledge Technologists and Women**
- Jobs well-adapted to women’s needs as childbearers and to increasing longevity
- Women now outnumber men in American higher education
- Can take time out for children and return to full-time work

---

## Needs of Knowledge Workers

**Two Main Needs**
1. **Formal education** to enter knowledge work
2. **Continuing education** throughout working life to keep knowledge up to date

**Continuing Education**
- Knowledge rapidly becomes obsolete
- Knowledge workers must regularly go back to school
- Will be delivered through nontraditional means: weekend seminars, online training, etc.
- Information revolution will have enormous impact on continuing education

---

## Identity and Loyalty

**Identify with Knowledge, Not Employer**
- Knowledge workers introduce themselves by specialty (“I am an anthropologist”)
- They “work at” an organization but do not “belong to” it
- Feel more in common with specialists in other institutions than with colleagues in different knowledge areas
- Primary allegiance is to their specialized branch of knowledge

**Mobility**
- Knowledge workers are highly mobile within their specialty
- Move between universities, companies, countries as long as they stay in same field
- Efforts to restore loyalty to employing organization will likely fail

---

## Nonhierarchical Nature of Knowledge

- Knowledge is either relevant or not—hierarchy doesn’t matter
- A speech therapist’s knowledge may be more relevant than a surgeon’s in a given situation
- Knowledge workers expect to be treated as professionals, not subordinates

**Motivation**
- Money is important but not the ultimate yardstick
- Cannot substitute for professional performance and achievement
- Most knowledge workers see their job as a *life*, not just a living

---

## Upward Mobility and Its Price

**Unlimited Upward Mobility**
- Knowledge cannot be inherited or bequeathed; every individual starts with equal ignorance
- Knowledge must be teachable and public, making it universally accessible
- First human society with potentially unlimited upward mobility

**Price of Success**
- Psychological pressures and emotional traumas of competition
- Winners require losers—unlike earlier societies
- Creates hostility to learning in countries like Japan, where exam pressures are intense
- Threatens to turn societies into plutocracies where only wealthy can afford university preparation

**Coping with the Rat Race**
- Many successful knowledge workers “plateau” in their forties
- Need to develop noncompetitive life and serious outside interests (volunteer work, music, local government) while still young
- Gives opportunity for personal contribution and achievement beyond work
