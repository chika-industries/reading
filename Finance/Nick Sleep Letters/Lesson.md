
## From Cigar Butts to Permanent Holdings

In the early letters (2001-2004), Sleep was deep in:
- Distressed turnarounds (Xerox, Stagecoach, Conseco – which failed)
- Deep value asset plays (Philippines cement at 1/4 replacement cost)
- Hated, temporary problems (Primedia, Lucent preferreds)

He called these "cigar butts" – one puff left, then discard.

But gradually, he realized that **the real wealth came from owning extraordinary businesses for decades**. By the 2009-2013 letters, the portfolio had transformed:

- **Costco** (scale efficiencies shared – 5:1 customer-to-shareholder benefit ratio)
- **Amazon** (same model, plus scalable internet skeleton)
- **Berkshire Hathaway** (the ultimate permanent holding, run by Buffett/Munger)
- **AirAsia** (Asian low-cost leader)
- **Asos** (online fashion)

And he stopped trading. The 2013 interim letter proudly announced: purchase and sale transactions have "all but ground to a halt." Charlie Munger's line became his mantra: **"You make your real money sitting on your assets."**

## The Lesson He Learned the Hard Way

Sleep admits his biggest mistake was **selling Stagecoach too early** (sold at 90p, it went to £2.50+). That taught him: if you own a great business with a durable advantage, don't trade it. The opportunity cost of selling a compounder far exceeds any loss from a bankruptcy.

By the final letter, he says flatly: "All Zak and I have done is catch some better waves and try to ride them to the shore."

## Why Simplicity Wins

The portfolio became **simpler, not more complex**, because:
- Great businesses are rare (his "super high-quality thinkers" list was ~15 names globally)
- Once you find them, you don't need many
- Activity is the enemy of returns (fees, taxes, mistakes)
- Patience is the only sustainable competitive advantage

So yes – the journey was from **many complicated, cheap, temporary ideas** to **a few simple, wonderful, permanent ones**. That's the master lesson of the Nomad letters.
