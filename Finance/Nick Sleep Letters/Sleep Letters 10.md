## Mental Shortcuts & The Zimbabwe Panelist

Sleep recounts a scene at Richard Zeckhauser's Harvard behavioural finance course. A panelist was asked: "One of our participants is buying Zimbabwean securities priced at a small percentage of net asset value. Would you try to invest with him?" The panelist replied: **"No!"** (exclamation mark original)

The panelist made up his mind the moment he saw the word "Zimbabwe" — a mental shortcut that blocked rational analysis. Charlie Munger: "The human mind is a lot like the human egg" — once one sperm enters, all others are locked out.

Sleep notes that most car advertising is read **after** the purchase decision, only to gather information to convince others the decision was rational. "We have all done it."

## Why Are Internet Retailers Growing "Slowly"?

An odd question: internet retailing has no physical constraints (store size, location, opening hours). So why aren't online retailers growing faster than Wal-Mart did at a similar stage?

Sleep's hunch: **consumer psychology**. We are creatures of habit. It takes time to replace comfort blankets — buying books online first, then trainers, then groceries. The process is a "drift" not an epiphany. Growth is regulated not by physical capacity but by the rate at which incumbent habits are replaced with more rational behaviour.

## The Wrong Models

Traditional retail growth models assume growth slows with time (regression to the mean). But if online growth is a function of attitude change rather than asset constraints, a firm being "quite large already" doesn't mean growth will slow.

Two years of 40% revenue growth doubles revenues in 24 months — regression-to-the-mean estimates would be off by a factor of two. "Although some online retailing firms may be quite large, they may also be quite young."

## Principal-Agent Conflict & Loss of Trust

Sleep laments the state of industry governance. Rules, regulation, and "due diligence" have replaced trust — draining the life out of the innocent. "Trust, once the cornerstone of the industry... has been all but lost."

He shares an exchange between a star fund manager and an analyst:

- **Analyst:** "If you did not own company X and company Y, your performance would have been only average."
- **Manager:** "But that's the point, I did!"

The industry's cynicism is "no substitute for wisdom." What's required is the rebuilding of a "web of deserved trust."
