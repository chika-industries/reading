# Summary of Nomad Investment Partnership's Fourth Year (2005)

## Performance
- **First half 2005:** +2.4% (vs MSCI World -0.3%)
- **2005 calendar year:** +9.2% (vs MSCI World +9.5%) — essentially matched the index
- **Since inception (Sept 2001 to Dec 2005):** +167.2% before fees, +21.4% annualized after fees (vs index +38.8% cumulative, +7.9% annualized)

Sleep notes that the chance of Nomad's results being so close to the index given the portfolio's concentration was "once in a long lifetime (approximately one year in seventy)." He warns against extrapolating the early 25%+ annualized returns.

## Key Conceptual Contributions

### The Robustness Ratio
Sleep introduces a framework to measure a company's moat: the ratio of customer savings to shareholder profits.

- **GEICO:** $1 saved by customers for every $1 earned by shareholders → 1:1 ratio
- **Costco:** ~$5 saved by customers for every $1 earned by shareholders → 5:1 ratio

The higher the ratio, the wider the moat and the harder to compete. Sleep argues Costco's moat is "unassailable" — for Sam's Club to match Costco's prices would cost ~$1.4 billion annually, plus another ~$750 million to match wages.

### Three Competitive Advantages in Investing
Citing Bill Miller, Sleep lists:
1. **Informational** (knowing something others don't) — largely gone
2. **Analytical** (cutting up public information differently) — valuable
3. **Psychological/behavioral** — the most enduring

He elaborates on psychological traps: social proof (following the crowd), availability (overweighting vivid evidence), poor probability-based thinking, and impatience.

### Zimbabwe as a Second Investment Model
Nomad began investing in Zimbabwe, described as "deep discount to replacement cost with latent pricing power."

- Zimcem (cement producer): replacement cost US$70-100m, market price at 1/70th of that
- Valued using Old Mutual arbitrage (Johannesburg/Harare dual listing) rather than official exchange rate
- Risks include nationalisation, but Sleep notes: "the investment case rests in your manager being fifty years younger than Bob Mugabe"

### Price-to-Value Ratio Over Performance
Sleep urges Partners to focus on the price-to-value ratio (68 cents on the dollar at mid-2005) rather than short-term performance. He cites neuroscience: immediate rewards activate the limbic (survival) system; stress overrides analytical thinking.

## Mistake Analysis (Learning from Conseco and Stagecoach)
Sleep admits two major analytical errors:

- **Conseco:** Anchored on original analysis, failed to evolve as facts changed → denial and ego
- **Stagecoach:** Sold at ~90p; shares later reached >£2.50 → opportunity cost ~US$12m

The lessons learned (destination analysis, avoiding denial) generated ~US$60m in gains during 2007. Mistakes become "net present value positive" if you learn from them.

## Berkshire Hathaway AGM Anecdote
Buffett declined a bet where he could win $10,000 but lose only $10. His reasoning: "If you are not disciplined in the little things, you won't be disciplined in the big things." Sleep uses this to illustrate the danger of "drift."

## Housekeeping
- Northern Trust appointed as custodian
- Performance fee amendment proposed: high-water mark also compounds at 6% per annum (rather than static)
- Sleep contemplates ability to shrink the Partnership and return capital when opportunity set is small

## Key Quote on Destination vs. Journey
> "If we could turn US$1 into US$16, does it matter if it takes 18 years or 22 years? There is a difference in the annual compound rate... but securing the destination is also important."

He notes Nomad was at ~US$2.70, aiming for US$16 after 20 years.
