# Summary of Nomad Investment Partnership's Fifth Year (2006)

## Performance
- **2006 calendar year:** +13.6% (vs MSCI World +20.1%) — underperformed the index
- **Since inception (Sept 2001 to Dec 2006):** +203.2% before fees, +20.4% annualized after fees (vs index +70.2% cumulative, +10.6% annualized)
- **First half 2006:** +2.4% (vs index -0.3%) — interim period before handover

Sleep notes that 2006 was the first full year where Nomad meaningfully lagged the index. He is unfazed: "The index is not our benchmark. Zak and I spend no time at all thinking about the index."

## Major Structural Change: Independence from Marathon
In March 2006, Sleep and Zakaria resigned from Marathon to form **Sleep, Zakaria and Company, Ltd.** The handover completed on September 12, 2006.

Key features of the new independent structure:
- **Cost-reimbursement management fee** (capped at 1% of NAV, currently ~0.7-1%, designed to decline as assets grow)
- **Refundable performance fee** with a contingent performance reserve (CPR): fees go into a bucket, available to refund to Partners in subsequent years of underperformance. The 6% annual hurdle resets each year (not compounded since subscription).
- Ability to **return capital to Partners** if opportunity set shrinks
- Legal lock preventing sale of Nomad to another manager

Sleep emphasizes: "You can sack us, but we won't sell you."

## The Benchmark Discussion
Sleep argues that viewing an index as "risk-free" is a mistake that leads to:
- Requirement to have an opinion on everything inside the index
- Unwillingness to invest in better opportunities outside it
- Over-diversification

He quotes Keynes: "Worldly wisdom teaches that it is better for reputation to fail conventionally than to succeed unconventionally."

## The Equity Yield Curve
Sleep reintroduces the concept: business outcomes can be **more predictable** several years out than in the near term. Returns can be both increased and de-risked by time.

Evidence from Nomad's portfolio: stocks held >4 years had superior annualized returns compared to those held 3-4 years, and so on. The average holding period of other investors in Nomad's stocks (excluding Berkshire and Jardine) is **twenty weeks**. Nomad expects to own them for ~260 weeks.

## Amazon.com Analysis
Sleep uses Amazon to illustrate the "scale efficiencies shared" model. Jeff Bezos' philosophy: lowering prices creates a virtuous cycle, even though short-term math says raise prices.

Key Bezos quote from the letter: "Advertising is the price you pay for having an unremarkable product or service."

Sleep estimates Amazon's discretionary investment spending (over maintenance) at ~$500m annually. The market at times valued the business as if that growth spending had no value.

## Locker Room Culture & Principal-Agent Conflict
Sleep criticizes the "locker room culture" — win at any cost, short-term orientation. He argues that when mutual funds seek short-term performance, they attract companies that also manage for short-term earnings. "Schlock begets schlock."

He revisits the Weetabix takeover (December 2003 letter): fund managers sell cheaply to private equity rather than defend shareholder interests. Nomad became part of a dissenting group at **Whitehead Mann**, owning ~19% — enough to block compulsory acquisition.

## Non-Transitive Dice
Sleep uses magic dice (where A beats B, B beats C, but C beats A) to illustrate:
- Any superiority in an investment process only emerges with time → patience is important
- The stock market's daily prices reveal information — investors can take the offer or wait
