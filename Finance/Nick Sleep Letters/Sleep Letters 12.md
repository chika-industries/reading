## David Attenborough & Charles Darwin on Thinking vs Data Collection

Sleep recounts a Desert Island Discs interview with David Attenborough. Kirsty Young noted Attenborough had "seen more of the world than any person who has ever lived." Attenborough's humble response:

> "I suppose so... but then on the other hand it is fairly salutary to remember that perhaps the greatest naturalist that ever lived and had more effect on our thinking than anybody, Charles Darwin, only spent four years travelling and the rest of the time thinking."

The lesson: the human mind trumps endless data collection. In today's information-soaked world, many professionals hardly have time to think at all. Sleep and Zakaria try to take Darwin's approach: de-emphasise data collection and **think**.

## Timeless Business Models

The business model that built Ford a hundred years ago (chart from 1927 showing production volumes rising and costs falling) is the same that built Wal-Mart in the 1970s, Southwest Airlines in the 1990s, and Amazon today. It will build empires in the future too.

Bezos: "I can't imagine that in ten years from now customers are going to say: I really love Amazon, but I wish their prices were a little higher" or less convenient, or less selection.

## Information as Food (JP Rangaswami's Analogy)

Sleep presents a provocative framework: think about information the same way we think about food.

**Cultivation:** Hunter-gatherers (free to roam) vs farmers (put up fences, define ownership) — the same applies to information (patent and copyright law).

**Preparation:** Distilled nutrition (a conclusion served to you) vs smorgasbord (raw data to mix yourself).

**Quality standards:** Food has labelling (fat content, ingredients). Information often does not — fact and fiction can be deliberately fused ("docudramas", "mocumentaries").

**Over-consumption:** Clay Shirky: "There is no such animal as information overload, only filter failure." Sleep notes that information, like food, has a sell-by date — next quarter's earnings are worthless after next quarter.

The information Sleep and Zakaria weigh most heavily is that with the longest shelf life — "almost axiomatic" — because it is the most valuable.

## Management Fee Update

Nomad's cost-reimbursement management fee is approaching **fewer than ten basis points** per annum. Sleep notes:

- Started at ~50bps six years ago
- Now close to 10bps
- Saving to Partners (mainly charities and endowments) compared to industry standard 1% fee: **~US$15m per annum** (or >US$30m vs hedge fund standard)

> "We mention this to pop the illusion that investment management is a Giffen good (one where, paradoxically, demand rises with price)."

Sleep argues that lowering management fees across the industry would improve aggregate behaviour.

## Reopening & Quick Closure

Nomad reopened for subscriptions in early 2012. In contrast to October 2008 ("you could have heard a pin drop"), this time demand was such that within two weeks they had to close again.

Sleep notes: "Zak and I do not like being open, and we are happy with our hermit-like state, minding our own business and getting the job done."
