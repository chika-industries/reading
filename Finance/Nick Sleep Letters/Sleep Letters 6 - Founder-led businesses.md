## Santa Fe Institute & Complexity Science

Sleep describes Nomad's membership in the Santa Fe Institute (SFI), founded by Murray Gell-Mann and Los Alamos scientists. SFI studies "complex adaptive systems" — economies that evolve as agents learn, contrasting with traditional economics' static, rational utility-maximizing framework.

Key insights:
- **Geoff West's scaling laws:** All mammals share ~1 billion heartbeats. Mice use them in 4 years (500 beats/min), elephants in 70 years (25 beats/min). This is due to circulatory efficiency scaling with size.
- **Application to business:** A firm's "skeletal structure" must support growth from mouse to elephant without re-engineering. Internet retailing (Amazon) has a far more scalable structure than high street retailing — fixed customer experience, no expensive real estate, controllable quality.

## Scaling & Amazon

Sleep argues that Amazon's simple, scalable "skeletal structure" combined with scale-efficiencies-shared and founder orientation makes it a "mouse that can turn into elephant." He asks: what was e-commerce as % of US retail last year? (Answer: 2.7-3.1%). What will it be in 10 years?

He admits that the largest error an investor can make is selling a Wal-Mart or Microsoft early. "Would selling Amazon today be the equivalent mistake of selling Wal-Mart in 1980?"

With ~1/6 of the Partnership in Amazon, results will be more volatile — but that doesn't bother him.

## Deep Reality of Markets

The portfolio is now >85% invested in **founder-managed or first-generation management** businesses. Sleep notes this was not planned — "We feel slightly foolish for not recognizing this trait in advance."

The principal-agent conflict between founders (long-term) and trading-oriented fund managers (short-term) is "the deep reality of the markets and probably the dominant characteristic of our careers."

## Future Capital Raising

Sleep proposes that any future capital raising would be via **redeemable shares** — giving Nomad the option to shrink and return crisis capital after the opportunity passes. He welcomes Partners' thoughts.

## Mistakes & Learning (Annual Letter)

The 2007 annual letter focuses extensively on mistakes:

**How to think about mistakes:** The best state is "non-judgmental forgiveness." If a company learns from its mistake, the stock price should rise after a mistake. But investors rarely forgive — they presume companies don't learn.

**MDC vs Bank X:** Both made property lending mistakes in early 1990s.
- MDC (Larry Mizel) learned: kept land bank minimal, used debt reluctantly, retained equity. Shares rose 10x.
- Bank X misdiagnosed: concluded "could not do commercial property" (output mistake, not input). Diversified internationally into investment banking. Shares rose ~1% per annum.

Sleep argues diversification can be dangerous: "If knowledge is a source of value added, and few things can be known for sure, then owning more stocks does not lower risk but raises it."

**Nomad's biggest mistakes:**
- Conseco: $5m loss, $10m opportunity cost — anchored on original analysis
- Stagecoach: sold at 90p, later >£2.50 — opportunity cost ~$12m and counting

**Lessons learned:** Destination analysis (not static price-to-value), avoiding denial and ego. The lessons generated ~$60m in 2007 gains — "a return on prior year losses."

## Say-Something Syndrome & Disclosure

Sleep quotes Walter Schloss: telling a nervous partner about holdings (bankrupt rail bonds) caused the partner to withdraw — and die a year later.

He questions whether disclosing holdings is wise, but continues to do so (under separate post) for curious partners who study investing "through a joy for the art."

## Ignatius Loyola's Prayer

> "Give me the strength to accept that which I cannot change, The courage to change that which I can, And the wisdom to tell the difference."

Sleep applies this to Nomad: they control research quality (which determines long-term success) but not annual performance. He asks Partners to focus on inputs, not outputs.
