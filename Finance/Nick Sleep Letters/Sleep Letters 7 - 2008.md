# Summary of Nomad Investment Partnership's Seventh Year (2008)

## Performance

**First half 2008:** -20.8% (vs MSCI World -10.6%)

**2008 calendar year:** -45.3% (vs MSCI World -40.7%)

**Since inception (Sept 2001 to Dec 2008):** +101.1% before fees, +7.1% annualized after fees (vs index +7.7% cumulative, +1.0% annualized)

A dollar invested at inception was worth ~$2.93 before fees after the first half, ending the year at ~$2.01 before fees. The refundable performance fee reserve was refunded to Partners (gain of ~2.5% of NAV) — "Zak and I look at our shoes as we are asked 'let me get this straight: you paid money back to your clients?'"

Sleep notes: five-year returns were "all but zero" but trough-to-trough (2001 to 2008) Nomad doubled while the index did nothing. "It may not feel like it but, in many respects, these are the best of times for an investor."

## The Crisis & "Slack"

Sleep argues the crisis revealed a lack of "slack" in the system — optimization over resilience. Taleb: "Capitalism does not teach slack, it teaches optimisation."

Examples of no slack:
- Itineraries filled with meetings
- Borrowing capacity fully used
- Cash "on the couch" considered sinful
- Too busy to think → "stupidly busy schedule means you have become insulated from the world"

Charlie Munger's advice on getting rich: "wait for an opportunity and invest the spare million dollars you have lying around." Slack cash is worth more than cash.

## Dilution Risk (Critical Concept)

Sleep explains why cheap stocks can stay cheap:

A firm worth $2bn (60m shares = $33/share). Value buyer buys at $15 (47c on dollar). But if firm issues 30m new shares at $10 to fund $300m losses, intrinsic value falls to $22 → purchase at 68c on dollar. Worse if capital raised at $5 or $1.

Institutions are "weak-minded" — they won't backstop capital raisings. Short sellers know this and create doubt to force dilutive placings.

MBIA sold for this reason: "however cheap MBIA may appear, the prospect of a future capital raising means one cannot place a valuation without knowing the number of shares outstanding."

## Where Have All the Long-Term Investors Gone?

Sleep blames the ascendancy of marketing and risk management over investment. Average holding period for US stocks in Nomad's portfolio (ex-Berkshire): **51 days** (they expect ~1,300 days).

Jack Bogle quote: "What went wrong was a pathological mutation in capitalism — from traditional owners' capitalism to a new and virulent managers' capitalism."

Charlie Munger noted the English Bubble Act (1720-1825) banned share issuance entirely — and Britain set the stage for the Industrial Revolution. "It is not clear we need to trade shares to be successful."

## Momentum Market & Giffen Goods

Current period is the "greatest momentum market since the dot com era" — stocks rise because they have risen (a "Giffen good" in economics). Scott McNealy (Sun Microsystems) 2002 interview quoted: "At ten times revenues... would any of you like to buy my stock at U$64? Do you realize how ridiculous those basic assumptions are?"

Sleep notes Potash Corporation now has similar valuation with six-week holding periods.

## IFRS 7 & Rules-Based Thinking

Accounting rules (IFRS 7) require describing how risks are managed over the next 12 months — meaningless for 5-year investors. "The accounting tries to bend Nomad out of shape."

Sleep contrasts **Drachten** (Netherlands), which removed traffic signals to make drivers responsible, with **Coventry**, which built a roundabout with "no exit" signs before every exit. "There are two routes: Drachten or Coventry."

## Reopening of Nomad

Despite few redemptions, also few subscriptions (Zak and I were 2nd and 3rd largest in October!). Opportunity set remains very favourable → Nomad will remain open for subscriptions.
