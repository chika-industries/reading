## The Darwin Approach to Information

Sleep opens the interim letter with a long quote from Charles Darwin's "On the Origin of Species" — emphasizing Darwin's humility, his admission of imperfection, and his willingness to state opposing facts. The key insight: **truths are often spoken with a whispered voice, while shaky suppositions are shouted.**

He applies this to Nomad's portfolio: Amazon, Costco, Berkshire, Games Workshop don't advertise; they don't provide earnings guidance; they give back margin to customers. Jeff Bezos: "Advertising is the price you pay for having an unremarkable product or service." GM spent $5.3bn on advertising in 2008 ($630 per car) — enough to retire half its debt.

## Scale Economics Shared Works in Recessions

Despite the crisis:
- Amazon's revenues up 60% since mid-2007
- AirAsia's revenues per seat/km up 33%, costs ex-fuel down 10%
- Weighted average revenue growth of Nomad's firms >10% organic

Sleep argues that hyper-efficient low-cost providers often take **permanent market share** during downturns. Sam Walton when asked about the early 1990s recession: "I've thought about it and decided not to participate."

## Why Great Companies Stay Cheap for Decades

Sleep presents a powerful chart showing Wal-Mart's share price (blue) vs the price one could have paid and still earned 10% annually (red). The red line is far above the blue line for decades.

**Key insight:** In 1972, an investor could have paid over 150x earnings (a P/E of 1,500!) and still earned 10% through to today. Ten years later, could have paid over 200x earnings. The market persistently undervalued Wal-Mart's longevity.

Why? Investors mis-weigh information:
- They focus on transitory items (margin trends, quarterly growth rates)
- They use the wrong mental model (the "Barbie problem" — one-hit wonders)
- They don't believe in "longevity of compound" (Empirical Research: only 1 in 5 growth stocks keep that status for 5 years; 1 in 10 for 10 years)

But a few exceptional companies — with scale economics shared and strong culture — are different. The market applies the average failure probability to all stocks, including those that won't fail. This creates decades of cheapness.

## The Fund Management Industry's Failure

No major fund management organization got rich owning Wal-Mart all the way through. Sleep recounts a story: a large firm analyzed its portfolio, realized selling IBM 30 years earlier was a huge error. They vowed to learn. Then they sold their Wal-Mart stake — which 30 years later would be worth more than their entire funds under management.

Reasons for this mistake:
1. Misanalysis (using Barbie model for Wal-Mart)
2. Structural (managers need to look active; short-term horizons; marketing needs new stories)
3. Odds (assuming good things don't last — true on average, but not for exceptional firms)

Seth Klarman on Buffett: each day Buffett chose not to do anything was a decision taken too.

## The Paradox of Choice (Annual Letter)

Sleep references Barry Schwartz's "The Paradox of Choice": too much choice creates expectation of perfection, leading to disappointment. The stock market has tens of thousands of investments changing price constantly — "it is very easy to feel unhappy about one's investments." There will always be someone who did better.

A Ferrari 250 GTO recently sold for US$52m — 50-year annual return over 20%. "There is always something going on that makes Nomad's performance look a bit weedy!"
