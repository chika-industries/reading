# Summary of Nomad Investment Partnership's Third Year (2004)

## Performance
- **First half 2004:** -0.97% (vs MSCI World +3.52%)
- **2004 calendar year:** +22.1% (vs index +14.7%)
- **Since inception (Sept 2001 to Dec 2004):** +98.5% before fees, +23.14% annualized after fees (vs index +14.37% cumulative, +4.85% annualized)

Sleep notes that any resemblance to closet index trackers is "entirely coincidental"—Nomad's portfolio is nothing like the index.

## Partnership Governance & Growth
- Partnership closed (considered "natural state") with ~US$100m AUM
- Sleep emphasizes: "Job one, two and three is investment performance, not asset gathering"
- Future reopenings will only occur during market stress—he trusts Partners to be contrarian (they drew down the queue within 48 hours when asked)
- Estimated price-to-value ratio: 61 cents on the dollar (down from 73c at end of 2003)

## Portfolio Concentration & Kelly Criterion
Sleep revisits the Kelly Criterion (2.1p - 1.1), arguing:
- If 75% certain of being right, correct weighting is 47.5% of portfolio
- Early Buffett Partnership had this level of concentration
- Industry overdiversification is "more about marketing" than investment excellence
- Owning many stocks dilutes attention to corporate governance

## Influence on Investee Companies
Sleep describes how large shareholders can drive change:
- **Jardine Matheson:** Brandes and Marathon proposed reorganization; watered-down version succeeded
- **Kersaf (South Africa):** Allan Gray and Marathon turned strategy around; shares quadrupled in dollars
- **Hollinger International:** Tweedy Browne pursued Conrad Black; shares doubled
- **HCI (South Africa):** Marathon resisting management buyout at massive discount in court

## "Super High-Quality Thinkers"
Sleep keeps a list of ~15 businesses with intellectually honest, economically rational capital allocation over many years. This is the "terminal portfolio"—firms where shareholders could abdicate trading rights. But prices matter: even Berkshire was half-priced in early 2000.

## Deconstruction of Costco (Major section)
Sleep provides extensive analysis:
- Fixed 14% mark-up, EDLP strategy
- Scale efficiencies shared with customers
- Revenue per square foot ~$830 vs Sam's Club ~$500
- Mature stores earn 16.5% on assets; new stores earn 6%
- Normalized growth ~13% per annum
- Purchase price ~$39 discounts 5-6% growth; fair value for 10% growth would be ~$62
- "The consensus has it that Costco is a low-margin, expensive retailer with a cost problem. In our judgement it is a cost-disciplined, intellectually honest, high-product-integrity, perpetual motion machine."

## Risks of Public Commitment
Sleep includes an essay warning that discussing specific stocks publicly creates psychological "mental chains"—managers convince themselves and lose objectivity. Evangelism is not healthy.

## Key Quote
> "It is only in such a topsy-turvy world that a common-sense Investment Partnership, such as Nomad, can be seen as lunatic fringe... Nomad is a value investor in the sense that we like to buy stocks at half price. But the largest holdings would be described by most of our peers as growth stocks."
