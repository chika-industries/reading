## Learning from a Welsh Insurance Company

Sleep describes a research trip to a Welsh auto insurer with a decades-long track record of excellent results despite selling a commoditized product (price-driven, no customer love). When asked for the "secret sauce," the executive replied: "We don't do one thing brilliantly, we do many, many things slightly better than others."

Sleep admits he had dismissed such answers as "fig leaves" for years — but now believes he was wrong.

**The insight:** A competitive advantage need not be one big thing. An interlocking, self-reinforcing network of small actions can be more successful and more durable than a single patent or brand.

## Costco & The 1967 Memo

Sleep recounts meeting Costco CEO Jim Sinegal, who stopped mid-sentence to pull a memo from 1967 written by Sol Price (founder of Fed-Mart, Costco's predecessor). The memo said:

> "Margin must be obtained by better buying, emphasis on selling the kind of goods we want to sell, operating efficiencies, lower markdowns, greater turnover, etc. Increasing the retail prices... could lead to a rude awakening... Let us concentrate on how cheap we can bring things to the people, rather than how much the traffic will bear."

Sleep has the memo framed on his office wall. Forty-three years later, Costco is the most valuable retailer of its type in the world. Cultures that care about little things all the time are very hard to create — and almost impossible if not put in place at the firm's genesis.

## Amazon's 452 Detailed Goals

Bezos' 2010 shareholder letter: "We believe that focusing our energy on the controllable inputs to our business is the most effective way to maximise our financial outputs over time... For 2010, we have 452 detailed goals, with owners, deliverables and targeted completion dates."

One employee initiative to remove light bulbs from vending machines saves $20,000 per year.

## The Fragility of "One Big Thing" Businesses

**Drug company example:** A patent gives a legal monopoly. But the firm doesn't need to be good at marketing, manufacturing, or R&D. When a rival displaces it, there's little to fall back on. Period of exceptional profitability is finite.

**Scale economics business:** To beat an incumbent, a rival must be superior at a million little actions — far harder.

## The Subtle Implication for Long-Term Investors

Sleep argues that short-term investors spend their time guessing "which branch next" in a decision tree. But for exceptional businesses, success on one favorable branch increases the probability of success on the next branch, and the next — a virtuous feedback loop.

**Air Asia example:** 
- Born with no-frills cost culture (branch 1)
- Employees take pride and suggest savings (branch 2)
- Savings passed to customers as lower prices (branch 3)
- Customer reciprocates, revenues rise (branch 4)
- Further scale advantages (branch 5)
- Further customer reciprocation (branch 6)
- Network crowds out inefficient airlines (branch 7)
- Competitors go out of business (branch 8)

The odds improve as one travels from branch to branch. If investors recognize this inevitability, they are often indifferent — "outside their time horizon." The result: long-term success remains undiscounted, and rewards for the patient few are enormous.
