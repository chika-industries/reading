# Summary of Nomad Investment Partnership's Second Year (2003)

## Performance
- **2003 calendar year:** +79.6% (vs MSCI World +33.1%)
- **Since inception (Sept 2001 to Dec 2003):** +100.4% (vs index +10.5%)
- Annualized since inception: +34.7% (vs index +4.4%)

Sleep notes that much of this performance came from the decline in investor fear—junk bond spreads normalized, and hated, distressed businesses rebounded sharply.

## Key Developments

### Unlisted Equity Investment
In February 2003, Partners unanimously approved a policy change allowing investment in unlisted equity. This enabled a **sizeable investment in Weetabix Limited** (UK breakfast cereal manufacturer). The quick response allowed purchase at depressed prices, generating a 20% profit by June.

### New Investment Themes
- **Weetabix:** Family-controlled since 1932, no share issuance since the 1930s. Sleep valued it at ~£70/share vs purchase at ~£20. Later received a takeover bid from Hicks Muse at £53.75—Sleep voted against, calling the price too low.
- **Lucent 8% Redeemable Convertible Preferreds:** Purchased at $350 (par $1,000) with a put option in 2004. Sleep urged management to buy back shares/debt rather than issue equity—management chose dilution instead. The preferreds later tripled.

### Share Discipline Screen
Sleep asked an investment bank to screen for US companies with no change in shares outstanding over 10 years. Only seven firms with market cap >$50m met the criteria, including Erie Indemnity (no shares issued since 1925!) and Hershey Creamery (notoriously hard to get annual reports).

## Investment Philosophy Refinements

### Expected Returns Framework
Sleep articulates a model: buy at 50 cents on the dollar, assume 10% annual growth in intrinsic value, leading to ~26% annual returns. Mistakes tend to "atrophy" rather than collapse. Overall portfolio target: ~13% compounded annually.

### Portfolio Concentration (Kelly Criterion)
Introduces the Kelly Criterion (2.1p - 1.1) to argue that high-conviction ideas should be very large weights. Sleep admits Nomad's concentration has at times been **too low**, and industry overdiversification is "more about marketing than investment excellence."

### Principal-Agent Conflict
Sleep emphasizes that owning >10% of a company allows influence over outcomes (e.g., blocking low-ball takeovers). Small holdings are "de minimus" and powerless.

## Weetabix Takeover Controversy
Hicks Muse bid £53.75/share. Sleep wrote to the Chairman, voted against, and argued:
- The price was ~25% too low
- Private equity buyers will flip it—natural competitors would pay more
- The "Scheme of Arrangement" (75% approval threshold) is a "ruthless method" to seize property from fragmented shareholders
- Bad practice spreads

Despite this, the deal proceeded. Sleep noted: "In no other sphere of capitalism can your property be seized in exchange for cents on the dollar."

## Partnership Growth & Closure Policy
The Partnership had grown 5x since inception, mostly from new subscriptions. Sleep commits to closing when:
- Cannot invest a unit size
- Marketing time exceeds 1% of working year
- Client quality is diluted

He prefers growth to be "investment-led," not marketing-led.

## Key Quote on Performance
> "It is quite possible that our annual results will be inferior to the market for a period, but this will only convey information about the timing of outcomes, whilst saying little about the end result itself."
