# Summary of Nomad Investment Partnership's 2013 Calendar Year (Annual Letter to December 31, 2013)

## Performance

- **2013 calendar year:** +62.2% (vs MSCI World +26.7%)
- **Since inception (Sept 10, 2001 to Dec 2013):** +921.1% before fees, +18.4% annualized after fees (vs index +116.9% cumulative, +6.5% annualized)

A dollar invested at inception was worth **$10.21 before fees** as of January 1, 2014, vs $2.17 for the index. The 14% annual advantage over 12+ years turned $1 into $10.21.

*This would be the final annual letter before the fund's closure in 2014.*

## Who Really Creates the Wealth

Sleep makes a striking admission:

> "It appears to all the world that the performance detailed above has been created by Zak and me. That is not the case... the performance that you receive, as partners in Nomad, is the capitalisation of the success of the firms in which we have invested... the wealth you receive came from the relationship our companies' employees have with their customers."

He jokes: "If you see them at the bar buy a drink for Messrs. Bezos, Robertson, Fernandes, Sinegal, Buffett, Steiner et al. (then call us, that would be quite some bar!)."

All Sleep and Zakaria have done is "catch some better waves and try to ride them to the shore." Riding waves is not expensive — Nomad's management fee remains around 10 basis points per annum.

## Regulation: The New Challenge

This letter introduces a major new theme: **regulation**. Since 2001, the number of regulators overseeing Nomad has doubled to four, and the scope has increased by "one order of magnitude."

The UK regulator has gone through four iterations: IMRO, SFA, FSA, and now the **Financial Conduct Authority (FCA)**.

Sleep's concerns:
- New regulation seeks separation of risk management from portfolio management (undefined terms)
- Mandates remuneration policies but is "oddly silent on dividend payments"
- An investment advisor in London may be treated as an offshore partnership's GP for regulation, but as a subcontracted adviser for taxation — "a contradiction that may not be long lived"
- No allowance for long-term, simple funds like Nomad; treated same as leveraged, long/short hedge funds trading constantly

> "The halcyon days of the budding stock picker running his friends and family partnership from the sunroom at home at 5505 Farnam Street, Omaha (an image so important to Zak and me) are long gone."

He predicts industry consolidation (a desired regulatory outcome) will bring "unintended, concomitant increase in market fragility" (paraphrasing Nassim Taleb).

His whispered solution: **"tax short-term investing."** Instead, we get more blanket rules for everyone.

## Zimbabwe: The Final Chapter

Sleep recounts Nomad's Zimbabwe adventure:
- Purchased cement, brewery, and construction/engineering firms at deep discounts
- Circumvented exchange controls via Old Mutual dual-listing arbitrage
- Survived: 50% economic decline, hyperinflation, currency abandonment, stock market suspension (zero valuation), violent farm nationalisation, assassination attempts, coalition government with Mugabe

**Result:** Nomad's Zimbabwe investments rose 3-8x in US dollars. Sold in 2013, dollars remitted to London.

Enclosed with the letter (analogue version) is a **100 trillion Zimbabwean dollar bank note** — worthless (except eBay novelty value). "Frame it for the study/boardroom?"

## The Paradox of Choice & Investment Happiness

Sleep revisits Barry Schwartz's "The Paradox of Choice": more choice creates expectation of perfection → disappointment. The stock market has tens of thousands of investments changing price constantly. On any given day, someone else did better.

**Ferrari 250 GTO update:** A different GTO recently sold for **US$52 million** — 50+ year annual return over 20%. "There is always something going on that makes Nomad's performance look a bit weedy!"

## This is the 25th Letter

Sleep notes this is the 25th letter to investors over 14 calendar years. They own shares for such long periods that they write about 20 letters during the life of the average investment. "At its heart, investing is simple, and to make it seem anything but... may be a conceit."

## The Postamble (2021)

The collection ends with a postamble written in Spring 2021, reflecting on the closure:

- They liquidated the portfolio a few months after the December 2013 letter
- "We did not like that final phase one bit: selling stakes built up over years felt wrong"
- Why close? Regulation was "irksome and unnecessarily blunt"; they wanted freedom from justifying actions to a revolving door of interested parties; they felt they had "wrung all that we could out of the investment process"
- They wound up in their mid-40s, forcing them to build something new (philanthropy, respite care)
- "Many of the successful and wealthy people we know are a little mystified by what the money really means... taking personal identity in everything above X-amount is not a route to building a better world"

The letters end with an invitation: join them on the journey from investing to giving.
