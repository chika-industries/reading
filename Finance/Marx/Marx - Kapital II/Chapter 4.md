# Summary of Chapter 4: The Three Formulas of the Circuit

## The Three Formulas Side by Side

Marx presents the three circuit forms together:

| Form | Formula |
|------|---------|
| I. Money-capital | M → C ... P ... C' → M' |
| II. Productive capital | P ... Tc ... P (where Tc = total circulation process) |
| III. Commodity-capital | Tc ... P (C') |

When combined, every element appears as point of departure, transit, and return. The total process is the **unity of production and circulation**.

## Common Feature

All three circuits share the same **compelling motive**: the self-expansion of value.
- Form I expresses this directly (M → M')
- Form II begins with P, the process of creating surplus-value
- Form III begins with already self-expanded value

## Simultaneous Existence of All Three Circuits

In reality, every individual industrial capital exists **simultaneously** in all three forms:
- One part functions as commodity-capital
- Another as money-capital
- Another as productive capital

**Co-existence (Nebeneinander) is the result of succession (Nacheinander)** – parts of capital pass through stages in sequence, which enables them to exist side by side.

## Continuity vs. Interruption

If capital stops in any phase:
- **M → C** interrupted → money-capital becomes a hoard
- **Production** interrupted → means of production idle, labor-power unemployed
- **C' → M'** interrupted → unsold commodities accumulate

Continuity requires that different parts of capital simultaneously occupy different stages.

## Money-Capital's Special Role

- The circuit of money-capital is the most **one-sided** but also the most **striking and typical** form
- It reveals capital as "money begetting money"
- It shows that the constituents of productive capital originate in the commodity-market
- Variable capital *must* be advanced in money-form (wages paid in cash)

## The Illusory Character of M → M'

The formula can be deceptive if fixed as the exclusive form:
- It seems to suggest profit comes from circulation (buying cheap, selling dear)
- But the mercantile system fell into this error
- In reality, the circuit points toward other forms – it presupposes capitalist production (wage-labor, means of production as capital)

## Intertwining of Metamorphoses

The circulation of industrial capital crosses with commodity circulation from **all modes of production** – slavery, peasant production, communes, etc. Commodities enter the circuit regardless of their origin.

## Natural Economy, Money-Economy, Credit-Economy

- These three are not equivalent phases of development
- Credit-economy is merely a form of money-economy
- Both correspond to different stages of capitalist production
- **Capitalist production = commodity production as the general form** – this only becomes fully developed when agricultural producers become wage-laborers

## Supply and Demand of the Capitalist Class

The capitalist's supply is always greater than his demand:
- Demand = c + v (constant + variable capital)
- Supply = c + v + s (including surplus-value)
- For a composition of 80c + 20v + 20s, demand is 1/5 smaller than supply

**The capitalist's aim is not to equalize supply and demand, but to maximize the inequality** – the excess of supply over demand.

This is the first chapter where Marx explicitly introduces the aggregate perspective that will dominate later parts, while also critiquing the confusion between simple commodity circulation and capital circulation.
