Here is a summary of Chapter 29 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 29: The Genesis of the Capitalist Farmer

In this short but important chapter, Marx traces the origin of the **capitalist farmer**—the agricultural capitalist who employs wage-labourers and produces for profit. Unlike the peasant who works his own land or the feudal lord who extracts rent from serfs, the capitalist farmer is a businessman. His emergence was a crucial step in the development of capitalism, bridging the expropriation of the peasantry (Chapter 27) and the rise of industrial capital (Chapters 30–31).

#### The Transition from Feudal to Capitalist Agriculture

The capitalist farmer did not arise overnight. Marx distinguishes several stages:

1.  **The Serf or Villein:** In feudal times, the land was worked by serfs who owed labour services or rents in kind to their lords. They were not free wage-labourers.
2.  **The Free Peasant or Tenant:** Over time, some serfs gained freedom and became tenants, paying a money rent to the landlord. These tenants often still worked primarily for their own subsistence, selling only their surplus.
3.  **The Capitalist Farmer:** This new figure emerges when the farmer hires wage-labourers, produces commodities for the market, and aims to make a profit. His own labour is no longer the main source of his income; instead, he lives off the surplus-value extracted from the workers he employs.

#### How the Capitalist Farmer Arose in England

Marx identifies several historical processes that created the capitalist farmer in England:

- **The Enclosure Movement (15th–18th centuries):** As landlords enclosed common lands and evicted peasants, they leased large consolidated farms to a new class of tenants. These tenants were not peasants but men with capital—merchants, lawyers, or wealthier peasants who could afford to pay high rents and invest in improvements.
- **The Reformation (16th century):** The confiscation of Church lands placed vast estates on the market. Many of these were bought by speculators who then leased them to capitalist farmers.
- **The Long Leases:** Landlords began granting long leases (often 99 years or three lives) to farmers, giving them security to invest in the land. This encouraged capitalist farming, as tenants could now expect to reap the benefits of their investments.
- **The Rise of Wool Prices:** The demand for wool (for the growing textile industry) made sheep farming highly profitable. Large-scale sheep farming required extensive pastures and a relatively small labour force, favouring the capitalist farmer over the small peasant.

#### The Farmer as a Capitalist

The capitalist farmer differs from the peasant in several key respects:

- **He employs wage-labourers,** not family labour or serfs.
- **He advances capital** (for seeds, tools, livestock, wages) and expects to recover it with a profit from the sale of his produce.
- **He pays rent to the landlord** out of the surplus-value he extracts from his workers. The landlord receives a portion of the surplus as rent; the farmer keeps the rest as profit.
- **He is subject to market competition.** If he does not produce efficiently, he will be driven out of business.

Marx notes that by the end of the 18th century, the capitalist farmer was the dominant figure in English agriculture. The old peasantry had been largely eliminated, and the land was divided into large estates (owned by aristocrats) and large farms (leased by capitalist tenants). The agricultural labourer, now a propertyless wage-worker, completed the picture.

#### The Significance for Capitalist Development

The emergence of the capitalist farmer was essential for the industrial revolution:

- **It created a home market for industrial goods.** The expropriated peasants had to buy manufactured goods (clothing, tools, etc.) with their wages, and the capitalist farmers and landlords had to buy machinery, fertilizers, and luxury goods.
- **It freed up labour for industry.** The displacement of peasants from the land provided a steady supply of workers for the new factories.
- **It increased agricultural productivity,** allowing fewer farmers to feed a growing urban population.

Thus, the capitalist farmer was not an isolated phenomenon but a key link in the chain of primitive accumulation. Together with the expropriated peasant (Chapter 27) and the "bloody legislation" (Chapter 28), the capitalist farmer helped clear the ground for full-blown industrial capitalism.

#### Conclusion

The genesis of the capitalist farmer, like the expropriation of the peasantry, was not a peaceful or natural process. It was the result of enclosures, state violence, and the transformation of traditional property relations. The capitalist farmer stands as a transitional figure: neither a feudal lord nor a peasant, but a capitalist who exploits wage-labour and produces for profit. His emergence marks the penetration of capitalist relations into the very soil of society.
