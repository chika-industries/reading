Here is a summary of Chapter 28 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 28: Bloody Legislation against the Expropriated since the End of the Fifteenth Century. The Forcing Down of Wages by Act of Parliament.

In this chapter, Marx continues his historical account of primitive accumulation by examining how the state responded to the masses of expropriated peasants who now roamed the countryside as vagrants, beggars, and potential labourers. Instead of helping them, the state unleashed a wave of **terroristic legislation** designed to force them into wage-labour on the harshest possible terms.

#### The Problem of Vagrancy

The expropriation of the peasantry (described in Chapter 27) created a massive population of dispossessed people with no means of livelihood. Many turned to vagrancy, begging, and petty crime. From the perspective of the emerging capitalist class, this was a double problem:

- Vagrants were a threat to public order.
- More importantly, they were **unwilling to accept wage-labour** at the low rates capitalists wanted to pay. Many preferred the precarious freedom of the road to the discipline of the workshop.

The state therefore stepped in to **compel the expropriated to work**—and to work for wages so low that they would be forced to labour continuously just to survive.

#### The "Bloody Legislation" (16th–18th Centuries)

Marx details a series of English statutes from the reign of Henry VII through George III that imposed brutal punishments on vagrants and "sturdy beggars":

- **1530 (Henry VIII):** Whipping for vagrants; repeat offenders had their ears cut off; third offenders were executed as felons.
- **1547 (Edward VI):** Anyone who refused to work (including able-bodied vagrants) could be condemned to slavery for two years. If they ran away, they were branded on the forehead with an "S" (for slave) and enslaved for life. Masters had the right to chain and beat their slaves. This law was so extreme that even the ruling class found it difficult to enforce; it was repealed in 1550.
- **1572 (Elizabeth I):** Vagrants over 14 were to be "grievously whipped and burned through the cartilage of the right ear with a hot iron of an inch." Those convicted a second time, if not taken into service, were to be executed as felons.
- **1597 (Elizabeth I):** Vagrants who were "dangerous" could be banished from the realm. Those who returned were to be executed.
- **18th century:** Punishments became less bloody but still severe: whipping, imprisonment in workhouses, and forced labour.

These laws were not aimed at punishing crime in any modern sense. Their explicit purpose was to **terrorize the dispossessed into accepting wage-labour** under any conditions the employer chose to offer.

#### The Forcing Down of Wages by Act of Parliament

The same statutes that punished vagrancy also sought to **fix maximum wages**. The state, acting on behalf of the capitalist class, set legal ceilings on wages that workers could receive. The Statute of Labourers (1349, renewed many times) fixed wages at pre-plague levels, despite a massive labour shortage. In 1563, the Statute of Artificers gave local justices the power to assess wages annually, ensuring that they remained low.

Workers who demanded higher wages could be imprisoned. Employers who paid higher wages, however, were only fined—a clear indication of whose side the law was on. As Marx notes, the law treated the worker's refusal to accept low wages as a crime, while the capitalist's greed was merely an infraction.

#### The Creation of the Modern Working Class

The combination of expropriation (Chapter 27) and terroristic labour legislation (Chapter 28) produced the modern working class:

- Workers were **propertyless** (no land, no tools, no means of subsistence).
- They were **legally compelled** to accept wage-labour on the capitalists' terms.
- They were **disciplined** by the threat of whipping, branding, enslavement, or execution if they resisted.

This, Marx argues, is the real origin of the "free" worker. Freedom meant freedom from the means of production—and freedom to be exploited.

#### The Irony of "Free Labour"

Bourgeois ideology celebrates the free wage-labourer who voluntarily contracts with the capitalist. But Marx shows that this "freedom" was **manufactured by state terror**. The worker is free in the sense that he is no longer a serf or a slave, but he is not free to choose his condition. He must sell his labour-power or face starvation—and if he chooses vagrancy, he faces the lash, the branding iron, and the gallows.

As Marx quotes an old proverb: **"Law is the instrument of the rich against the poor."** The bloody legislation against the expropriated was not an aberration; it was a systematic policy to create a docile, desperate, and exploitable workforce.

#### Conclusion

Chapter 28 completes the historical picture of primitive accumulation by showing how the state used **violence and terror** to force the dispossessed into wage-labour. The "free" labour market, far from being a natural outgrowth of individual liberty, was created by centuries of bloody coercion. The capitalist class, having stolen the land from the peasants, then used the state to steal the workers' freedom to resist.
