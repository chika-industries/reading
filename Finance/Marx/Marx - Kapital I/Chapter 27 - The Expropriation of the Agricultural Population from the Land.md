Here is a summary of Chapter 27 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 27: The Expropriation of the Agricultural Population from the Land

In this chapter, Marx provides a detailed historical account of how the English peasantry was systematically driven off the land, creating the propertyless working class essential for capitalism. This process, which took place from the end of the 15th century through the 19th century, is a central example of **primitive accumulation**.

#### The Pre-Capitalist English Countryside

Before the rise of capitalism, the English countryside was populated by a class of **free peasants** who owned or rented small plots of land, often with common rights to graze animals, gather fuel, and use forests and wasteland. These peasants were not wage-labourers; they produced largely for their own subsistence. Alongside them were some yeomen farmers and feudal landlords, but the peasantry formed the backbone of rural society.

#### The First Wave: The Reformation and Theft of Church Lands

The first major blow to the peasantry came with the **Reformation** under Henry VIII. The state confiscated vast Church properties (monasteries, convents, ecclesiastical estates) and gave them to royal favourites and speculators. These new owners had no feudal obligations to the peasants and immediately began to evict them and enclose the land for sheep grazing. The "clearing of estates" became a mass phenomenon.

#### The Enclosure Movement

The driving force behind expropriation was the **enclosure of common lands**. Common land was not "ownerless"; it was subject to traditional rights of use by villagers. Enclosure meant fencing off these lands and converting them into private property, usually for large-scale sheep farming. Wool was highly profitable in the 16th century, and landlords could make far more money by grazing sheep on enclosed land than by collecting small rents from peasants.

The enclosure movement proceeded in waves:
- **15th–16th centuries:** Enclosures for sheep farming, often accompanied by the demolition of peasant cottages and entire villages. The wandering sheep, as Marx notes, "devoured men."
- **18th century:** A new wave of enclosures driven by the need for arable land to feed a growing urban population and by the development of large-scale capitalist agriculture.
- **19th century:** The final enclosure of remaining open fields and waste lands, completed by Acts of Parliament.

#### The Role of the State and Parliament

The state played an active role in this expropriation. In the 16th century, the Crown issued proclamations against enclosure (largely ineffective) while simultaneously granting enclosure licenses to its favourites. By the 18th and 19th centuries, Parliament itself became the instrument of enclosure. Between 1710 and 1820, over 3,500 enclosure Acts were passed, transferring millions of acres from common use to private ownership.

These Acts were not neutral; they were promoted by large landowners and their allies. Small peasants, unable to afford the legal costs of opposing enclosure, simply lost their rights. The result was the creation of a huge, landless population—a proletariat—that had no choice but to seek employment as wage-labourers on the very estates from which they had been expelled.

#### The Transformation of the Landlord and the Farmer

The expropriation of the peasantry transformed the English countryside into a tripartite class structure:
- **The landed aristocracy:** A small number of great landlords who owned vast estates, often leasing them to capitalist farmers.
- **The capitalist farmer:** A tenant who rented land from the landlord, employed wage-labourers, and sold his produce for profit. The farmer was a capitalist, not a peasant.
- **The agricultural wage-labourer:** The expropriated peasant, now a propertyless worker who sold his labour-power to the farmer.

This structure was fully in place by the end of the 18th century. The English peasantry, once the backbone of rural society, had been almost entirely eliminated.

#### The Human Cost

Marx does not spare the human cost of this process. The expropriated peasants swelled the ranks of vagrants, beggars, and criminals (to be addressed in the next chapter). Whole villages were abandoned; the ruins of peasant cottages could be seen across the English landscape. The dispossessed were driven into overcrowded towns, where they formed the raw material for the industrial proletariat.

Marx quotes contemporary writers who described the enclosures as a form of "legalized robbery." He cites the case of the Scottish Highlands, where landlords (the "chiefs" turned into "landlords") evicted entire clans to make way for sheep pastures, often burning their cottages over their heads.

#### Conclusion

The expropriation of the agricultural population from the land was not an accidental byproduct of economic development; it was a **deliberate, violent process** driven by the profit motive and enforced by state power. It created the two conditions necessary for capitalism:
1.  A **propertyless working class** with no means of subsistence except the sale of its labour-power.
2.  A **class of capitalists** (landlords and tenant farmers) who owned the means of production and could exploit wage-labour.

This chapter, along with the next, forms Marx's historical counter-narrative to the bourgeois fable of peaceful, thrifty accumulation. The birth of capitalism, he insists, was not a quiet affair of saving and investment but a bloody expropriation.
