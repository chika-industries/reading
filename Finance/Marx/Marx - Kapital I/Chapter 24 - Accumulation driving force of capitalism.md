Here is a summary of Chapter 24 of *Capital*, Volume 1.

### Summary of Chapter 24: The Transformation of Surplus-Value into Capital

This chapter examines how surplus-value is not merely consumed by the capitalist but is often converted into additional capital, a process Marx calls **accumulation of capital**. He analyzes the transformation of property laws under capitalism, critiques the "abstinence" theory of profit, and identifies the key factors that determine the scale of accumulation.

#### 1. Capitalist Production on a Progressively Increasing Scale. The Inversion of Property Laws

Marx begins by considering simple reproduction (where all surplus-value is consumed) and then moves to **expanded reproduction** (accumulation). Under simple commodity production (e.g., independent peasants or artisans), the law of appropriation was simple: the producer owned the product of his own labour. Under capitalism, this law is turned upside down.

- The capitalist initially acquires means of production and labour-power by exchanging equivalents (money for commodities). This respects the law of commodity exchange.
- But once production begins, the product belongs to the capitalist, not to the worker. The capitalist then sells the product, realizes its value, and obtains more money than he advanced. This surplus-value is the result of unpaid labour.
- When the capitalist uses part of this surplus-value to buy additional labour-power and means of production, he is **appropriating the product of other people's labour without equivalent** and then using that product to command even more labour. The original act of exchange (buying labour-power at its value) is now the mere surface of a process in which the capitalist constantly appropriates unpaid labour and uses it to purchase more unpaid labour.

Thus, **the law of private property based on one's own labour is transformed into its opposite: the right to appropriate the labour of others without equivalent.** Capitalist property is nothing but the accumulated product of unpaid labour.

#### 2. The Political Economists' Erroneous Conception of Reproduction on an Increasing Scale

Classical economists (Smith, Ricardo) recognized that accumulation requires a portion of surplus-value to be turned into capital rather than consumed. But they failed to see that this capital is always and everywhere the congealed form of unpaid labour. Instead, they spoke of the capitalist's "abstinence" or "thrift" as the source of capital. They also imagined that the "labour fund" (the wages advanced to workers) is a fixed quantity that cannot be increased without reducing the capitalist's consumption.

Marx ridicules this notion. The labour fund is not a fixed pot; it grows with accumulation. The more surplus-value is turned into capital, the more workers can be employed (though in changing proportions). There is no inherent limit to accumulation except the limits of surplus-value production itself.

#### 3. Division of Surplus-Value into Capital and Revenue. The Abstinence Theory

Marx now launches a direct critique of the "abstinence" theory, most famously associated with Nassau Senior (whom Marx already attacked in Chapter 9). The theory holds that profit is the reward for the capitalist's abstinence from consuming his capital. The more the capitalist abstains (saves), the more capital accumulates.

Marx argues that this is absurd on several grounds:

- **Capitalists do not abstain from consumption**; they live luxuriously while still accumulating. The rich do not become richer by living like monks.
- **Abstinence is not a productive act.** Not consuming does not create value. The source of surplus-value is unpaid labour, not the capitalist's self-denial.
- **The capitalist's "abstinence" is actually the compulsion to accumulate** driven by competition, not a free moral choice. He must accumulate or be ruined.
- **The historical record** shows that primitive accumulation (the original separation of the worker from the means of production) was not accomplished by thrift but by violence, conquest, and expropriation.

The abstinence theory is therefore an apologetic fiction, designed to present capitalist exploitation as a virtuous sacrifice.

#### 4. The Circumstances Determining the Extent of Accumulation (Independently of the Division of Surplus-Value)

Given that a portion of surplus-value is turned into capital, the *scale* of accumulation depends on several factors, which Marx enumerates:

- **The degree of exploitation of labour-power** (the rate of surplus-value). The higher the rate, the larger the surplus-value available for accumulation.
- **The productivity of labour.** Higher productivity produces more use-values in the same time, cheapening means of production and subsistence, and thus allowing a given surplus-value to command more additional capital.
- **The growing difference between capital employed and capital consumed.** Machinery, buildings, etc., are used up only gradually, but their full value is advanced. The more fixed capital relative to circulating capital, the larger the mass of value that can be productively employed without being consumed. This acts as a "gratuitous gift" to capital.
- **The magnitude of the capital advanced.** Even with the same rate of exploitation, a larger capital produces more surplus-value, enabling faster accumulation.

Marx also notes that a rising rate of surplus-value can compensate for a falling rate of profit in terms of accumulation. Even if profits are squeezed, the mass of surplus-value may still grow if the number of workers exploited increases or if exploitation intensifies.

#### 5. The So-Called Labour Fund

Bourgeois economists speak of a "labour fund" or "wage fund" as a fixed sum of capital set aside for wages, which cannot be increased without reducing profits or consumption. Marx dismisses this as another apologetic fiction.

- The labour fund is not fixed; it grows with accumulation. Each year, more capital is advanced in wages than the year before.
- The wage fund is nothing but the value created by workers that is returned to them (partially) in the form of wages. There is no separate "fund" outside the process of production.
- The idea that wages are paid out of a pre-existing fund is an illusion created by the money form. In reality, the worker's wages are advanced out of the value he himself has already created, as shown in Chapter 23.

The labour fund theory is used to argue that trade unions cannot raise wages permanently, because there is only so much "fund" to go around. Marx shows that this is false: the fund expands with accumulation, and the struggle over wages is a struggle over the division of the value created, not over a fixed quantity.

#### Conclusion

Chapter 24 establishes accumulation as the driving force of capitalism. It shows that:

- Accumulation is the conversion of surplus-value into additional capital.
- This process inverts the original law of private property: from "each owns his own product" to "the capitalist owns the product of others' unpaid labour."
- The "abstinence" theory is ideological nonsense.
- The scale of accumulation depends on the rate of exploitation, productivity, the composition of capital, and the total capital advanced.
- The so-called labour fund is not fixed but expands with accumulation.

This sets the stage for Chapter 25, where Marx will analyze the **general law of capitalist accumulation** and its effects on the working class.
