# Summary of Chapter 5: Classification of Securities

## Conventional Grouping and Its Objections
Securities are traditionally divided into bonds and stocks (with preferred and common stocks). The bondholder has a fixed, prior claim; the stockholder assumes risks and shares profits.

**Three objections to this conventional grouping:**

1. **Preferred stocks grouped with common stocks** – For investment purposes, preferred stocks belong with bonds, since they are bought for fixed income and safety of principal, not partnership in the business.

2. **Bond form identified with safety** – This leads investors to believe the name “bond” carries special assurance against loss. In truth, safety depends entirely on the debtor’s ability to pay, not on the form. A bond of a business without assets or earning power is as valueless as its stock.

3. **Titles fail to describe issues accurately** – Many securities modify or mix standard provisions, making the traditional labels misleading.

## Standard Patterns (the “straight” forms)

**Bond pattern:**
- Unqualified right to fixed interest on fixed dates
- Unqualified right to repayment of fixed principal at maturity
- No further interest in assets/profits, no management voice

**Preferred stock pattern:**
- Stated dividend priority over common (but discretionary if common gets nothing)
- Priority in dissolution up to stated principal amount
- Limited or no voting rights

**Common stock pattern:**
- Pro rata ownership of assets after prior claims
- Pro rata interest in profits after prior deductions
- Pro rata voting rights

## Numerous Deviations from Standard Patterns
Examples include: income bonds; convertible bonds and preferred stocks; bonds with stock-purchase warrants; participating preferred stocks; common stocks with preferential features; nonvoting common stock; bonds with interest payable in cash or common stock at holder’s option; callable features.

More extreme deviations: Great Northern Railway Preferred (in all respects a plain common issue); Associated Gas and Electric bonds convertible into preferred at the *company’s* option (not true bonds). Option warrants (e.g., American and Foreign Power Company – aggregate market value over $1 billion in 1929) became major financial instruments with tremendous mischief-making power.

## New Classification Suggested
Rather than relying on titles, classify by **risk-and-profit characteristics as the buyer would reasonably view them**:

| Class | Representative Issue | Characteristics |
|-------|---------------------|------------------|
| **I. Fixed-value type** | High-grade bond or preferred stock | Prospective change of value minor; owner’s dominant interest is safety and steady income |
| **II. Senior securities of variable-value type** | | |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;A. Well-protected with profit possibilities | High-grade convertible bond | Hopes for safety plus added profit from conversion privilege |
| &nbsp;&nbsp;B. Inadequately protected | Lower-grade bond or preferred stock | Definite risk offset by corresponding chance of profit; profit possibilities limited |
| **III. Common-stock type** | Common stock | Unlimited potential gain (or loss) |

## Borderline and Marginal Cases
- A sound convertible bond with remote conversion level belongs in Class I.
- An investment-grade bond selling at an unduly low price belongs in Class II (the buyer expects appreciation).
- American Tel. & Tel. convertible 4½% bonds selling at 200 are effectively common-stock commitments.
- Kreuger & Toll Participating Debentures at offering price were definitely common-stock type (their title was misleading).
- A preferred stock selling at 10¢ on the dollar has no junior equity – it functions as a common stock, not a senior security.

## High-Grade Preferred Stocks
Gilt-edged preferreds (e.g., National Biscuit Company Preferred) belong in Class I with high-grade bonds. However, such issues constitute a **very small percentage** of all preferred stocks. Most preferreds are not sufficiently well protected and belong in Class II (variable/speculative senior issues). A sharp distinction must be drawn between the typical and the exceptional preferred stock.
