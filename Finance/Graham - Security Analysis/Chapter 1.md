# Summary of Chapter 1: The Scope and Limitations of Security Analysis. The Concept of Intrinsic Value

## Core Definition of Analysis
Analysis involves careful study of available facts to draw conclusions based on established principles and sound logic. However, investment is not an exact science—like law and medicine, both individual skill and chance determine success.

## Historical Context
Security analysis experienced a brilliant rise until 1927, followed by an "ignominious fall." The "new era" beginning in 1927 essentially abandoned analytical approaches. While the 1929 crash surprised few analysts who maintained discipline, the subsequent business collapse and its devastating effects on earning power threw their calculations off course.

## Three Functions of Security Analysis

### 1. Descriptive Function
Marshalling important facts about an issue and presenting them coherently. This includes revealing strengths/weaknesses, comparing with similar issues, and appraising factors likely to influence future performance.

### 2. Selective Function
Expressing specific judgments about whether an issue should be bought, sold, retained, or exchanged. The chapter provides several examples:
- **Rejection example**: St. Louis-San Francisco Railway 6% noncumulative preferred stock (1928) - earnings never reached 1.5× fixed charges and preferred dividends
- **Acceptance example**: Owens-Illinois Glass 5% bonds (1932) at 70 - earnings many times interest requirements, amply covered by current assets
- **Undervalued example**: Wright Aeronautical stock (1922) at $8 - paying $1 dividend, earning over $2, with $8+ per share in cash assets
- **Overvalued example**: Same stock (1928) at $280 - earning only $8 per share, net asset value under $50
- **Arbitrage example**: Interborough Rapid Transit 7% notes vs. 5% bonds - mathematical relationship suggested substantial undervaluation

### 3. Critical Function
Applying standards to facts, evaluating accounting methods, and assessing corporate policies affecting security owners (capitalization, dividend policies, management compensation, etc.).

## The Concept of Intrinsic Value
Intrinsic value is "elusive"—not as definite or determinable as market price. The analyst does not need to determine exact intrinsic value, but only to establish whether:
- Value is adequate (to protect a bond or justify a stock purchase), or
- Value is considerably higher or lower than market price

The author uses a homely simile: "It is quite possible to decide by inspection that a woman is old enough to vote without knowing her age."

The degree of precision needed varies by case. For J.I. Case Company common stock (1933), the range of approximate value might be $30-$130—too wide for a conclusion at $30, but a conclusion would be warranted if the price were $10.

## Three Principal Obstacles to Analyst Success

1. **Inadequate or Incorrect Data**: Deliberate falsification is rare, but concealment is common. Skilled analysts can detect most accounting artifacts.

2. **Uncertainties of the Future**: Future changes are largely unpredictable. Analysis is therefore more useful for senior securities (protected against change), for inherently stable businesses, and under normal conditions.

3. **Irrational Behavior of the Market**: Undervaluations may persist inconveniently long; new factors may supervene before prices adjust. The analyst must guard against this by favoring situations not subject to sudden change and by tempering activities to general financial conditions.

## Analysis vs. Speculation
Analysis is better suited to investment than speculation for several reasons:
- Mechanics of speculation (commissions, interest charges, bid-asked spreads) handicap the speculator
- Analytical factors in speculative situations are subject to swift revision
- Those "on the inside" often have advantages that nullify analytical premises
- Where chance plays a dominant role, even a mathematical advantage may prove ineffective

The author uses a roulette analogy: a small mathematical advantage practically assures good results in investment, but proves entirely ineffective where luck is the overshadowing influence.
