
# Summary of Chapter 3: Sources of Information

This chapter outlines where the analyst can obtain data about security terms, the issuing company, and the industry, along with critical observations on their utility and limitations.

## Data on the Terms of the Issue
- **Security manuals and statistical services** provide basic summaries.
- **Bond indentures (deeds of trust)** – detailed bond contracts; obtainable from the trustee.
- **Stock charters (articles of association)** – full terms of stock issues; for listed stocks, listing applications contain all pertinent provisions and are readily available.

## Data on the Company

### Reports to Stockholders (including interim news releases)
Quality and frequency vary widely:

- **Railroads** – monthly figures (often down to net income), weekly carloadings, detailed annual reports.
- **Public utilities** – quarterly or monthly statements (gross, net after taxes, balance for dividends); some publish only moving 12-month totals; many supply weekly/monthly kilowatt-hour sales.
- **Industrials** – policies vary by company. Monthly statements exist (chain stores sales, copper output, GM unit sales, U.S. Steel unfilled orders/deliveries, Baldwin orders, pipeline operations). Monthly net earnings figures are sporadic and often discontinued when earnings decline.

**Quarterly statements** are standard in most industries, encouraged by the NYSE. Seasonal businesses (sugar, fertilizers, farm implements) may be exempt; some conceal seasonality by publishing moving 12-month figures. Many companies (e.g., large cigarette makers, most department stores, Woolworth) still fail to publish quarterly net profits. GM is cited as an outstanding example of detailed quarterly reporting.

**Semiannual reports** are not standard for any industrial group (except possibly rubber companies).

**Annual reports** are published by every listed company. They invariably include the balance sheet. The income account varies greatly; some give only earnings available for dividends and dividends paid.

### Required Elements of a Reasonably Complete Income Account
The authors list: (1) sales, (2) net earnings (before following items), (3) depreciation (and depletion), (4) interest charges, (5) non-operating income (in detail), (6) income taxes, (7) dividends paid, (8) surplus adjustments (in detail). Less than half of important enterprises supply this minimum. Withholding sales or depreciation data handicaps analysis and may benefit insiders.

**U.S. Steel Corporation** is praised as a model of comprehensiveness (production/sales in units, division of sales, operating expense details, capital expenditures, inventory details, property owned, number of stockholders).

### Balance Sheet
Better standardized than the income account. Former defect (failure to separate intangibles from fixed assets) is now rare. Shortcomings include: stating only net property without depreciation deduction, failing to state market value of securities owned, not identifying investments as marketable or nonliquid, inventory not at lower of cost or market, vague miscellaneous reserves, and failure to disclose treasury stock holdings.

### Periodic Reports to Public Agencies
Railroads and most utilities file detailed data with federal/state commissions – often more complete than shareholder reports. Examples:
- **Consolidated Gas of NY** – a “mystery stock” due to sparse shareholder info, but complete data were always in NY Public Service Commission reports.
- **Mackay Companies** (Postal Telegraph) – reported little to shareholders but much to the ICC.
- **Standard Oil pipeline companies** – chary with shareholders, but detailed ICC reports revealed large cash and securities holdings.
- **Survey of Current Business** (U.S. Dept. of Commerce) – includes chain-store sales figures not publicly released elsewhere.

### Listing Applications (to NYSE)
Among the most important non-periodic sources. They contain far more detail than shareholder reports: sales in dollars, output in units, federal taxes, subsidiary operations, depreciation/depletion basis, property owned, contracts, accounting methods. Highly valuable but appear irregularly.

### Registration Statements and Prospectuses (Securities Act of 1933)
Require voluminous filings with the Federal Trade Commission. These promise great value to analysts and investors.

### Miscellaneous Official Reports
Examples: U.S. Coal Commission report (1925) gave previously unavailable anthracite data; Federal Trade Commission reports on utility holding companies; ICC opinions; trustee reports under mortgages (e.g., revealed that Mason City & Fort Dodge Railroad 4s interest was not truly earned).

### Statistical and Financial Publications
- **Comprehensive manuals** (Poor’s, Moody’s) – annual with supplements.
- **Card services and frequently revised manuals** (Standard Statistics, Fitch, Poor’s).
- **Daily news digests** (Standard Corporation Records, Fitch).
These services have improved greatly but cannot be relied upon for all data from original sources. **The analyst should consult original reports wherever possible.**

- **The Commercial and Financial Chronicle** – weekly with statistical supplements; unusually comprehensive; detailed reproduction of corporate reports.

### Requests for Direct Information from the Company
Stockholders are owners and have a right to ask legitimate questions. Courts have held that a bona fide stockholder has the same right to full information as a partner in a private business, subject only to not injuring the corporation. While legal enforcement is expensive, vigorous demands for legitimate information are often acceded to, especially when the data are regularly published by other companies in the same field.

## Information Regarding the Industry
Abundant industry-wide data:
- **Survey of Current Business** – monthly figures on output, consumption, stocks, unfilled orders.
- **Statistical Abstract**, **World Almanac**, **Biennial Census of Manufacturers** – annual or periodic data.
- **Trade journals** – frequent summary figures and continuous detailed pictures of industry conditions.

Thus the analyst can usually acquire a fairly complete background knowledge of the industry without undue difficulty.
